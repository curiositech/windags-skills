# Cultural Context as Active Analytical Tool: From Background to Layer

## The Standard (Mistaken) View of Context

The dominant view in conversation analysis treats context as something to be held at arm's length. Schegloff's position — that only "relevant context" should be used, and that it should emerge from the talk itself rather than being imposed a priori — reflects a legitimate concern: if the analyst pre-loads cultural assumptions, they will find what they brought, not what is there.

But Adamson's thesis shows that the extreme position creates a different problem: it renders certain phenomena systematically invisible. Behaviors that are only comprehensible through cultural knowledge cannot be correctly interpreted by a method that refuses cultural knowledge. The analyst who sees only surface coding cannot distinguish between:

- A short answer that reflects comprehension failure
- A short answer that reflects classroom-socialized norms of appropriate response
- A short answer that reflects face-preserving compliance
- A short answer that reflects genuine confidence that brevity is the correct answer

The cultural layer is not optional. It is the difference between surface reading and understanding.

## The Mulder Framework: A Worked Example

Adamson adopts Mulder's (1996) analysis of Thai social behavior as his primary cultural layer. The key construct is the **khuna-decha continuum**:

| Khuna (intimate circle) | Decha (outside world) |
|---|---|
| Family, trust, safety | Powerful influences, danger, uncertainty |
| Revealing true opinions | Retaining true opinions |
| Krengcaj (considerate deference) | Krenengklua (awe, respectful fear) |
| Moral bunkhun relations | Pragmatic bunkhun relations |
| Khwaampenkaneeng (mutual informality) | Formality, hierarchy |

Every interaction is located somewhere on this continuum. The Thai participant's behavior is shaped by where they perceive the relationship to fall. The NS teacher-researcher-interviewer is a complex object: western (decha), authority figure (decha), unknown quantity (decha), but simultaneously the source of an unprecedented personal request for the student's own knowledge and experience (potentially khuna-adjacent).

The complexity creates individual variation. Some students — those with prior experience of NS interaction, those who have read the relationship as less formal — move toward the khuna end and exhibit interruptions, casual language use, and topic extensions. Most remain firmly in the decha realm and exhibit deference, short answers, suppressed breakdown, and polite smiling through non-comprehension.

> "In terms of the status of participants in van Lier's representation of contingency, much reference is made to 'symmetrical' and 'asymmetrical' relations. According to Luckmann high symmetry is more associated with every day conversation and less with more formal interaction." (p. 27)

## Key Cultural Concepts and Their Analytical Applications

**Krengjai (deference to authority, avoidance of face-threat to superiors)**

The most analytically pervasive concept in the thesis. Krengjai explains:
- Short answers (avoiding the presumption that expansion is invited)
- Suppressed breakdown requests (avoiding the implication that the authority figure's question was unclear)
- False positive responses (avoiding the face-threat of sustained non-comprehension)
- Rapid closure of breakdown sequences (avoiding prolonged public admission of difficulty)
- Absence of challenge or disagreement moves (avoiding direct contradiction)

Crucially, krengjai is not passivity or stupidity. It is an active, sophisticated social skill — the management of face relations in asymmetric contexts. Its operation requires the interviewee to continuously model the authority figure's likely response to any move and pre-empt face-threatening outcomes.

**Bunkhun (reciprocal obligation, merit-earning in hierarchical relations)**

Every interaction with an authority figure is an opportunity to earn bun (merit). How bun is earned differs by individual socialization:
- For students from large, traditional classrooms (Burin): short, accurate answers earn bun
- For students with more NS experience (Sangdaew, Kik): extended responses and engagement earn bun
- For students with high conceptual awareness (Serm): taking initiative and showing insight earn bun

This means the *same underlying motivation* (earn merit, demonstrate appropriate deference) produces *different observable behaviors* depending on what the individual has learned counts as appropriate response to authority. The analyst who sees only behavior and not its cultural grammar will mistake Burin's brevity for incapacity and Sangdaew's expansiveness for cultural non-conformity.

**Sam ruam (calm under pressure)**

An unexpected finding: Adamson initially applies this concept in the standard CA fashion — using it post-hoc to explain patterns found in the data. But he then deliberately "foregrounds" it in an anthropological manner, using it as a prior lens through which to look at the data. The result: he notices that most male interviewees showed visible anxiety (contradicting sam ruam ideals) while most female interviewees showed composure. This gender-differentiated pattern was not discoverable from the surface coding or from the other cultural concepts.

The methodological lesson is significant: **occasionally applying a cultural concept as a prior lens rather than a post-hoc explanation reveals patterns that the standard analytical approach cannot access**. This is a controlled deviation from CA orthodoxy, justified by its results.

> "Working backwards by using cultural context as a 'foreground' consideration could be regarded as an extension of my proposed eclectic use of 'layers of insight'. Indeed, if taking some Thai cultural criteria to seek out whether they occur or not within the discourse actually reveals something more in the discourse than the typical conversation analyst's approach of working primarily without context... then working backwards can be justified." (p. 165-166)

## The Danger of Anglo-Centric Default Categories

A persistent theme in the thesis is the inadequacy of western-default analytical categories for Thai behavior. Terms like "shy," "reticent," "passive," "uncritical" are not descriptively neutral — they encode western behavioral norms as the standard from which Thai behavior deviates. They explain Thai behavior as deficit.

Mulder's (1996) Thai-language categories (krengjai, bunkhun, etc.) explain the same behaviors as competences — culturally-specific skills that make perfect sense within their operating context. The analytical shift from "this student is passive" to "this student is exercising krengjai" is not merely semantic. It changes what the analyst looks for, what questions get asked, and what interventions might actually help.

> "Mulder (1996) proposes that interpretations of Thai behaviour must be made with reference to Thai cultural terms. This concurs with Wierzbicka's (1991) rejection of 'monolingual universals or static global comparisons.'" (p. 67)

This is an argument against analytical imperialism: the application of one cultural framework's norms to judge behavior that is operating under a different framework. The same argument applies wherever analytical frameworks are borrowed from one context and applied to another without examining what assumptions they carry.

## The "Common Ground" Alternative

Clark (1996), cited by Adamson, offers a partial alternative to the national-culture framing: instead of asking "what do Thais do," ask "what do these specific participants share." "Communal common ground" (shared membership in communities of practice) and "personal common ground" (shared individual experience) provide a more fine-grained basis for predicting communicative behavior than nationality alone.

This matters practically because several of Adamson's interviewees don't fit the Thai default: one is part-German, several have had extensive periods abroad. The national-culture framework would misread their behavior; the common-ground framework would look instead for what specific communities and experiences have shaped their communicative expectations.

## When Context-as-Layer Should NOT Be Applied

Adamson is honest about the risks of over-applying cultural context:

1. **Risk of confirmation bias**: If cultural categories are applied before data examination, the analyst will find what the categories predict. The CA insistence on letting context emerge from the talk is a legitimate guard against this.

2. **Risk of stereotyping**: Applying group-level cultural categories to individual behavior ignores idiosyncratic variation. Burin is not short because he is Thai; he is short because of his specific classroom socialization history. The category is suggestive, not definitive.

3. **Risk of category imperialism**: Adopting Mulder's categories does not eliminate the problem of analytical imperialism — it simply shifts from Anglo-centric to Thai-centric. Mulder's categories may themselves embed assumptions that don't apply to all Thai participants in all contexts.

The appropriate use is exploratory rather than definitive: cultural categories generate hypotheses about what might be happening; the data must confirm or disconfirm them.

## Application to Agent Systems

**Domain knowledge is a legitimate analytical layer, not a source of bias.** When analyzing code, an agent that knows nothing about common architectural patterns in the domain will misread code that deviates from generic best practices but conforms to domain-specific conventions. Domain knowledge should be a deployable layer, not a background assumption.

**Build in explicit cultural/domain calibration for cross-context analysis.** A system optimized for analyzing English-language text will misread features of Arabic-script text that reads right-to-left. A system optimized for enterprise Java will misread Python that uses idiomatic functional patterns. Context calibration is not a nice-to-have; it is a necessary condition for accurate analysis.

**Distinguish between "behavior violates the norm" and "behavior operates under a different norm."** Many analytical systems are built with implicit baseline norms. Behavior that deviates from the baseline is flagged as anomalous. But the anomaly may be normal within a different but legitimate framework. Build systems that can ask: "Is this deviation from my expected norm, or is it conformance with a different norm I don't have?"

**Foreground unusual lenses occasionally.** Standard analytical loops can be supplemented by periodic application of unusual analytical frameworks — security analysis from an attacker's perspective, performance analysis from an end-user's perspective, code review from a maintainer's perspective. These foregrounded lenses catch patterns that the standard loop cannot see.

**Group-level patterns inform but don't determine individual analysis.** Cross-interview patterns (Thai students tend to exhibit krengjai) provide priors, not conclusions. Each individual analysis should be able to override group-level priors when the specific data demands it.
