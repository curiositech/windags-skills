# The Methodology of Revisiting Data: How to Return to the Same Source with New Questions

## Why Revisiting Is Not Simply Re-Reading

Returning to previously analyzed data is not the same as reading it again. To return *with new tools* and *new purposes* is to encounter different data — not because the data has changed, but because the analytical lens determines what becomes visible. Adamson's thesis is structured around exactly this operation: ten interview transcripts originally analyzed for learning-strategies content are revisited for turn-taking structure, and the revisitation yields findings the first analysis could not have produced.

But the thesis also shows that revisiting requires *method*. Without structure, revisitation becomes re-reading, and re-reading without new tools produces confirmation of what was already known. The methodological innovations in the thesis are precisely what transforms revisitation into new discovery.

## The Two-Step Process

Adamson formalizes his revisitation methodology as two sequential steps with internal structure:

**Step 1: Learning Strategies Layer**
1. Re-read the transcript with attention to content (learning strategies), not discourse structure
2. Review each interviewee's individual "crystallisations" (A6) — prior analysis of their expressed strategies
3. Compare with group summaries (A7) and language learning histories (A8)
4. Note commonalities, differences, and idiosyncrasies
5. Ask the standardized "dialogue question": *"How are this interviewee's language learning strategies different from those of the others?"*

**Step 2: Turn-Taking Layer**
1. Re-read the transcript with attention to discourse structure (coding), not content
2. Examine exchange sequences (A2), interview summaries (A3), and turn-taking trends (A4)
3. Apply Thai cultural criteria (A5) — note significant patterns
4. Ask the standardized "dialogue questions":
   - *"What is of note about the turn-taking behaviour of both participants?"*
   - *"Are any of these observations to be explained by the semi-structured interview format?"*
   - *"Are these observations repeated among other participants?"*
   - *"To what extent is this interviewee's awareness of learning strategies influential in their turn-taking behaviour?"*
   - *"Is the 'voice' of the interviewee/interviewer consistent throughout the interview(s)?"*

The two-step structure is important: the learning-strategies step is not a warm-up — it actively enriches the turn-taking step by providing a different kind of contextual attention before the formal discourse analysis begins.

## The Role of Dialogue Questions

The "dialogue questions" function as *perspective-forcing mechanisms*. Without them, the analyst would remain in whatever perspective they happen to be in — typically, close attention to local coding decisions. The questions force a pull-back to a higher level of observation:

- Does this finding hold across multiple interviews or only this one?
- Can this pattern be explained by the interview format itself, or by the interviewee?
- Is there consistency in this participant's behavior, or is there "voice" variation?

These questions cannot be answered by looking at a single exchange. They require moving between the individual transcript and the cross-interview comparison tools — the exchange sequences (A2), the turn-taking trends (A4), the group summaries (A7). The questions create the obligation to make those comparisons.

> "The same standardised questions are to be asked after each step serving the purpose of allowing me to view my findings objectively. Without this opportunity, I feel unable to gain an overall clear perspective on what the findings may be showing on both an individual or collective basis." (p. 142)

## The Balance Between Eclecticism and Structure

The methodology is deliberately paradoxical: the application of tools is "intentionally eclectic," but the overall process is formally structured (two steps, standardized dialogue questions, required tools). This is not contradiction — it is the necessary design for complex analytical work.

**The formal structure provides**:
- Minimum coverage guarantees (every interview receives both steps)
- Cross-interview comparability (same questions asked about every interview)
- Protection against premature closure (standardized questions force consideration of aspects the analyst might otherwise skip)

**The eclecticism provides**:
- Responsiveness to what the data actually shows (not all tools are equally relevant for every interview)
- Protection against mechanical analysis (not every tool must be applied to every exchange)
- Openness to unexpected findings (an unusual pattern in one interview may call for a tool not used in any other)

> "This represents a purely experimental, probing and instinctive element in the conduct of the analysis which enables the researcher to interact, or in Kvale's (1996: 182) analogy, to enter into 'dialogue' with the data." (p. 136)

## The Concept of "Well-Formedness" as Expectation Management

A recurring theme in the thesis is "well-formedness" (Stubbs 1981): the intuitive sense, held by both participants in an interaction, of what constitutes a correctly structured exchange. Well-formedness expectations are cultural, social, and contextually calibrated. They determine what is noticed as anomalous.

In the context of revisiting data:
- The first analysis established certain expectations about what the data contained
- The second analysis may find patterns that violate those expectations — exchanges that look wrong from the first analysis's perspective but look right from the second's
- The violation is itself informative: it marks the place where the first analysis's model of well-formedness was inadequate

This is an argument for recording not just findings but *expectations*: what the analyst expected to find, and where the data violated those expectations. The expectations are as informative as the findings, because they reveal the analyst's model of the domain.

## What Cannot Be Recovered in Revisitation

Adamson is honest about the limits of revisitation. Some information is irrecoverably lost between the first analysis and the second:

- **Non-verbal signals** are captured imperfectly in brackets within the transcript ("(unsure look)," "(smiles)"). These annotations reflect the analyst's perception at the time of transcription and cannot be revised or expanded retrospectively.
- **Paralinguistic features** — tone of voice, pace, stress — are partially captured in the transcription conventions but imperfectly. The recording preserves them; the transcript approximates them.
- **The analyst's state** during the original interviews — what they noticed in real time, what they didn't notice, what they decided was relevant versus irrelevant — shapes the transcript in ways the later analyst cannot fully reconstruct.

> "Duranti (1997) addresses this issue by suggesting that interview transcripts should not be regarded as static after one listening but should be allowed to evolve over time and further considerations (including presumably listenings)." (p. 46)

The solution is not to pretend the losses don't exist, but to return to the recording when the transcript proves inadequate. The recording is the ground truth; the transcript is an interpretation. Revisitation must ultimately be capable of going back to the recording, not just the transcript.

## The Accumulation Principle

Each revisitation deposits new insights that become available for subsequent revisitations. The tools accumulated from the learning-strategies study become part of the toolkit for the turn-taking study. The findings from the turn-taking study would become part of the toolkit for a third study.

> "Every research undertaken is accumulated and carried forward... The emergence of new insights into the data has a historical perspective which will reinforce the methodological framework each time that data is revisited since every research undertaken is accumulated and carried forward." (p. 205)

This is an argument against treating any single analysis as definitive. Data has latent interpretive potential that can only be accessed when the right tools are available. The tools available today are not the tools available tomorrow. Therefore: preserve the data, preserve the metadata about the data, preserve the analytical tools used, and design for revisitation.

## Application to Agent Systems

**Design analytical tasks to deposit reusable context, not just produce conclusions.** When an agent completes a complex analysis, the intermediate findings — what was noticed, what was uncertain, what was set aside — should be preserved as metadata available to future analysis tasks.

**Implement two-pass analysis for high-stakes tasks.** A first pass for content (what does this say?), a second pass for structure and patterns (how is it said? what is the format? what is anomalous about the interaction?). The second pass enriches the first by bringing different analytical attention.

**Build perspective-forcing checkpoints into reasoning loops.** At set intervals, the system should ask standardized meta-level questions: "Does this finding hold across all instances I've processed? What would have to be true for this pattern NOT to hold? What alternative explanation would also fit the data?" These questions prevent premature closure and force broader pattern recognition.

**Preserve the gap between surface analysis and deeper interpretation.** First-pass coding produces surface classifications. Second-pass interpretation applies additional frameworks to those classifications. Keep these stages distinct; don't collapse them into a single-pass conclusion.

**Design for the tools you don't have yet.** If the system today can only analyze syntax, design the data storage so that semantic analysis can be applied retroactively when that capability is added. If the system today lacks domain knowledge, store enough raw material that domain knowledge can be applied when it becomes available.

**Treat recordings as primary, transcriptions as interpretation.** Where an analytical system operates on derived artifacts (summaries, structured representations, intermediate outputs) rather than the original data, it should retain access to the original. Revisitation that cannot go back to the original source is limited by the accuracy and completeness of the first interpretation.
