# Asymmetry, Status, and the Problem of Pre-Determined Interaction

## The Core Tension

Every structured interaction — an interview, a classroom exchange, a job assessment, a doctor-patient consultation, an agent-user dialogue — involves status asymmetry. One party has more authority, more control over the agenda, more power to determine what counts as a valid response. This asymmetry shapes everything: what gets said, what gets suppressed, how turns are allocated, who initiates topics, who closes them, what counts as "correct."

Adamson's thesis examines this asymmetry in fine detail through the lens of native-speaker teacher-interviewer and Thai student-interviewee. The findings have broad implications for any system where one party designs the interaction and another must navigate it.

> "The concept of 'contingency' has two main aspects, that of the degree of dependency between participants and the unpredictable nature of that interaction... In its extreme form of low contingency between participants towards the center of the web, there is little unexpected interaction involved." (p. 26-27)

## Van Lier's Contingency Axis

Van Lier (1996) provides the conceptual tool: a **contingency axis** from low to high. Low contingency: pre-determined, predictable, monologue-like, asymmetric. High contingency: spontaneous, unpredictable, conversational, symmetric.

Classroom teacher-student interaction is typically low contingency — the teacher controls the agenda, selects topics, pre-determines what counts as a valid response, has the right to evaluate. Interview interaction is structurally similar but can be pushed toward higher contingency by the interviewer's deliberate choices.

But here's the key finding: the parties may have *different readings of where on the contingency axis the interaction is*. The interviewer may intend high contingency (friendly rapport, open questions, space for extended responses). The interviewee may expect low contingency (formal assessment, pre-determined correct answers, asymmetric rights). These misaligned readings produce behavioral responses that seem contradictory from either perspective alone.

> "Moves to create the casual 'rapport' necessary for ethnographic interviewing, along with unfamiliarity with interviews conducted in English, may confuse Thai participants who may actually expect a lack of parity in a one-to-one meeting with an older teacher-researcher." (p. 59)

## The Hangover Effect

McCarthy's (1991) concept of "hangover from the classroom" is central to the thesis. When teacher and student interact outside the classroom, the classroom relationship doesn't disappear — it persists as a baseline expectation for how turns should be allocated, who has the right to evaluate, and what constitutes appropriate participation.

This "hangover" is not simply a Thai phenomenon. It appears in any asymmetric long-term relationship: doctor-patient, employer-employee, senior-junior developer, experienced agent-new agent. The prior relationship structure pre-allocates interactional rights in ways that the parties carry forward unconsciously.

The consequences for data quality are significant: if the interviewee is experiencing a classroom-style interaction and the interviewer is conducting a peer-style conversation, the data gathered reflects neither the interviewer's intent nor the interviewee's actual knowledge — it reflects the dissonance between the two relational models being applied simultaneously.

## Sacks et al's Pre-Allocation Claim

Sacks, Schegloff, and Jefferson's (1974) foundational claim that formal speech events have "massively present" pre-allocated turn structures is both supported and challenged by Adamson's data.

**Supported**: The interviews are overwhelmingly IRF-structured. The interviewer initiates, the interviewee responds, the interviewer provides feedback. This three-part pattern recurs across all ten interviews for most exchanges. It is the default of formal institutional interaction.

**Challenged**: The claim that turns are *unpredictable* in size, content, distribution, and order is not borne out. Within the pre-allocated structure, there is considerable variability — some interviewees produce extended multi-clause responses; some interviewer feedback moves transform into new initiations; topic boundaries shift unpredictably when interviewees extend beyond the scripted question. The pre-allocation creates the frame but does not determine its contents.

The more significant challenge: the participant with higher status retains the ability to *vary* the contingency level strategically. The interviewer can push toward higher contingency (asking open questions, allowing topic extension, providing minimal feedback to encourage elaboration) or pull back toward lower contingency (closed questions, evaluative feedback, rapid topic closure). The interviewee cannot. This asymmetry in the ability to manage contingency level is itself a form of power.

## The Paradox of Attempted De-Formalization

One of the most interesting findings in the thesis: the interviewer's attempts to de-formalize the interaction — to move it toward higher contingency, to create friendly rapport — may have actively disrupted the interviewees' ability to participate effectively.

Thai students who expected formal, low-contingency interaction were not prepared for an authority figure trying to be friendly. The mismatch was confusing. Attempts at warmth may have registered as strange behavior from a superior, creating uncertainty about what behavioral norms applied.

> "Attempts to de-formalise interviewing in the Thai setting may be regarded by the student-respondents as being contrary to their adherence to 'krengjai'... Moves to create the casual 'rapport' necessary for ethnographic interviewing... may confuse Thai participants who may actually expect a lack of parity in a one-to-one meeting with an older teacher-researcher." (p. 59)

This is a generalizable finding: **de-formalization by the higher-status party does not automatically reduce asymmetry — it may shift the burden of managing asymmetry onto the lower-status party**, who must now navigate both the original asymmetry and the unfamiliar new framing imposed by the higher-status party's de-formalization attempt.

## The IRF System as Power Structure

The IRF exchange system is not merely a neutral description of how interaction is organized — it encodes a power structure. The party in the I position controls the agenda; the party in the R position is obligated to respond; the party in the F position evaluates. In teacher-student interaction, all three positions are consistently occupied by the same party for two of the three moves. The student has one slot.

> "In the classroom this knowledge is most likely, in a strictly transmission style of teaching, to be held by the teacher, asking questions — opening exchanges with Initiations (I) — and providing follow-up moves (F) which evaluate student responses (R). In educational research interviews, the reverse situation may be the case, as student-interviewees surely act as the 'primary knowers'..." (p. 25)

The research interview is supposed to invert this structure — the interviewee is the "primary knower" who holds the relevant information. But the structural form of interaction (I→R→F with the interviewer in positions I and F) encodes the old power structure regardless of intent. The interviewee-as-knower is structurally placed in the respondent position, answering questions designed by someone else, evaluated by that same person.

This structural contradiction — the interviewee is supposed to have authority over the content but the interaction structure gives them only the response slot — is one reason why interview data is often thinner than it could be.

## Topic Control and Interviewee Agency

Despite the structural dominance of the interviewer at the exchange level, the thesis documents meaningful interviewee agency at the *topic* level. Several interviewees:

- Extended responses to explore sub-topics not asked about (Komkrit's extended discussion of maths vs. English)
- Interrupted the interviewer to continue an extension (Sangdaew, Kik)
- Shifted the topic direction slightly through their response, causing the interviewer to follow

This topic-level agency is often invisible in exchange-sequence analysis because it manifests within the response slot — technically, the interviewee is still responding. But the content of the response has redirected the interaction in ways the interviewer's script did not anticipate.

McCarthy's distinction between "speaker's topic" and "full conversational topic" is relevant here: the interviewee's topic extension only becomes a full interactional topic if the interviewer follows it. When the interviewer does follow (as in the Komkrit exchange above), the interaction temporarily achieves something closer to symmetry, even within the formal IRF structure.

## Application to Agent Systems

**Design for the asymmetry you have, not the asymmetry you intend.** An agent-user interaction may be designed with the intention of peer-level exchange, but if the agent controls the agenda, allocates the turns, and evaluates the responses, the structural asymmetry remains regardless of tone. Design decisions about who controls the agenda, who can initiate topics, and who evaluates outputs determine the actual power structure.

**Recognize that de-formalization by the higher-status party creates its own burdens.** If an orchestrating agent tries to make itself "friendlier" to downstream agents by using less structured communication, it may actually increase downstream uncertainty about what is expected. The downstream agent that was calibrated for formal, structured instructions may perform worse with informal, unstructured requests.

**Track the contingency level of each interaction.** High-contingency interactions (exploratory, open-ended, high tolerance for surprising responses) require different design from low-contingency interactions (structured, expected responses, formal evaluation). Systems should know which mode they are in and calibrate accordingly.

**Build mechanisms for the lower-status party to redirect topics.** If user-agent interaction is structured so that only the agent can introduce new topics or shift analytical direction, valuable signals from the user about what they actually need will be lost. Explicit mechanisms for user-initiated topic extension — and agent willingness to follow — can recover this.

**Distinguish between structural response and substantive response.** An agent that fills the response slot (produces a well-formed output in the expected format) is not necessarily providing substantive response (output that addresses the actual informational need). Evaluation systems that check format compliance without checking substantive adequacy will systematically miss this failure mode.

**Pre-allocated structures are defaults, not constraints.** IRF patterns emerge as defaults because they are efficient for formal, asymmetric interactions. But the parties can vary them — and sometimes should. Agent systems should be able to recognize when the pre-allocated structure is failing and shift to a different mode (more exploratory, more symmetric, more collaborative).
