# When Expert Intuition Fails — and Why: A Taxonomy for Intelligent Systems

## Starting from the Paradox

The paper opens with what the authors call "the obvious fact": professional intuition is sometimes marvelous and sometimes flawed. Fireground commanders make life-or-death decisions in seconds with impressive accuracy. Chess grandmasters identify the best move in complex positions without calculating every possibility. Neonatal nurses detect early sepsis before blood tests return positive.

And yet: experienced clinicians are outperformed by simple statistical models. Finance professionals with decades of market experience cannot reliably predict individual stock performance. Intelligence analysts with access to classified information cannot forecast major geopolitical events better than educated laypeople. The USS Vincennes, crewed by highly trained military professionals operating the most advanced naval combat system available, shot down a civilian airliner.

The paradox dissolves when you understand that these are not the same situation. The conditions that make expert intuition reliable are specific, definable, and absent in many of the cases where experts are most confidently wrong. This document maps those conditions systematically.

## Failure Mode 1: Zero-Validity Environment

**The mechanism**: Expert intuition requires a high-validity environment — one with stable, learnable relationships between observable cues and outcomes. In a zero-validity environment, no such relationships exist. Any patterns an expert perceives are illusory. Any learning they undergo reinforces those illusions.

**The critical feature**: In zero-validity environments, skilled and unskilled judges perform equally — both do no better than chance. But the skilled judge is dramatically more *confident* in their wrong answers, because their confidence is generated by their (incorrect) sense of having learned the domain.

**The paradigm case**: Tetlock's (2005) 15-year study of expert political forecasters. These were highly educated, experienced professionals with access to excellent information, who had spent careers studying the domains they were forecasting. Their long-term forecasts (beyond about 1-2 years) were no more accurate than those of newspaper-reading non-experts. The consistency of this result across experts and domains points to the environment, not the experts: "Long-term forecasting must fail because large-scale historical developments are too complex to be forecast."

Tetlock's thought experiment makes the point vividly: "Consider what the history of the 20th century might have been if the three fertilized eggs that became Hitler, Stalin, and Mao had been female. The century would surely have been very different, but can one know how?"

**Implication for agents**: Agents deployed on zero-validity tasks will produce outputs with unwarranted confidence. There is nothing wrong with the agent's architecture — the domain itself provides no valid signal to learn from. The correct response is not to build a better agent for these tasks but to recognize them as zero-validity and either switch to explicit uncertainty quantification or to acknowledge the limits of prediction.

## Failure Mode 2: Wicked Learning Environment

**The mechanism**: More dangerous than zero validity is negative validity — an environment where feedback is systematically misleading. The expert learns from experience, but the experiences teach the wrong lesson. Over time, the expert builds a confident, internally coherent, empirically validated, *wrong* model of the domain.

**The paradigm case**: Hogarth's (2001) early 20th-century physician who developed accurate-seeming intuitions about which patients would develop typhoid. He confirmed his intuitions by palpating patients' tongues without washing his hands — thereby *causing* the infections he was predicting. "The intuitions were disastrously self-fulfilling."

The physician experienced:
- Real pattern recognition (he was genuinely noticing something in patients before they developed symptoms)
- Consistent feedback (patients he identified *did* develop typhoid)
- Growing confidence (supported by statistical confirmation over years)
- Genuine causal structure (his action caused the outcome)

Everything that should produce calibrated expertise was present. The causal arrow was simply reversed.

**Implication for agents**: Wicked learning environments can corrupt agent training pipelines in exactly analogous ways. If an agent's actions change the ground truth it will later be evaluated against — or if feedback is systematically selected in ways that reinforce certain outputs — the agent can develop confident, validated, wrong models. Detecting this requires external validation against independent ground truth, not just evaluation within the system.

Key markers of wicked environments for agents:
- The agent's outputs influence what data it will later receive
- Feedback is provided by the same system that generates the task (circular evaluation)
- Success is defined by the outputs being accepted, not by independent outcome measures
- Rare, high-impact failures are systematically excluded from the feedback loop

## Failure Mode 3: Insufficient Learning Opportunity

**The mechanism**: Even in a high-validity environment, skill requires practice. If a professional encounters a type of case too rarely, or without adequate feedback, they cannot build the pattern library required for reliable intuition in that case type — even if they have genuine expertise in adjacent cases.

**The paradigm case**: Weather forecasters are reliably accurate for temperature and precipitation (common events with clear feedback) but significantly less accurate for hail (rare, local, limited feedback even in a high-validity physical environment).

Fireground commanders encounter novel building types (skyscrapers with unexpected damage to heat shielding) that their experience with standard residential and commercial buildings does not prepare them for. The environment has valid cues — but the commander has not had the opportunity to learn what those cues mean.

**Implication for agents**: Training data coverage is the direct analog of learning opportunity. An agent with extensive training on common case types and sparse training on rare case types has fractionated expertise that mirrors human expert fractionation. The agent's pattern library is deep in well-covered domains and shallow in sparse ones.

This is measurable: track the distribution of training examples by subtask type and flag cases where the current query falls in a sparse region of the training distribution. Treat sparse coverage as analogous to insufficient learning opportunity — reason for reduced trust, not for silence.

## Failure Mode 4: Fractionated Expertise Crossing Domain Boundaries

**The mechanism**: Genuine expertise in one subtask generates confidence that transfers to adjacent subtasks, even when the adjacent subtasks have different validity profiles or haven't been practiced enough to build skill.

**The paradigm case**: Financial analysts who are skilled at evaluating a company's commercial prospects (a moderately high-validity task with some learnable patterns) are not skilled at judging whether the company's stock is underpriced (a near-zero-validity task, since publicly available information is already priced in). But their knowledge about the company feels directly relevant to the stock question — and it is relevant, just insufficient to make the stock judgment valid.

The feedback asymmetry amplifies this: analysts get clearer, faster feedback on their commercial assessments (the company succeeds or fails, clearly and relatively quickly) than on their stock calls (stock movements are noisy, delayed, and confounded by market-wide factors).

**Implication for agents**: When an agent is routed a task that is adjacent to but outside its high-validity scope, it will produce confident output because its internal experience of processing the task feels like the experience of processing tasks it's genuinely good at. The task has similar features, similar structure, similar vocabulary. The confidence is real. The validity is absent.

The fix is not a smarter agent — it's better routing. Know the scope boundaries of each agent's expertise (empirically, not just theoretically) and treat scope violations as routing failures, not agent failures.

## Failure Mode 5: Heuristic Substitution Without Detection

**The mechanism**: When faced with a question for which they lack relevant experience, people (and agents) automatically substitute a simpler, related question and answer that instead. The substituted question may be answerable. The original question may not be. The resulting judgment is delivered with the confidence appropriate to the easier question, not the harder one.

**The paradigm cases**:
- "What will Julie's GPA be?" → answered as "How impressive is her early reading?" (attribute substitution)
- "Is the average price of German cars more or less than $100,000?" → anchors the subsequent estimate (anchoring via memory sampling)
- "A ball and a bat together cost $1.10..." → the computation "1.10 - 1.00 = 0.10" is performed instead of the correct simultaneous equations (fluent but wrong algorithm)

In each case, System 1 produces a confident answer to a question that wasn't asked. System 2 has the capacity to catch this — but doesn't always, either because checking is effortful or because the System 1 answer feels correct.

**Implication for agents**: Language model agents are particularly vulnerable to attribute substitution because they are trained to produce fluent, coherent, contextually appropriate outputs. A question that is difficult to answer may generate a confident response to a related but easier question, with no internal or external marker distinguishing the two.

Mitigation: require agents to explicitly state what question they are answering before they answer it. Discrepancy between the stated question and the actual question (as evaluated by a monitor agent) is a reliable signal that substitution has occurred.

## Failure Mode 6: High-Confidence Lucky Performance (Illusion of Skill)

**The mechanism**: In low-validity environments, chance variation means some practitioners will have strings of successes. These practitioners develop high confidence based on genuine experiential feedback — they were right many times. But their success was statistical noise, not skill. They will revert to chance performance when the run ends, but they won't be able to detect when the run has ended.

**The paradigm case**: The financial industry. Some fund managers outperform the market for several years running. This is expected by chance in a large industry — someone will have a 5-year run even if all are performing randomly. Those managers develop confident theories about their "edge," give interviews, write books. When performance reverts, they attribute it to unusual market conditions, not to the absence of the skill they believed they had.

"These 'lucky' individuals will be susceptible to an illusion of skill and to overconfidence."

**Implication for agents**: If an agent is evaluated on a low-validity task over a limited period, its measured accuracy may be higher than its true accuracy by chance. This is a training and evaluation problem, not an architecture problem. Mitigation: evaluate over long periods, across diverse test conditions, and explicitly test the hypothesis that performance is better than chance (not just better than naive baselines).

## Common Thread: No Internal Marker

All six failure modes share a feature that makes them especially dangerous: **none produce an internal signal distinguishing them from genuine skilled performance**. The confident, pattern-matched, coherent output of an expert in a high-validity domain is phenomenologically identical to the confident, heuristic-generated, coherent output of an expert operating outside their domain, in a low-validity environment, with insufficient learning opportunity, substituting a tractable question for an intractable one.

For intelligent agent systems, this means: failure detection cannot rely on the agent reporting its own failure. It requires external, structural assessment of whether the conditions for valid performance are met — and explicit testing against ground truth where it exists.
