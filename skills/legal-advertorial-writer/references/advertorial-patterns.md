# Advertorial Patterns — Annotated Examples

## What Makes Legal Advertorial Copy Work

### Pattern 1: The "You Already Know This Problem" Hook

Effective legal advertorial doesn't introduce the problem — it names the problem the reader already has.

**Weak hook (abstract):**
> "Every year, millions of Americans struggle to find employment due to criminal records."

Why it fails: Tells the reader they're a statistic. Feels like a government pamphlet. No emotional pull.

**Strong hook (specific, experiential):**
> "You passed the phone screen. You did well in the interview. The job was yours. Then came the background check. They never called back."

Why it works: Reader either recognizes this exact experience or can vividly imagine it. The frustration is felt before a single product feature is mentioned.

---

### Pattern 2: The Education-First Trust Build

The middle of the article should be genuinely useful — information the reader can act on whether or not they buy anything.

**Example (explaining data brokers for a record sweep product):**

> When a court expunges your record, it seals the case file. The clerk marks it. Prosecutors can't use it. Background check companies that pull directly from court databases will no longer see it.
>
> But here's what expungement doesn't do: it doesn't reach back in time and erase the 60-plus companies that already copied your record before the expungement. Data brokers like Spokeo, BeenVerified, and Intelius scraped public court records years ago. They stored them. They sell access. And they are under no automatic obligation to update their files when a court order comes through.
>
> You have to tell them. In writing. Citing the law. One by one.

Why it works: The reader learns something real. The complexity is presented honestly. The product (dispute letters, one by one) is implied without being named yet. Trust is earned before the ask.

---

### Pattern 3: Honest Competitor Acknowledgment

Naming the free/DIY option and explaining its tradeoffs is counterintuitive but effective.

**Example:**

> You can do this yourself. The FCRA gives you the right to dispute inaccurate records directly with data brokers. Each company has a dispute process — usually a web form, a mailing address, or both. You write a letter, include your court order, send it.
>
> If you have one or two records and time to spare, that's a reasonable path. The letters aren't legally complex.
>
> The problem is volume. There are more than 100 companies that may have your record. Each has a different process, different address, different required documentation. Doing this thoroughly takes 15–20 hours and weeks of follow-up.

Why it works: Reader trusts you because you didn't hide the free option. Now the product's value proposition (saves 15–20 hours) is concrete instead of vague.

---

### Pattern 4: The Earned CTA

After honest education and fair competitive framing, the CTA can be short because the work is done.

**Weak CTA (pressure-based):**
> Don't let your past hold you back any longer. Take control of your future today. Get started now — this offer won't last.

Why it fails: "Won't last" is a lie. "Take control" is a cliché. "Hold you back" is condescending.

**Strong CTA (honest, specific, respectful):**
> If your record is expunged and you want to make sure the data brokers know it, Record Sweep generates a personalized dispute letter for every company that may have your file — 100+ letters, addressed and ready to send, for $99.

Why it works: Describes exactly what happens. States the price. No pressure. Assumes the reader is an adult who can make a decision.

---

### Pattern 5: The 60% In-Article Soft Mention

For longer articles (1,000+ words), a brief product mention around the 60% mark primes the reader before the end CTA. It should feel like a natural aside, not a sales break.

**Example (embedded mid-article):**
> This is exactly the gap that products like Record Sweep are designed to fill — you've done the legal work, and the automated letter generation handles the data broker follow-through so you don't have to do it 100 times by hand.

Then continue with more education. Don't linger. The CTA at the end will close it.

## Common Mistakes in Legal Advertorial

### Mistake 1: Outcome promises
**Wrong:** "Record Sweep will remove your arrest from all background checks."
**Right:** "Record Sweep generates dispute letters to 100+ background check companies, exercising your FCRA rights. Most companies must respond within 30 days."

The difference is liability and trust. Readers have been promised too much before. Hedged accuracy is more persuasive than confident claims.

### Mistake 2: Complexity dumbing-down
**Wrong:** "Expungement is simple — just file a form and your record disappears."
**Right:** "Expungement eligibility depends on your charge type, state, and waiting period. For many people it's straightforward. For others it requires an attorney."

Simplifying falsely loses readers who know better. And if the product then turns out to be harder than implied, refunds and chargebacks follow.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring the emotional state
Legal problems carry shame, fear, and exhaustion. Ignoring these creates copy that feels tone-deaf.

**Wrong:** "Optimize your background check results with our proven system."
**Right:** "Most people in this situation have been carrying this quietly for years. That's worth acknowledging."

The emotional acknowledgment doesn't have to be dramatic. A single sentence that says "this is hard and you're not alone" earns more goodwill than a paragraph of feature bullets.

### Mistake 4: Burying the price
If the product costs money, say so early or in the CTA. Don't make the reader hunt for it. Hidden pricing feels predatory and increases bounce on the checkout page.

### Mistake 5: Multiple CTAs
One article, one CTA. Offering "Start Free," "Buy Now," and "Talk to an Expert" in the same article creates decision paralysis. Pick the one most appropriate for the article's audience and commit to it.
