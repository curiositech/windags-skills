# Dealer Offer Architecture

Use this file when the question is "what should the sign say?" rather than "how big should it be?"

## The dealership roadside stack

For automotive dealers, roadside creative usually needs to do one of four jobs:

1. `Locate the dealership`
2. `Announce one event or inventory push`
3. `Direct traffic into service or a sub-lot`
4. `Prime a later conversion in lower-friction media`

Trying to do more than one of those jobs on one face is where most dealer signage falls apart.

## Match the message to the canvas

| Objective | Best sign types | Message pattern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Store identity | Pylon, monument, fascia, major roadside ID sign | `BRAND / STORE NAME / NEXT EXIT or NEXT RIGHT` |
| Used inventory push | Frontage board, digital monument, near-dealer billboard | `USED or CERTIFIED / ONE proof point / LOCATION` |
| New model or seasonal event | Billboard, frontage banner, digital roadside | `MODEL or EVENT / WHY NOW / DEALER` |
| Service retention | Service-lane sign, roadside directionals near service drive | `SERVICE BENEFIT / ARROW or TURN / BRAND only if needed` |
| Price-heavy or payment-heavy sale | Low-speed frontage, landing page, VDP, radio-TV support | `ONLY if disclosure burden fits the medium` |

## Offer stack patterns that work

### Brand-led

Use when the store needs recognition more than persuasion.

- `SMITH TOYOTA`
- `NEXT EXIT`

### Inventory-led

Use when the store wins because selection is the proof.

- `150+ USED CARS`
- `NEXT RIGHT`

### Event-led

Use when urgency matters more than detailed financing.

- `TRUCK MONTH`
- `ENDS SATURDAY`
- `JONES FORD`

### Service-led

Use when the goal is operational flow or fixed-ops revenue.

- `EXPRESS SERVICE`
- `NO APPOINTMENT NEEDED`
- `->`

## What to simplify first

When a dealer insists on "more information," cut in this order:

1. extra logos
2. secondary vehicle lines
3. phone numbers
4. multiple incentives
5. decorative flourishes

Keep only what changes behavior from the road.

## Dealer-specific shibboleths

### 1. Inventory proof usually beats abstract bragging

"150+ Used Cars" is usually more useful than "Best Deals in Town" because it tells the shopper why they should deviate now.

### 2. Numerals often beat nouns

Dealer boards benefit from bold numerals:

- number of vehicles
- year/model
- one event date

But finance numerals create disclosure consequences. If the numeral is a payment, stop and load the compliance file.

### 3. Route cues outperform generic CTAs

`NEXT EXIT`, `NEXT RIGHT`, and `2 MILES AHEAD` usually outperform `VISIT US TODAY` because they close the spatial gap between ad and store.

### 4. Used, certified, and service each need different signage logic

- `Used`: inventory, price cue, urgency
- `Certified`: trust, inspection, inventory proof
- `New`: model launch, franchise identity, event framing
- `Service`: operational clarity, speed, convenience

## Context and localization

OAAA's guidance emphasizes contextual relevance and adapting the message to the local environment. For dealers this means:

- naming the local road, neighborhood, or exit when that improves navigation
- swapping creative by weather or daypart on digital boards when the frame still stays simple
- separating commuter-route boards from near-lot boards instead of reusing one layout everywhere

## Automotive OOH evidence

Clear Channel Outdoor's automotive page, citing OAAA, Harris Poll, and Scarborough data, claims:

- 38% of U.S. adults recall seeing OOH ads for auto brands or retailers
- among those who recall them, many visit websites or do research immediately
- among adults planning to buy a vehicle in the next 12 months, OOH ranked highly for driving retailer and website visits

Treat these as directional planning evidence rather than law. The practical takeaway is still useful: dealer OOH performs best when it is placed near commuter routes and near dealership approach paths, and when the creative is specific enough to trigger a next step.
