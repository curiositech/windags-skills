# The Gap Between Evidence and Practice: Why Proven Methods Fail to Displace Inferior Ones

## The Central Puzzle

One of the most important contributions of Brimbal et al. (2021) is not the 
demonstration that rapport-based interviewing works — that evidence existed before 
this study. The contribution is the demonstration that **evidence of effectiveness is 
not sufficient to change practice**, and that specific structural and psychological 
barriers must be understood and addressed for evidence-based methods to be adopted.

The study opens with this observation:

> "Policing is unlike many other fields of practice (e.g., medicine) in that 
> historically researchers and practitioners do not consistently collaborate 
> (Weisburd & Neyroud, 2011). As a prominent example, for more than 50 years 
> interviewing techniques have been based on customary knowledge developed through 
> practitioner experience and informed by anecdotal evidence (Hartwig et al., 2014). 
> This lack of empiricism has prolonged the use of a coercive accusatorial approach 
> for suspect interviewing that is not the most effective at eliciting accurate and 
> actionable information." (p. 56)

Fifty years of inferior practice, maintained despite mounting evidence of its inferiority. 
This is not a policing-specific pathology — it is a general feature of how skilled 
practitioners in any domain relate to evidence about their practice.

## Why Evidence-Based Practices Fail to Displace Entrenched Methods

### 1. Practitioners Develop and Trust Anecdotal Evidence
Experienced practitioners have extensive personal experience with their methods. 
That experience is real and the confidence it generates is not irrational — within 
the range of cases they have encountered, their methods have produced acceptable 
results (or at least results they could attribute to their methods). The fact that 
a controlled study shows better outcomes under different conditions doesn't override 
the practitioner's personal, embodied experience.

> "the difficulty of implementing evidence-based changes to long-held law enforcement 
> procedures more generally (e.g., Bayley, 1998; Lum et al., 2012)" (p. 58)

### 2. The Resistant Subject Objection
The most consistent practitioner objection to rapport-based approaches is that they 
work with cooperative subjects but fail with resistant ones. The accusatorial approach 
is justified precisely for the hard cases — the suspects who won't talk.

> "Practitioners often criticize a rapport-based approach for its lack of efficacy 
> with resistant subjects." (Abstract, p. 55)

This objection is almost exactly backwards. The evidence shows that rapport-based 
methods are *especially* effective with resistant subjects because resistance is 
primarily a relational phenomenon, not a fixed state. But the intuitive logic — 
more resistance requires more pressure — is compelling and difficult to dislodge 
without experiential demonstration.

### 3. The Toolbox Problem
Even practitioners who accept the evidence-base for new methods tend to adopt them 
selectively rather than systematically:

> "the selective adoption of evidence-based practices often associated with a 'toolbox' 
> approach (see Snook et al., 2020), it is critical to assess the extent to which an 
> information-gathering approach might be perceived as useful and adopted by practitioners." 
> (p. 58)

The toolbox approach takes individual techniques from evidence-based frameworks and 
adds them to an existing practice without adopting the underlying philosophy. The 
result is that the techniques are used in contexts where they don't work (because 
the overall approach is still accusatorial) and the practitioner concludes that the 
technique doesn't work, rather than that the technique requires the supporting framework.

### 4. Self-Evaluation Blindness
Practitioners cannot accurately assess their own use of techniques, as documented 
in the pre-training self-reports showing high familiarity with tactics that behavioral 
coding revealed were used infrequently and ineffectively. Without external behavioral 
feedback, practitioners have no mechanism to detect the gap between what they believe 
they are doing and what they are actually doing.

### 5. The Time Horizon Problem
Rapport-based approaches require investment before the payoff. Building rapport takes 
time in an individual interview; building a research base and training infrastructure 
takes years. The accusatorial approach produces output faster (even if the output is 
contaminated). In any system where practitioners are evaluated on short-term outputs, 
evidence-based methods that invest in long-term quality face structural disadvantages.

## What Actually Works to Close the Gap

The study's training design embodies specific strategies for closing the evidence-practice 
gap that are generalizable:

### Demonstrate Efficacy in the Hardest Cases
The study specifically used a semi-cooperative subject design — simulating the resistant-subject 
scenario that practitioners most cite as the failure point of rapport-based methods. 
Demonstrating that rapport-based methods work with resistant subjects directly addresses 
the primary objection.

For any domain: evidence of efficacy in easy cases is less persuasive than evidence 
of efficacy in the cases where practitioners are most skeptical. Target the hardest 
case, not the average case.

### Use Behavioral Assessment Rather Than Self-Report
The study's methodology — assessing actual behavioral change rather than self-reported 
learning — is itself a demonstration that behavioral change is possible and measurable. 
Showing practitioners objective evidence of their own pre/post behavioral change makes 
the learning visible in a way that subjective self-report cannot.

### Provide an Integrated Framework, Not a Toolbox
The training provided "an empirically supported, rapport-based model of interviewing" 
(p. 59) — a coherent framework with a theoretical rationale, not a collection of 
techniques. The framework included an explicit account of the mechanism (rapport → 
cooperation → disclosure) that explains *why* the techniques work, making them 
interpretable rather than arbitrary.

> "Trainers presented productive questioning tactics as the foundation of an effective 
> information-gathering interview, followed by conversational rapport tactics... and 
> relational rapport tactics as tools to overcome resistance and obtain cooperation." (p. 56)

The layered presentation — foundation first, then elaboration — creates a coherent 
architecture that practitioners can internalize rather than a list they must memorize.

### Include Practical Exercises with Immediate Feedback
The training's pilot study identified which exercises were most helpful and refined 
the format accordingly. Behavioral change requires behavioral practice. Lectures 
provide conceptual knowledge; only supervised practice with feedback produces 
behavioral integration.

### Address the Resistance Objection Directly
The training explicitly taught "a framework with which to understand and manage a 
subject's resistance" (p. 59). Rather than ignoring the practitioner's primary 
concern, the training centered it — and then demonstrated that the evidence-based 
approach specifically addresses it.

## Structural Conditions That Enable Adoption

The study also reveals structural conditions without which training, however well-designed, 
will fail to produce lasting change:

### Skill Maintenance Requirements
The study acknowledges the limitation of immediate post-training assessment:

> "It is possible that over time investigators may not maintain the skills they 
> demonstrated in this immediate assessment, as previous research has often observed 
> a decline in training effects following extended periods." (p. 64)

Behavioral skills decay. A training intervention that is not supported by ongoing 
practice, supervision, and feedback will produce initial gains that erode. Any 
implementation of evidence-based methods that treats training as a one-time event 
will be disappointed.

### Follow-Up Assessment
The study itself notes the limitation of lacking follow-up assessment:

> "Limitations in funding and the logistics of engaging in follow-up evaluations 
> precluded us from examining the observed training effects following a time delay." (p. 64)

Without follow-up, there is no mechanism for detecting and correcting skill erosion, 
and no accountability for maintaining the trained approach.

### Field Conditions
The study's 20-minute interview format, which investigators found constraining, 
suggests that real-world conditions — including time pressure, case complexity, 
and institutional demands — can undermine the adoption of evidence-based methods 
even in practitioners who genuinely intend to use them. Structural supports (adequate 
time, appropriate case allocation, supervisory encouragement) are prerequisites for 
sustained behavioral change.

## Implications for Agent Systems

The evidence-practice gap maps directly onto a persistent challenge in agent system 
design: the gap between what a system is theoretically capable of and what it actually 
does in deployment.

### The Capability-Deployment Gap
An agent system may have access to sophisticated reasoning capabilities, nuanced 
evaluation methods, or evidence-based decision frameworks — but default to simpler, 
more automatic patterns under time pressure, high load, or in domains where the 
more sophisticated approach is not continuously reinforced. This is the agent-system 
equivalent of practitioners reverting to accusatorial methods under pressure.

### Toolbox Routing Failure
A system that routes tasks to specialized skill modules but doesn't provide those 
modules with the theoretical framework that makes the skills effective is implementing 
a toolbox approach. The skills will be invoked in contexts where they don't work, and 
the result will be an inference that the skill module is ineffective rather than that 
the framework is missing.

### Feedback Requirements for Maintaining Quality
Agent systems require ongoing behavioral feedback — output quality assessment against 
external standards — not just deployment. Without feedback loops that detect when 
agents are producing outputs of the type that emerges from "skill erosion" (defaulting 
to simpler, worse patterns), quality will degrade over time and the degradation will 
be invisible from within the system.

### The Resistant-Case Heuristic
Design and test agent capabilities specifically in the hard cases — the cases where 
the naive, default approach fails — rather than demonstrating average-case performance. 
Evidence of capability in hard cases is far more persuasive and far more indicative 
of genuine competence than evidence of capability in easy cases.

---

## Summary Principle

> **Evidence of effectiveness is necessary but not sufficient for practice change. 
> Entrenched methods persist because practitioners have genuine experiential evidence 
> for them, because the hardest cases (where evidence-based methods most excel) are 
> precisely where practitioners are most skeptical of alternatives, and because selective 
> adoption (toolbox approach) produces evidence-based labels on essentially unchanged 
> practice. Closing the gap requires demonstrating efficacy in hard cases, providing 
> integrated frameworks not technique collections, behavioral practice with external 
> feedback, and structural conditions that sustain the trained approach over time.**
