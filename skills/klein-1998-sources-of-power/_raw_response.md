## BOOK IDENTITY
**Title**: Sources of Power: How People Make Decisions
**Author**: Gary Klein
**Core Question**: How do skilled practitioners actually make good decisions under time pressure, uncertainty, and high stakes — and what does this reveal about the nature of expertise, intuition, and human intelligence?
**Irreplaceable Contribution**: Klein spent years embedding with firefighters, military commanders, nurses, naval officers, and chess masters to observe real decisions in real conditions. What he found demolished the dominant paradigm: experts don't compare options, don't follow decision trees, and don't eliminate uncertainty before acting. Instead, they *recognize* situations and *simulate* outcomes — two cognitive operations that look nothing like rational choice theory. This book is irreplaceable because it contains the actual cognitive mechanisms of expertise, grounded in hundreds of field cases, not laboratory puzzles. No other book captures this with the same depth of evidence and the same humility about what we don't know.

---

## KEY IDEAS

1. **Recognition-Primed Decision Making (RPD)**: Experts don't choose between options — they recognize a situation as a type, see what to do, and simulate whether it will work. In 80% of 156 firefighting decisions Klein studied, commanders generated one option and evaluated it alone. This isn't impulsivity; it's expertise operating at speed.

2. **Mental simulation as the core evaluation tool**: Rather than scoring options against criteria, experts imagine a course of action playing out — running it forward in time, watching for problems. This simulation is bounded: it handles roughly three "moving parts" and six state transitions before working memory collapses. Experts chunk complex sequences into single abstract units to stay within these limits.

3. **Intuition is pattern recognition, not instinct**: When a firefighter evacuates a building he can't explain why, he's detecting an expectancy violation — the heat level, sound pattern, and fire behavior don't match his mental model of the situation. "Intuition grows out of experience," Klein writes. It can be trained, and it can be wrong.

4. **Errors come from system failures, not biased brains**: Klein's review of 600+ decision points found 64% of errors traced to lack of experience, 20% to missing information, and only 16% to mental simulation failures. The operator blamed for an error is usually the victim of latent design failures — bad interfaces, inadequate training, corrupted data.

5. **Expertise is perceptual, not procedural**: The deepest finding is that experts *see differently*. They detect patterns novices can't perceive, notice anomalies (things that *didn't* happen), read the past and future of a situation, and manage their own cognitive limitations. You cannot build this through rules and procedures alone — it requires exposure to thousands of varied cases with accurate feedback.

# Recognition-Primed Decision Making: How Experts Actually Decide

## The Finding That Changed Everything

Gary Klein went to study how experienced fireground commanders make decisions under pressure. He expected to find them comparing options, weighing tradeoffs, selecting the best choice. Instead, he found something that didn't fit any theory he had.

When Klein asked a fireground commander how he made decisions, the man said: **"I don't make decisions. I don't remember when I've ever made a decision."**

Klein pressed him: when you arrive at a fire, how do you decide what to do? The commander said there was no decision — the situation simply told him what to do. He saw the fire, recognized what kind of fire it was, and knew the response. "The structure would burn down by the time he finished listing all the options, let alone evaluating them."

This led Klein to reformulate everything. "We were not studying decision making at all" — not in the classical sense. The commanders weren't choosing between alternatives. They were **recognizing** situations.

## The Recognition-Primed Decision Model

The model Klein built from his field research has three variations:

**Variation 1 (Simple recognition)**: The expert recognizes the situation as familiar, sees what kind of situation it is, understands the reasonable course of action, and acts. No deliberation. No comparison.

**Variation 2 (Ambiguous diagnosis)**: The situation isn't immediately clear. The expert forms hypotheses, runs mental simulations to test them, and identifies the most plausible diagnosis before acting.

**Variation 3 (Complex evaluation)**: The expert recognizes the situation and generates one course of action, then imagines it playing out to check for problems. If problems appear, the action is modified or discarded, and the next most reasonable option is tried.

What's absent in all three variations: **comparing options side by side**. Experts evaluated options one at a time, in sequence. They were not choosing the *best* option; they were identifying the *first workable* option. This is distinct from Herbert Simon's "satisficing" — the expert doesn't just take the first option that clears a bar. They run it through mental simulation and evaluate it on its own merits. If it fails simulation, they move on to the next option, also evaluated alone.

In Klein's study of 156 decision points, **127 (80%) involved recognitional decisions** with no option comparison at all.

## What Recognition Actually Is

Recognition, in Klein's framework, gives the expert four things simultaneously:

1. **Which cues are important** — so information doesn't overwhelm them
2. **What to expect next** — so surprises register as surprises
3. **Relevant goals** — so the situation itself shapes what they're trying to achieve
4. **Typical courses of action** — so the response comes with the recognition

"The commanders' secret was that their experience let them see a situation, even a nonroutine one, as an example of a prototype, so they knew the typical course of action right away."

Experts didn't need perfect information because recognition fills in the gaps. When a fireground commander arrives and recognizes "this is a high-rise fire with vertical spread," the prototype carries with it a whole set of expectations: where the fire is going, what tactics work, what dangers to watch for. The recognition is not a raw percept but a richly structured cognitive object.

## Intuition Is Pattern Recognition

Klein is explicit about what intuition is and is not:

"Intuition depends on the use of experience to recognize key patterns that indicate the dynamics of the situation."

Not: inborn talent. Not: ESP. Not: a feeling that transcends reasoning.

Experts often cannot articulate their intuitions for three reasons Klein identified:
1. Experience didn't give them specific facts to recall — it changed *how they perceive*
2. They were reacting to things that were **not happening** rather than things that were (negative cues, violated expectations)
3. Many similar incidents blended together, so no single memory can be retrieved

The firefighter who evacuates a building moments before the floor collapses isn't accessing a mystical sense. He's running an expectancy model built from hundreds of fires: for this type of fire, the heat should be at a certain level, the noise should sound a certain way, the behavior should follow a certain pattern. When reality violates that model — hotter than expected, suspiciously quiet — his nervous system tells him something is wrong before he can consciously articulate what.

Klein describes the key case: a seemingly routine kitchen fire in a house. The commander's crew sprays water and it "roars back." The living room is hotter than a kitchen fire should make it. The fire is quiet — strange, because fire is noisy, and the floor was acting as a baffle. The whole pattern violated his expectancies. He orders evacuation. The floor collapses beneath where they'd been standing. Fire was in the basement, not the kitchen.

He didn't consciously work through these steps. He felt "something wrong." But the mechanism was: **anomaly detection via pattern mismatch**.

## The Overpass Rescue: Mental Simulation in Action

The clearest illustration of how RPD works in novel situations comes from the overpass rescue case.

A woman is dangling from overpass structural supports, semiconscious. Standard harnesses require the victim to be in a sitting or upright position — she can't do that. The rescue commander thinks through options one by one, imagining each one playing out.

Standard harness from above? The geometry won't work — no good bracing. Standard strap from behind? The fit is wrong. Then he thinks of a **ladder belt** — normally worn by firefighters ascending ladders. He imagines:
- Slide it under her
- Buckle from behind (one buckle needed)
- Tie rope to snap hook
- Lift her up
- Run the sequence again in his mind — looks workable

The decision takes roughly one minute. He executes. The belt doesn't hold — it was designed for sturdy firefighters in heavy coats, and the woman in a thin sweater "slithered through like spaghetti." She's caught by a hook-and-ladder crew member directly below her.

Several things are visible in this case:

First, **singular evaluation**: he considered options one at a time, rejected each, and moved to the next. No comparison of the strap vs. the belt.

Second, **mental simulation as evaluation**: he didn't score the ladder belt against criteria. He imagined it working. The evaluation *was* the simulation.

Third, **the simulation's limits**: he missed the fatal gap (belt sizing). Mental simulation isn't foolproof. But it led him to a creative solution that no standard approach provided.

## What Novices Do Instead

Klein's comparison of novice and expert behavior clarifies the model:

Novices, lacking pattern libraries, cannot recognize situations. Without recognition, they have no starting point — no "here's what kind of situation this is, so here's what we do." So they fall back to what they've been taught: **deliberate option comparison**.

In the Christmas Fire case, volunteer firefighters faced a 50-100 foot wall of flames at an oil tank farm — a fire far outside their experience. Their response: "Our heads turned to stone." They generated options, compared them, argued about them. Over two days of chaos:
- No water source initially
- Only 1,000 gallons of foam (need much more)
- Multiple departments operating independently with no clear commander
- Endless deliberation about ladder truck positioning
- Breakthrough only when someone asked "are all incoming pipes shut off?" — discovering a 22-inch pipe still pumping oil into the burning tanks

"Although the commanders in this case study had been firefighters for a long time, they had no experience with a fire this large. Deliberating about options makes a lot of sense for novices."

The implication: **formal decision analysis is not the ideal — it's compensation for inexperience**. Teaching people to list options and evaluate criteria is teaching them how to cope without expertise, not how to build expertise.

## Where Comparative Evaluation Does Belong

Klein is not saying rational analysis is worthless. His data show clear conditions where comparative evaluation is the right approach:

**RPD is more likely when:**
- Time pressure is high
- Experience level is high
- Conditions are dynamic
- Goals are ill-defined
- No need to justify to authority
- No conflict requiring a common metric

**Comparative evaluation is more likely when:**
- Must justify a choice to an authority
- Resolving conflict between stakeholders with different priorities
- Optimizing (finding the best, not just the first workable)
- Problem is computationally complex and cannot be solved intuitively

The Denver Bullets case illustrates legitimate comparative analysis: a political dispute about which police ammunition to adopt. Ken Hammond's formal analysis elicited value judgments about stopping power, injury severity, and bystander risk, then gathered scientific data on 80 bullet types. The analysis found a bullet that was better on *all* dimensions than either of the two under debate — a result that pure recognition couldn't have produced, because the question required integrating value judgments across stakeholders.

Klein's boundary table (Table 7.1) shows that "experts working alone under time pressure" will use RPD; "multiple stakeholders with conflicting interests" benefit from formal methods. The mistake is using formal methods in all cases, especially the first type.

## Empirical Breadth: RPD Across Domains

Klein and his colleagues compiled data showing RPD's prevalence is not unique to firefighting:

| Domain | RPD % | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Urban firefighters | 80% | 156 decision points |
| Expert fireground (tough decisions) | 58% | Higher bar, more difficult cases |
| Novice fireground | 46% | Experience gap visible |
| Tank platoon leaders (novice) | 42% | Minimal experience |
| Wildland incident commanders | 51% | Functional decisions; 31% organizational |
| Design engineers | 60% | Extended time, no pressure |
| Battle command teams | 96% | Teams; 27 points |
| AEGIS Navy commanders | 95% | High pressure, clear guidelines |

Supporting studies from other researchers:
- Mosier (1991): Boeing 727 crews made "recognitional, almost reflexive judgment based on few critical items; spent bulk of time in situation assessment rather than alternative generation"
- Flin et al. (1996): Offshore oil platform managers — 90% RPD, mean 10% multi-option generation
- Randel et al. (1996): Electronic warfare technicians — 93% serial deliberation; experts emphasized situation assessment, novices emphasized course selection

The pattern holds across domains, time pressures, and team vs. individual settings. It holds even in low-pressure extended-time settings (design engineers at 60%). The constraint isn't time pressure; it's **whether pattern matching is possible**.

## What This Means for How We Train People

Klein is blunt: there is no reason to teach someone to follow the RPD model. It describes what experienced people already do. The question is how to develop that capability.

Three training implications:

**1. Teach by exposing to diverse cases, especially rare ones.** Recognition libraries aren't built by understanding principles; they're built by encountering many instances. The Christmas Fire happened partly because the firefighters had never seen an oil tank fire. Training systems should expose people to common AND rare cases.

**2. Use tactical decision games and cognitive critiques.** Low-fidelity scenarios (paper problems with a 3-5 minute decision window) build pattern recognition without the cost of field exercises. After decisions, structured reflection (cognitive critiques) builds understanding of why decisions were right or wrong.

**3. Build perceptual knowledge, not just procedural knowledge.** The NICU nurses who could detect sepsis before it showed on instruments weren't following rules — they'd developed sensitivity to subtle cue patterns. This requires teaching people what to *notice*, not just what to *do*.

## The Challenge for Instruction

One of Klein's most counterintuitive findings: MIT students being taught Soelberg's rational choice method made gut choices anyway, then used rational analysis to construct post-hoc justifications. When confronted, they defended those justifications as if they were the real reasoning.

This wasn't irrational. It was adaptive. Real decisions don't allow the luxury of comparative analysis. The students learned to recognize what felt right, and then to package that for communication.

Klein's conclusion: the research doesn't condemn intuition. It reveals that intuition is the operating system for expert decision-making, and analytical reasoning is more like a word processor running on top of it — useful for documentation and communication, but not what's actually running the decisions.

The implication for any training system: focus first on building the pattern library. The reasoning can be scaffolded later. But without the patterns, all the reasoning procedures in the world produce nothing but slow, poor decisions dressed in the clothes of rigor.

# Mental Simulation: The Expert's Internal Laboratory

## What Mental Simulation Is

When a fireground commander needs to evaluate a course of action, he doesn't score it against criteria or compare it to alternatives. He **imagines it playing out**. He runs the action forward in time in his mind, watching for problems, feeling for plausibility. If he spots a pitfall, he adjusts or discards the option and tries the next one.

Klein calls this **mental simulation** — and his research across multiple domains revealed it as the primary evaluation tool experts use. It's not metaphor. It's the actual cognitive operation.

"Fireground commanders use the power of mental simulation, running the action through in their minds. If they spot a potential problem... they move on to the next option, and the next, until they find one that seems to work."

Mental simulation is distinct from mere imagining because it has **causal structure**: the expert isn't just picturing a scene, they're tracking how one state causes the next, how variables interact, where the sequence might fail. The simulation is evaluated continuously for plausibility, consistency, and the absence of fatal problems.

## The Three Moving Parts Constraint

Mental simulation has a hard limit. Klein's research with colleagues (coded across 79 cases) found:

- **Maximum three "moving parts"** — causal factors simultaneously tracked
- **Six transitions** — state changes the simulation can sustain before working memory collapses

This is not a failure of human cognition. It's why expertise works the way it does. Experts deal with this constraint by **chunking**: they collapse complex sequences into single abstract units. "Removal of the car roof" becomes one step in the simulation, hiding the multi-person coordination required to actually do it. If they're nervous about a chunked step, they run a separate, lower-level simulation of just that step.

The Polish economy example from Klein's work makes this vivid. In January 1990, as Poland converted overnight from socialism to a market economy, Klein watched expert economist Andrzej Bloch construct a mental simulation of what would happen. Bloch identified three key variables:
- Inflation rate
- Unemployment rate
- Foreign exchange rate

He projected each by quarter for 1990, treating the exchange rate as the balancing mechanism. His inflation simulation:
- Government won't fight inflation artificially
- Current rate (~80%/year) will spike to ~1,000%/year (annualized, meaning ~80%/month)
- Goods become expensive → demand falls → prices stabilize
- Timeline: ~3 months
- Risk: food shortages could topple government, but Solidarity euphoria might hold

**Actual outcome:** Accurate. ~1,000% in January-February, down to 20-25% by April.

Two less-expert observers (an economics student and a Marxist professor) couldn't construct any simulation:
- The student only knew about inflation and unemployment, didn't know baseline rates, had no balancing mechanism
- The professor was ideologically motivated (wanted capitalism to fail) and generated no systematic reasoning

The difference wasn't intelligence. It was the **depth of domain knowledge** that makes it possible to identify *which three variables matter*, to know the baseline rates, to understand the causal mechanisms, and to model how they interact.

"The person assembling a mental simulation needs to have a lot of familiarity with the task and needs to be able to think at the right level of abstraction."

## Mental Simulation for Diagnosis

Mental simulation isn't only for evaluating actions. It's also how experts diagnose what's happening in a situation.

"The diagnosis is a mental simulation that weaves together different events into a story that shows how the causes led to the effects."

Pennington and Hastie's research on jury decisions showed this directly: jurors don't weigh evidence item by item. They **construct narratives** — causal stories — and then ask which story best accounts for the evidence. This is mental simulation applied to retrospective diagnosis.

Physicians do the same. Elstein and colleagues found that doctors form hypotheses from the very beginning of an examination, using those hypotheses to direct their inquiry rather than suppressing explanations until all data is collected. The hypothesis drives the simulation, and the simulation generates predictions that get tested against the patient's responses.

"The more experienced the decision makers are, the more clear-cut the expectancies are."

A confident diagnosis generates strong expectations. When those expectations are violated — when the patient doesn't respond as the simulation predicted — the mismatch is a signal to update or abandon the diagnosis.

## The Sixth Sense: Expectancy Violation as Anomaly Detection

Klein's most powerful example of mental simulation as a diagnostic tool is what he calls "the Sixth Sense" — a firefighting case that has become a touchstone in the field.

A house fire, apparently in the kitchen. The crew is suppressing it. The fire "roars back" when hit with water. The living room is hotter than a kitchen fire should make it. The fire is unusually quiet.

The commander, without being able to articulate why, orders evacuation. The floor collapses beneath where they'd been standing.

What happened? The commander's experience had built an expectancy model for fires: for a kitchen fire, the heat should be at a certain level, the acoustics should be a certain way (fire is noisy; the floor of a burning basement acts as a baffle, muting it), the fire's response to water should follow a pattern. When reality violated all three expectations, his system flagged "something wrong."

He wasn't detecting the actual condition (basement fire). He was detecting that reality didn't match his model. The conclusion — something is wrong, get out — was correct even without knowing the specific cause.

This is the functional form of intuition: **not ESP, not a mystical sense, but a discrepancy detector** built from accumulated experience with patterns. The expert's mental model generates predictions. When predictions fail, the expert gets a signal before they can consciously identify what's wrong.

## Mental Simulation and the "De Minimus" Trap

Mental simulation can also be the source of major errors. The mechanism is what Klein, following Charles Perrow's *Normal Accidents*, calls **de minimus explanations** — the process of explaining away warning signs to preserve a mental model you're invested in.

Once you've constructed a simulation that makes sense of a situation, contradicting evidence gets explained away rather than incorporated:
- "They must have built the dock since this morning" (when you see an unexpected dock on a lake that supposedly has none)
- "That must be a family trait" (a nurse explaining away a distended belly in a baby who actually has necrotizing enterocolitis)

The nurse in the NICU case is crucial. She notices three warning signs:
- Distended stomach → "like her sister had"
- Blood in stool → "just the nasogastral tube"
- 3cc aspirate → "not unusual by itself"

Each symptom is individually rationalized. The problem isn't that she ignores evidence. She notices it. But each anomaly gets a local explanation that preserves her existing mental model. The catastrophic failure is that she never *assembles the pattern*.

Michael Cohen's research suggests mental simulations self-correct when explanations accumulate until they become "too complicated" — a process he calls "snap-back." But there's no reliable mechanism for knowing when you've crossed that threshold. The de minimus strategy is rational in the short term (don't rebuild your whole model every time you see a surprising piece of evidence) but dangerous when the model is fundamentally wrong.

Two techniques help break this trap:
1. **The premortem** (also called the crystal ball technique): Imagine the plan has failed. Work backward from failure to explain why. This forces surface of failure modes the forward-running simulation missed.
2. **Hypothetical probes**: "If this diagnosis were wrong, what else could explain these symptoms?" Klein used this in field interviews (Marvin Cohen's probe: "Imagine that didn't happen. Why not?") to surface hidden assumptions in experts' mental models.

## The Libyan Airliner: When Mental Simulations Collide

The 1973 shootdown of Libyan Arab Airlines Flight 114 by Israeli fighters reveals what happens when two parties are each running coherent but incompatible simulations.

**Israeli commanders' simulation**: "If I were a civilian captain, I'd land when given clear signals. This captain sees our insignia, sees the airfield, lowers landing gear, then... retracts it and flies away? Only a terrorist would do this." Conclusion: shoot it down.

**The Libyan crew's simulation** (reconstructed from the black box): "We've overshot Cairo West. Those must be Egyptian MiGs warning us away from Cairo East. Why are friendly Egyptian escorts shooting at us? They're crazy. We'll turn to avoid the military base and fly toward the civilian airport."

Each simulation was internally consistent. Each led to coherent action. Each was based on false initial assumptions about who the other party was and what they wanted. The pilots didn't know they were over Israeli-controlled Sinai. The Israelis couldn't simulate "a legitimate captain takes this many risks" — because a legitimate captain wouldn't.

108 people died.

The lesson is not that mental simulation is unreliable. The lesson is that simulation is **powerful in proportion to the accuracy of its starting assumptions**, and dangerous precisely when those starting assumptions are wrong but internally consistent.

## Mental Simulation in Chess: Empirical Validation

Klein's chess studies provide the clearest empirical validation of how mental simulation generates options.

**Study 1** (Calderwood, Klein, Crandall 1988): Masters versus Class B players under regulation versus blitz (6 seconds per move) conditions.
- Masters maintained high move quality at both speeds
- Class B players' blunder rate jumped from 11% to 25% under time pressure; masters stayed at 7-8%

This directly tests the RPD model: if experts generate options by simulation rather than deliberate analysis, they should be relatively unaffected by time pressure that devastates deliberate analysis. The data confirm this.

**Study 2** (Klein, Wolf, Militello, Zsambok 1995): Players think aloud in mid-game positions and name all moves considered. After all boards, they rate all legal moves on quality.

Results:
- Players rated their first moves considered as highly promising — much higher than the average legal move
- Objectively (rated by grandmasters): 4 out of 6 first moves were "worth considering" — against a random expectation of 1 in 6

An objection Klein addresses: "This is obvious — people naturally generate good options first." But if that's true, it refutes the advice that problem-solvers should generate large option sets. And there's a deeper point: the number of possible moves (including things like throwing your drink at the opponent) is effectively infinite. There must be a **generative mechanism** that produces only reasonable options without consciously filtering bad ones.

That mechanism is pattern recognition triggering mental simulation — the recognition of a situation type that carries with it the relevant response classes.

## How Mental Simulation Differs from Visualization

Klein notes a common misunderstanding: mental simulation is not the same as visual imagery.

"Some people do this with visual imagery... Others claim that they figure it out logically without ever using visual images."

The key element isn't the modality (visual vs. abstract) but the **causal reasoning**: tracking how one state changes another, how that changed state interacts with other elements, what the resulting configuration implies. The simulation is a *causal chain*, not a movie.

This matters for implementation: when building systems that reason about action sequences, the goal is not to produce visual renderings but to track **transitions and state changes** with appropriate attention to interactions between elements.

## Mental Simulation as the Bridge Between Intuition and Analysis

Klein's framework positions mental simulation as the mechanism that connects intuitive pattern recognition with deliberate evaluation.

Pattern recognition gets you to a candidate action quickly. Mental simulation tests whether that candidate is actually workable. If the simulation reveals a problem, you adjust the action or discard it — and recognition generates the next candidate.

This means expert decision-making isn't purely automatic (just pattern recognition) or purely analytical (comparing options against criteria). It's a **recognition-simulation cycle**: fast recognition to generate candidates, slower simulation to evaluate them, with the simulation drawing on the same experiential knowledge that drove the recognition.

The entire cycle can happen in seconds for a highly practiced expert in a familiar domain. It may take minutes for novel problems where the recognition is less confident and the simulation requires more careful construction.

What's consistent across all these cases: **the process never requires comparison between simultaneously-held options**. Options are generated one at a time, tested one at a time, and either kept or discarded. The comparison that classical decision theory assumes — option A vs. option B vs. option C — simply doesn't appear in naturalistic settings. Experts find a workable option and act.

# Expertise: The Power to See the Invisible

## The Fundamental Claim

"The accumulation of experience does not weigh people down; it lightens them up."

"Experts see the world differently. They see things the rest of us cannot. Often experts do not realize that the rest of us are unable to detect what seems obvious to them."

This is Klein's most fundamental claim about expertise: it is primarily **perceptual**, not procedural. The traditional model holds that experts know more rules, facts, and procedures. Klein's research inverts this. Expertise is *learning to see* — developing sensitivity to patterns, anomalies, and dynamics that are invisible to the novice.

Rules are almost incidental. In fact, CPR instructors — who knew the rules better than anyone — were *worse* at recognizing expert performance than paramedics who had stopped thinking about rules years ago.

## Eight Dimensions of Expert Vision

Klein's research across multiple domains identified eight things experts perceive that novices don't:

### 1. Patterns

Experts recognize typicality — what kind of situation this is — instantly. A veteran nurse looks at a premature baby's behavior and knows something is wrong before she can articulate what. A fireground commander walks in and immediately categorizes the fire type, which brings with it an entire set of expectations, goals, and tactics.

Klein describes this using a rhodopsin analogy from his own experience. After having one eye shielded from light for a period, that eye regenerated rhodopsin and became hypersensitive. When both eyes had different sensitivities, the contrast was so disorienting he hallucinated that one eye had "fallen out." The analogy: **the expert eye sees patterns invisible to the novice eye, and cannot see what it is missing** — doesn't know what darkness feels like. Experts often cannot understand why novices find situations confusing, because the patterns are so obvious to them.

### 2. Anomalies

Crucially, experts perceive **negative cues** — things that *didn't* happen, patterns that *should* be present but aren't. Sherlock Holmes and the dog that didn't bark. The fireground commander who noticed the unusual quiet. The NICU nurse who recognized that a baby that always cried when weighed was suddenly not crying.

Novices cannot detect negative cues because they don't form expectancies. Without expecting the bark, you can't notice its absence. Expectancy models, built through experience, are what make anomaly detection possible.

### 3. The Big Picture

Experts maintain situation awareness — an understanding of what's happening at a level that makes sense of the details. They see which factors matter, which can be ignored, and how everything fits together. Novices see details in isolation.

### 4. The Way Things Work

Experts have mental models of how systems behave — how subtasks integrate, how equipment can fail, how people will react. A navy electronics technician who called his console "a liar" had a model of its failure modes. He knew when to trust it and when not to. This is fundamentally different from knowing the console's operating manual.

### 5. Opportunities and Improvisations

Experts spot leverage points — small differences that make large differences. They see the resource or timing window that novices overlook. And they can improvise, combining elements in ways that protocols don't specify, because they understand *why* each element works.

### 6. The Past and the Future

Experts can mentally simulate both how the current situation arose and where it will go. Weather forecasters who are true experts can imagine the sequence of meteorological events that would cause the ceiling to lift by noon rather than 2 PM. Novices can only report their official forecast; they cannot generate alternative futures because they don't understand the causal mechanisms well enough.

Klein tested this directly: a weather forecaster who had correctly predicted a 2 PM ceiling lift was asked what sequence of events might produce a noon lift instead. He couldn't do it. He had followed rules to generate the prediction and couldn't imagine a different world. The ceiling actually lifted by 1 PM. He was not an expert.

### 7. Fine Discriminations

Experts detect differences too small for novices to perceive. Wine tasting. X-ray interpretation. Radiologists at a conference, when presented with what organizers intended as a prank — a doctored double-exposure X-ray — detected the doctoring and hypothesized the superimposition of two different lungs. Their sensitivity to the *statistics of normality* was so refined that the anomaly was visible to them.

### 8. Managing Cognitive Limitations

Experts know their own mental limitations and design around them. They know when their situation awareness is slipping. They adapt strategies to avoid their weaknesses. An expert student in a timed exam identifies a hard problem, estimates it will take too long, skims it to let the subconscious work on it, moves to other problems, and returns. This is metacognitive decision-making — allocating cognitive resources based on self-knowledge.

## The CPR Instructors Study: Rules vs. Perceptual Expertise

Klein and Beth Crandall created six videotapes of CPR performance: five from novices just completing an 8-hour training course, one from a paramedic with years of field experience. Three audiences evaluated the tapes: novices, CPR instructors, and paramedics.

**Results:**
- Paramedics: 9 of 10 correctly identified the paramedic's tape
- Novices: 5 of 10 (better than chance)
- **Instructors: 3 of 10** — worse than chance

The instructors had *procedural expertise*: they knew where to place hands, how to count compressions, what the protocol required. But their expert was violating those rules — the movements were smooth and integrated in a way that couldn't be decomposed into the taught elements. The instructors saw violation where the paramedics saw mastery.

The instructors were teaching a choppy, decomposed version of CPR because they couldn't teach the integrated performance — and they didn't realize experts had moved beyond the rules into something that *looked like* rule violation but was actually fluency.

This is one of the clearest demonstrations that **procedural expertise and perceptual expertise are different things**. You can know all the rules and still be unable to recognize mastery.

## The Recipe for Disaster: Expertise as Multi-Layered Perception

Klein traces his own incompetence at cooking through several layers:

**Perception**: He doesn't know what "brown on top" means — the spectrum from pale to smoking is opaque to him. He can't see what an expert cook sees.

**Mental Models**: He can't imagine how ingredients will taste together. He doesn't know typical potato size, so he can't adjust the recipe. He doesn't understand why steps exist, so he can't adapt when conditions change.

**Metacognition**: He doesn't account for his own speed (grating potatoes takes him longer than the recipe assumes). He doesn't know his ability to judge doneness. He can't estimate whether the dish will be ready when guests arrive.

**Fine Discrimination**: He misses the negative cue — lack of bubbling around edges means the dish isn't done. He sees darkening on top but can't distinguish "done" from "burning."

Procedural rules (temperature X, time Y) are useless without perceptual calibration. Expertise is the learned ability to *interpret* the conditions that rules refer to. The rules don't stand alone; they're intelligible only to someone who can see what they're pointing at.

## Time Horizon and Decentering

Klein describes two related expert perceptual capabilities that novices lack:

**Time horizon**: The ability to see far enough ahead that your current actions can affect the future. Jacobs and Jaques (1991) found that different organizational levels require different time horizons:
- Line managers: minutes to hours
- Middle managers: weeks
- Senior managers: months to years
- Presidents: 5-10 years ahead

This isn't just strategic thinking. It's perceptual — seeing further ahead means the future is visible, not just calculated.

**Decentering**: The ability to see the situation from another perspective — especially an adversary's or observer's perspective. Tank platoon leaders in training would take cover at positions that felt right. Their instructors immediately spotted the flaws — because the instructors had *decentered*, imagining approaching from the enemy's direction, seeing what the enemy would see.

The trainees were college-educated, some from West Point. The instructors were enlisted personnel without degrees. Intelligence wasn't the difference. Decentering through experience was.

## The Conditions for Expertise Development

Klein and James Shanteau identified the conditions under which genuine expertise develops versus where it fails to develop:

**Conditions enabling expertise:**
- Rapid, accurate feedback from the environment
- Clear causal links between action and outcome
- Many repeated trials with variation
- Stable underlying dynamics

**Conditions preventing expertise:**
- Dynamic, unpredictable domain
- Need to predict human behavior
- Long delays between action and outcome
- Few repeated trials
- Tangled, confounded causality

This produces an uncomfortable conclusion: **politicians, stock pickers, and many corporate strategists don't actually develop expertise in their domains**. The feedback is too slow, the causality too tangled, and the confounds too numerous. They develop routines and self-confidence, which looks like expertise — but their mental models aren't reliably richer or more accurate than laypersons'.

Klein quotes Franklin Roosevelt's economic interventions: "Some historians and economists argue that these actions did the trick; others claim that they made the problem worse. The Great Depression was an event of major importance... that has received enormous scrutiny, and we still do not know if Roosevelt's actions were effective."

If we can't pin down the causal effects of major historical events with decades of analysis, the actors within those events certainly couldn't build expertise from the feedback.

The implication is not nihilistic but clarifying: invest in building expertise where it can actually develop (domains with fast, accurate feedback, repeated trials, stable dynamics), and be appropriately humble about claimed expertise in domains where these conditions don't hold.

## Extracting Tacit Knowledge: Cognitive Task Analysis

Since experts can't simply articulate what they know (their knowledge is perceptual, not propositional), Klein developed methods to extract it through structured elicitation.

The **Critical Decision Method (CDM)** uses a four-pass interview structure:

**Pass 1 — Brief version**: Get the story quickly. Does it have good potential? Which parts to probe deeply?

**Pass 2 — Full timeline**: Pin down the sequence of events. Diagram knowledge transformations. Note situation awareness at each stage. Catch inconsistencies.

**Pass 3 — Probe thought processes**: What triggered assessment changes? What alternative goals existed? What other options were available? Hypotheticals: "If information X hadn't arrived, what would you have done?" Cohen's probe: "Imagine that didn't happen. Why not? What could explain such a thing?" — this uncovers hidden assumptions.

**Pass 4 — Novice vulnerability analysis**: At each choice point, could a novice get confused? "If I were pressed into service, would I see this the same way? What mistakes might I make? Why?" This directly elicits the expert's perception of the knowledge gap.

Klein gives the example of Beth Crandall's work with NICU nurses. Asked how they detected sepsis: "experience and intuition." Stories about specific incidents revealed specific perceptual cues — including nearly half that were not in the medical literature, and some that were *opposite* to adult sepsis patterns (adults become more irritable; premature babies become *less* irritable). A baby that always cries when weighed suddenly not crying? Danger sign. Crandall compiled a master list, validated it with neonatology specialists, and created training materials embedding the cues in stories and context.

This is the practical form of the rhodopsin principle: you can't just tell people what to see. But you can put them in contact with stories that include the cues, contextualize them, and let perceptual learning happen through structured exposure.

## Knowledge Engineering: The Petroleum Analogy

Klein uses an analogy to frame the organizational problem of tacit expertise:

| Process | Petroleum | Knowledge |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Search | Where to drill | Where expertise is located |
| Evaluate | Whether to drill | Is the knowledge worth extracting? |
| Extract | Drilling rigs | Knowledge elicitation methods |
| Process | Refining | Codifying into usable form |
| Apply | Heat and power | Training, system design, corporate memory |

Most organizations treat expertise as having no value until it's gone — letting a 30-year veteran walk out the door with irreplaceable tacit knowledge, then struggling to fill the gap with new hires who start the learning cycle over. Klein argues expertise should be treated as a **strategic resource** with explicit effort invested in its location, extraction, and preservation.

One AT&T software debugging project spent significant resources on expert knowledge elicitation, then deployed the resulting materials to reach many engineers. The investment paid for itself many times over. The calculation is the same as petroleum: if the knowledge is there, and it's valuable, the question is only whether the extraction cost justifies the yield.

## The Dreyfus Inversion

Dreyfus and Dreyfus made a fundamental point that Klein's research confirms from a different direction: novices follow rules; experts do not. Learning to ride a bicycle isn't about mastering training wheels — it's about *outgrowing* them. The training wheels enable movement but prevent the development of balance. They have to be removed.

Rule-based instruction does the same thing. Procedures enable performance at the novice level but can actively prevent the development of the integrated, perception-driven expertise that characterizes mastery. When CPR instructors taught decomposed, rule-following CPR, they were helping beginners perform adequately while creating a ceiling beyond which they could not teach.

The implication for training design: identify at what point rules should be *replaced* by principle-based understanding, and at what point principle-based understanding should give way to perceptual mastery that transcends articulation. Continuing to teach rules to people ready for the next stage doesn't just fail to help — it may actively impede development.

# Errors, Uncertainty, and the Limits of Decision-Making

## Rethinking What a Poor Decision Is

Klein opens his analysis of errors with a definition that cuts against most of how we evaluate decisions:

"A person will consider a decision to be poor if the knowledge gained would lead to a different decision if a similar situation arose. Simply knowing that the outcome was unfavorable should not matter. Knowing what you failed to consider would matter."

This is not how organizations typically evaluate decisions. They evaluate outcomes. A decision that led to a bad outcome gets scrutinized; one that led to a good outcome gets celebrated. Klein argues this confounds decision quality with luck.

A genuinely poor decision is one where the decision process itself was flawed — where available information was ignored, where mental models were demonstrably wrong, where the reasoning was inadequate to the situation. A good decision that happened to produce a bad outcome is not a poor decision; it's evidence that the world is uncertain.

This distinction matters enormously for learning. If you evaluate by outcomes, you learn the wrong lessons. You punish good decisions that got unlucky and reward bad decisions that got lucky. The incentives corrupt the learning process.

## What Actually Causes Errors

Klein reviewed over 600 decision points across multiple domains and categorized 25 error types. The breakdown:

- **64% — Lack of experience**: The decision-maker lacked the mental models and pattern libraries to recognize the situation correctly
- **20% — Lack of information**: Critical information was unavailable, not received, or not in usable form
- **16% — Mental simulation failure**: The de minimus error — explaining away warning signs to preserve a favored explanation

The majority of errors traced to experience or information, not to reasoning failures. This has profound implications: **blaming operators for biased reasoning is usually wrong**. The operator is often the victim of latent design failures — poor training, bad interfaces, missing information — not the perpetrator of cognitive errors.

James Reason's concept of **latent pathogens** captures this: accidents don't happen because one operator made one mistake. They happen because a system has accumulated latent failures (poor design, inadequate training, inconsistent procedures) that wait for the right triggering conditions. The operator who "caused" the accident merely activated a trap that was already set.

## The Vincennes Shootdown: A Case Study in System Failure

On July 3, 1988, the USS Vincennes shot down Iran Air Flight 655, killing all 290 people aboard. The incident became a textbook case in decision-making research — typically cited as a tragic example of "expectancy bias" or "scenario fulfillment." Klein systematically dismantles this interpretation.

**The setup**: The Vincennes was engaged in surface combat with Iranian gunboats. A contact appeared on radar, rising from Bandar Abbas airport. The crew assigned track number 4474; the USS Sides assigned 4131; the computer merged them to 4131. The crew reported Mode II IFF (military aircraft), no radar lock, radio silence, and descent toward the ship.

**The problem — Table 6.1 shows it clearly**:
- F-14 story: Radar off (could use P-3 for targeting), Mode III IFF (Iran had faked this before), radio silence (hostile), descent (attack profile) ✓
- Commercial airliner story: Fly into surface battle? ✗ Not on schedule? ✗ Off centerline? ✗ Mode II signal? ✗ Descending? ✗

**What actually happened:**
1. **Mode II error**: The range gate had been left open from track 4474; a military aircraft coincidentally took off nearby; the crew saw *that aircraft's* Mode II signal, not Flight 655's
2. **Descent error**: Two crews were tracking two different aircraft (4474 vs. 4131); under ship pitch and time pressure, many punched the wrong track number into their keypads; some saw ascending, others descending
3. **Radio silence**: The commercial crew was monitoring Bandar Abbas departure frequency and Dubai arrival frequency — neither is the international distress channel

The decision had a 189-second window from first contact to engagement. Altitude was displayed as a 4-digit number on a *separate screen*, requiring 5-10 seconds of focused attention to read and another second to interpret the trend. During those 189 seconds, while the ship was pitching in combat, under noise, with split attention, this information was effectively inaccessible.

Klein's argument: "The cues were all consistent with a hostile aircraft: The IFF report showing that it was an F-14. The failure to respond to radio warnings. The timing of the takeoff to correspond to the attack by the gunboats. The final descent toward the Vincennes."

And then: "In order to reject this scenario fulfillment or expectancy bias explanation, we need to come up with a better one... the computer screens that the crew members worked with were not easy to read."

**The real diagnosis**: Track number recycling without clear announcement, altitude data on a secondary display without trend visualization, range gate errors from prior operations, crews tracking different contacts — these are system design failures. Context: 83% of challenges in the Gulf (June-July 1988) were to Iranian military; only 1.3% to commercial aircraft. If the Vincennes had *not* fired and the aircraft had been an attacking F-14, researchers would attribute that to "expectancy bias in reverse."

"This means that the decision bias approach explains too much." An explanation that explains everything explains nothing.

## The Heuristics and Biases Problem

The Kahneman-Tversky-Slovic research program identified numerous systematic deviations from rational choice theory: anchoring, availability bias, representativeness heuristic, confirmation bias, and many others. This framework became enormously influential, generating a picture of humans as fundamentally flawed reasoners.

Klein doesn't reject this research, but he argues it's been badly misinterpreted. The original work aimed to uncover cognitive processes, not prove inherent bias. And its critics have identified serious problems:

**Lola Lopes's critique**: The research confounds performance errors with genuine cognitive limitations. When you test people on statistics problems without giving them any statistical training, they perform poorly. But this tells us nothing about their everyday reasoning, which doesn't take the form of formal probability estimation.

**The irony of confirmation bias**: "One of the primary 'biases' is confirmation bias... Yet one of the most common strategies of scientific research is to derive a prediction from a favorite theory and test it... Researchers working in the heuristics and biases paradigm condemn this sort of bias in their subjects, even as those same researchers perform more laboratory studies confirming their theories."

The accusers are performing the accused behavior.

**The ecological validity problem**: Lab experiments use abstract problems (probability puzzles, verbal gambles) under conditions of no feedback, no meaningful stakes, and no domain expertise. Naturalistic decision-making researchers find that when they test people in their domains of expertise, with realistic stakes and feedback structures, performance looks much better.

The critical question isn't "are humans biased reasoners?" but "under what conditions does human reasoning work well, and under what conditions does it fail?"

## Four Sources of Uncertainty

Schmitt and Klein (1996) identified four distinct sources of uncertainty that experts navigate:

1. **Missing information** — unavailable because no one collected it, or available but not received
2. **Unreliable information** — low credibility due to source problems or distortion in transmission
3. **Ambiguous/conflicting information** — multiple plausible interpretations of the same data
4. **Complex information** — too many interacting elements to integrate straightforwardly

These require different responses. Missing information can sometimes be waited for; sometimes you must proceed with assumptions. Unreliable information calls for corroboration or confidence discounting. Ambiguous information calls for mental simulation of alternative interpretations. Complex information calls for decomposition into manageable elements.

**The critical insight**: Better technology doesn't eliminate uncertainty. When radar was introduced to shipping to prevent collisions in poor visibility, ships increased speed — so accident rates stayed roughly constant. Better information enabled faster decision cycles, which absorbed the safety margin the new information created.

"Previously, information was missing because no one had collected it; in the future, information will be missing because no one can find it."

The information age hasn't reduced uncertainty; it's changed its form. The decision-maker still has to make judgments under incomplete information; now the incompleteness comes from being unable to find the relevant signal in an enormous amount of data rather than from having too little data.

## How Great Commanders Handle Uncertainty

Klein's research on effective commanders reveals a consistent pattern: **they accept uncertainty rather than trying to eliminate it**, and they shape the battlefield to shift the uncertainty burden to the adversary.

Grant, Rommel, and other commanders who consistently won against larger or better-equipped forces shared several characteristics:
- Acted on incomplete information without paralysis
- Maintained flexibility rather than over-committing to detailed plans
- Focused on creating conditions where the enemy's uncertainty was greater than their own
- Appreciated that chance would always play a role, and worked with this rather than against it

"Skilled decision makers appear to know when to wait and when to act. Most important, they accept the need to act despite uncertainty."

The contrast with bureaucratic decision-making is stark. Bureaucratic processes often require certainty before action, which produces paralysis when certainty is unavailable (always) and creates the illusion of control when certainty is claimed (often falsely).

## Superstition and the Causality Problem

Klein makes an uncomfortable observation about domains where expertise cannot develop:

"Our lives are just as governed by superstitions as those of less advanced cultures. The content of the superstitions has changed but not the degree to which they control us. The reason is that for many important aspects of our lives, we cannot pin down the causal relationships."

We pass laws without evidence they work. Corporations reorganize without proof that the new structure helps. We follow management fads. Politicians claim expertise in governing — but governance is a domain with long feedback delays, many confounds, and few controlled trials. The feedback loop that builds genuine expertise is broken.

This is not a minor critique. It means that much of what we treat as expertise in important domains — politics, organizational management, long-term investing, child-rearing — may be ritual and superstition dressed in the clothes of experience.

James Shanteau's conditions for real expertise development:
- Domain is stable (not highly dynamic)
- No need to predict human behavior primarily
- Rapid feedback available
- Task is repetitive with variation
- Many trials possible

Few high-stakes domains meet all these criteria. The implication is that we should be much more humble about claiming expertise, and much more curious about which claimed experts are genuinely better than novices versus which merely have smoother routines.

## The De Minimus Error: Noticing and Explaining Away

Klein identifies a subtle but critical failure mode: **the de minimus error** — noticing warning signs but explaining each one away rather than recognizing the pattern they constitute.

The NICU nurse noticed:
- Distended stomach ("like her sister had")
- Blood in stool ("just from the nasogastral tube")
- 3cc aspirate ("not unusual by itself")

Each symptom has a local explanation. The problem isn't that she's ignoring evidence — she's noticing it. The problem is that she's never *assembling the pattern*. Necrotizing enterocolitis announces itself through a combination of these signs; individually, each sign is ambiguous. Recognition requires seeing the gestalt, not just the parts.

This is different from confirmation bias (seeking evidence that confirms your hypothesis). It's closer to **local coherence without global integration**: each piece of evidence is individually contextualized, and the contextualizations prevent the overall pattern from surfacing.

Klein and Perrow suggest this error is especially dangerous because it feels like good reasoning. You have an explanation for each anomaly. You're not ignoring data. You're being appropriately contextual. The failure is invisible until catastrophe.

Two interventions help:
1. **Premortem technique**: Before finalizing a diagnosis, explicitly imagine it's wrong and ask "what would we be missing if this explanation were incorrect?"
2. **Cue checklists**: For domains with known de minimus error risks (NICU, aviation, military intelligence), explicit pattern checklists force integration across symptoms rather than individual contextualization

## Stress and Decision Quality

A common assumption: stress degrades decision quality, particularly by warping judgment. Klein's research complicates this significantly.

"Stressors such as time pressure, noise, and ambiguity, result in the following effects: The stressors do not give us a chance to gather as much information. The stressors disrupt our ability to use our working memory to sort things out. The stressors distract our attention from the task at hand."

The mechanism is **information limitation and cognitive load** — not warped judgment. When decision quality degrades under stress, the primary cause is that the decision-maker has less information and less cognitive capacity for integration. The reasoning isn't biased; it's starved.

This distinction matters because it points to different solutions:
- If the problem were biased judgment, you'd need cognitive debiasing techniques
- If the problem is information limitation, you need better information design and selective attention training
- If the problem is cognitive load, you need to reduce extraneous demands and build automaticity through practice

The chess evidence makes this concrete: master chess players under extreme time pressure (6 seconds per move) maintain move quality and keep blunder rates at 7-8%. Class B players' blunder rates jump from 11% to 25%. The difference is that masters use recognition-based strategies that are fast and robust under cognitive load; Class B players use deliberative strategies that require more processing time.

Under stress, you still get expertise-driven recognition. What you don't get is time for deliberation and option comparison. If your decision-making relies on recognition, stress hurts you less. If it relies on deliberation, stress hurts you greatly.

This reframes training priorities: develop robust recognition rather than teaching deliberative procedures that won't function under realistic operational conditions.

# Communicating Intent and the Team Mind: Distributed Cognition Under Pressure

## The Fundamental Challenge of Coordination

When Klein asks a veteran assistant (Barbara Law) to edit a manuscript, she does more than correct prose. She finds number discrepancies with a prior study, realizes the sponsor (Jim Banks) recently saw that prior study, infers Klein wouldn't want Banks to discover mismatches, and holds up the draft until the discrepancies are resolved — without being asked.

She accomplished this by knowing Klein's priorities, past concerns, and long-term goals well enough to simulate what he would want in a situation he hadn't anticipated. She could "read his mind."

This is the ideal of coordination: subordinates who understand intent well enough to make good decisions in situations the principal couldn't foresee. It requires two parties to extend themselves: the person delegating must communicate *why*, not just *what*; the person receiving must imagine what the other person really wants when situations don't fit the instructions.

The alternative — complete specification of all relevant details in advance — is impossible. "Usually we cannot specify all these details in advance. If you are doing a favor for me, I have to rely on your ability to read my mind and imagine how I would have made all the choices. I do not believe I could anticipate all the relevant details to tell you."

## The Seven Facets of Intent

Klein's research identified seven components of effective intent communication. Together they create a picture complete enough that subordinates can operate independently and improvise appropriately:

1. **Purpose** (higher-level goals) — the big picture of why this matters
2. **Objective** (desired outcome image) — what success looks like concretely
3. **Sequence of steps** (the plan) — how to proceed under normal conditions
4. **Rationale** (reasoning behind the plan) — why this plan and not another
5. **Key decisions** (contingencies and priorities) — what to watch for and how to respond
6. **Antigoals** (unwanted outcomes) — what NOT to do, which situations to avoid
7. **Constraints and considerations** — relevant context the subordinate may not have

Karl Weick's distillation of effective intent communication: "Here's what I think we face. Here's what I think we should do. Here's why. Here's what we should keep our eye on. Now, talk to me."

This is not a long document. It's a brief statement of situation, intention, reasoning, and critical uncertainties — followed by an invitation to respond. The art is giving *enough* to enable autonomy without so much that the main points get buried.

"The more details you pack in, the more you obscure your main points. However, if you leave out an important consideration, you run the risk that the person will become confused at a critical decision point."

## The Goeben Disaster: When Intent Fails

At the start of World War I, the German battleship Goeben was in the Mediterranean. Churchill ordered British naval forces: "Hunt down the Goeben but do not at this stage be brought to action against superior forces."

Churchill's intent: destroy the Goeben. The warning about superior forces was directed at the Austrian battle fleet.

Admiral Troubridge's interpretation: The Goeben is larger than any of my ships. It could be considered a "superior force." To avoid engaging without safety guarantees, he should regroup.

The Goeben escaped through the Dardanelles. The Ottoman Empire entered the war on the German side. Russian shipping was bottled up. This contributed to the Russian Revolution. The British Mandate in the Near East was shaped by these events.

One ambiguous phrase, one interpretive failure. Churchill failed to communicate the *intent* (destroy the Goeben at all costs). The order, taken literally, supported Troubridge's decision. The intent would have demanded the opposite.

## The Fatal Tug-of-War: Conflicting Intents

A surgeon is operating and orders a lowering of the patient's blood pressure. The anesthesiologist receives the instruction but interprets it through her own standing intent: keep vital signs stable.

When blood pressure drops, the anesthesiologist increases BP-raising medication. The surgeon sees BP rising, orders more pressure-lowering medication. The cycle continues until the patient dies.

Neither party was irrational. Each was following their own intent, which was incompatible with the other's. The surgeon never explained *why* he wanted lower pressure. The anesthesiologist had no way to know her standing intent was in conflict. Both were pursuing what they believed was correct.

This illustrates why intent communication must include *rationale* — not just what to do but why. "Lower blood pressure" is an instruction; "lower blood pressure because we need to reduce bleeding at the surgical site, which takes priority over normal vital signs during this phase" is intent. The second allows the anesthesiologist to understand when her standing goals should yield.

## Marvin's Mouse: The Trap of Specifying Means Without Rationale

Marvin's computer mouse fails. He wants an exact replacement from the same manufacturer. He writes a purchase request including the old purchase order number and the part number.

The front office receives the request and interprets the old purchase order as indicating a preferred vendor — not a required specific model. They order the closest price-equivalent mouse from that vendor. It's incompatible with his machine.

Marvin provided complete procedural specification. He specified the means in detail. What he didn't provide was rationale: why he wanted this specific item (compatibility with his specific hardware configuration). The front office lacked the technical knowledge to infer the compatibility requirement from the documentation he provided.

The fix is simple: "I need an inexpensive mouse that works with [specific machine]." This communicates intent (hardware compatibility is the constraint) rather than means (this specific product from this specific vendor). Anyone receiving that request can make sensible decisions when the specified means aren't available.

## The Prussian Principle: Training for Independent Judgment

After the Napoleonic wars, the Prussian Army undertook a systematic reform. One celebrated episode captures the cultural shift:

A major excused a tactical blunder by explaining that he was following orders. Prince Frederick Charles responded: "His Majesty made you a major because he believed you would know when **not** to obey his orders."

The Prussian military culture trained officers to understand intent deeply enough to know when literal compliance would violate the underlying purpose. They were trained *not* to follow orders blindly — but to follow the intent behind the orders, even when that required departure from the letter of the instruction.

This stands in stark contrast to the organizational structures that emerged in the 20th century, which often trained compliance rather than judgment. The problem: compliance works only when instructions are correct. In dynamic, unpredictable situations, instructions become obsolete before they're fully executed. Only intent survives contact with a changing reality.

## The Research Gap: Intent in Practice

Klein and colleagues analyzed 35 real Commander's Intent statements from military operations:

- Only 14 (40%) mentioned purpose at all
- Only 5 (14%) included antigoals
- Only 13 (37%) addressed key decisions
- Average expert rating: below midpoint, closer to "very ineffective" than "very effective"

Shattuck (1995) asked battalion commanders how they'd want subordinates to respond to unexpected developments, then asked company commanders how they would respond. **Only 34% match** between what commanders wanted and what subordinates said they'd do.

This isn't because people are incompetent. It's because communicating intent is genuinely difficult — it requires making implicit reasoning explicit, anticipating what subordinates might misunderstand, and finding the minimum sufficient statement that enables autonomy without creating ambiguity.

## Machines Need to Communicate Intent Too

On a B-727 at 30,000 feet, a passenger accidentally kicked the rudder control blade. The Flight Management System compensated automatically. Pilots noticed nothing — the system didn't signal that it was compensating.

Eventually the system reached its limits and handed back control to pilots who had no idea anything had been wrong. They were startled by the sudden change in aircraft behavior, initially misdiagnosed it as an engine problem, and the aircraft entered a dangerous descent before recovery.

Wiener (1989) captured the pilot's experience: "What is it doing? Why is it doing that? What is it going to do next?"

These three questions capture exactly what intent communication should answer. A system that can't answer them — whether the system is an automated aircraft or a human subordinate — leaves the human partner unable to coordinate effectively.

Designing for intent communication in automated systems is hard because the system must signal its state and reasoning at the right moments, at appropriate levels of detail, without creating data overload that drowns the signal. But the alternative — a system that acts without communicating its logic — creates exactly the fragile coordination seen in the flight example.

## The Team Mind: What Teams Can Do That Individuals Cannot

"Team decision making includes properties that we might never predict if we study only individuals — for example, the ability of a team to come up with ideas that are beyond the skills of any single team member."

Klein's research reveals that advanced teams have a qualitatively different cognitive character from collections of individuals. The team mind is not additive. It has properties that emerge from interaction.

### Four Features of Team Development

**1. Team Competencies**: Individual skill levels, shared routines, automaticity in coordination. Without sufficient competency, team members consume all their attention on basic tasks and have nothing left for team-level awareness.

**2. Team Identity**: Understanding of who the team is, what its boundaries are, what it controls and doesn't control. Immature teams ask "just tell me my job." Mature teams ask "what's our overall situation?" Members identify with team outputs, not just individual assignments. They think: "I might need to compensate for others, ask for help, pitch in for team goals even if I abandon my own tasks."

**3. Team Cognition**: Shared situation awareness — do members understand the same situation? Shared time horizon — how far ahead does the team look? Ability to manage uncertainty across distributed knowledge.

**4. Team Metacognition**: The ability to monitor the team's own performance and adjust. Can the team notice it's on the wrong track? Can it change strategy when the current strategy is failing — even when that change is emotionally difficult?

### The Wildland Firefighters: What Excellent Teams Look Like

Klein studied U.S. Forest Service wildland firefighting teams that routinely assembled thousands of firefighters in days, on complex terrain, under extreme conditions. These teams produced remarkably smooth, competent performance under conditions that would paralyze most organizations.

Why they excel — five factors that are individually common but rarely appear together:

1. **Continuous experience**: Fires every season; many cases per year; the adversary (fire behavior) doesn't change its tactics unpredictably
2. **Year-round sharpness**: Core members fight fires in both hemispheres, maintaining practice through the off-season
3. **Stable teams**: Core command teams work together every fire season for decades — contrasting with military 3-5 year rotation cycles that constantly reset relationships
4. **Promotion from within**: Leaders know from experience what it's like to be led; crews trust leader competence
5. **Dense networks**: A closely-knit community; commanders have worked together on large fires before; trust and communication patterns already established

The behavioral signatures: calm in the face of complexity; decisions made quickly with fragmentary information (where to set a firebreak?); command staff meetings that are blunt, efficient, and ego-robust (criticism delivered and received without defensive collapse); sensitivity to morale; disagreement expressed privately unless the stakes are critical.

One detail that Klein finds particularly striking: even during major crises, experienced teams are **simultaneously training successors** — investing in the development of the next generation. This reflects a time horizon and team identity that transcends the immediate situation.

### The Crisis Management Disaster: What Immature Teams Look Like

Klein contrasts the wildland firefighters with crisis management teams he observed in exercises. The contrast is stark.

**The Reaction Time Blindness case**: A crisis manager ordered "double the guards at sector H." Thirty-one minutes later, terrorists invaded sector H.

The crisis manager was shocked: "The order had no effect." But:
- 10 minutes: relay order, brief sector H chief, discuss hostage situation
- 2+ hours minimum: sector chief lines up extra staff, calls people at home, arranges transport
- 5-10 minutes: delay from attack start until anyone can call headquarters

The window available was 31 minutes. The time required was 2.5+ hours. He was trying to control events with a 2:1 disadvantage in response time. The analogy: an infant reaching for a moving ball — reaction time so slow the ball is gone before the hand arrives.

"The team didn't know its own reaction time."

This is a profound form of ignorance. The team was making decisions with a completely miscalibrated understanding of its own capabilities.

**The Caucus Case**: A different crisis manager, burned by previous coordination failures, instituted regular all-hands calls to keep everyone informed. He called for a caucus every 9 minutes (timed by an observer). Just as the security force made phone contact with external units, the leader would yell "Caucus!" — and they'd hang up.

The manager then let the caucus run without agenda, didn't announce when it was over, and berated the security force for not completing calls that his own caucuses had interrupted.

Intended communication actually destroyed coordination. The tool designed to create alignment created fragmentation.

### The Preconscious Failure: Three People, Three Mental Models

A B-727 simulation involved a fuel management problem. Three crew members made a decision together. In the post-flight debrief:
- Copilot (who was flying the plane): "We had two generators"
- Captain: "One and a half" (one good, one on standby)
- Flight Engineer: "One" (standby takes 1-2 minutes to activate; not available for landing)

All three had made the same decision with different mental models of the critical system state. **The team never noticed they disagreed.** The most dangerous answer belonged to the person actually flying the aircraft, and he had low confidence in it — but never surfaced his uncertainty.

This is the team's preconscious in action: what team members know but haven't voiced, ideas in parallel that never surface, thoughts that remain at a preconscious level while the team proceeds on misaligned assumptions.

The fix is explicit verification at critical decision points: "Before we commit to this, let's make sure we're seeing the same situation. How many generators do we have?" Immature teams assume shared understanding. Mature teams verify it.

### The Metacognitive Shift: When Teams Can Change Themselves

During a massive northern California forest fire, a command team was deploying all available resources but making no progress. Fires were not being extinguished.

A metacognitive moment: the team convened and realized they were "fighting fires, not putting fires out." Their current strategy had them responding to active fires, but the fires were overwhelming their capacity to suppress them individually. The solution was to stop fighting every fire, list all fires, prioritize by which ones could be extinguished with available resources, and work through the list systematically.

This required abandoning fires some crews had been protecting. It required telling firefighters their work was being deprioritized. Some friendships were permanently damaged. The decision was emotionally costly.

And it worked. For the first time, fires were actually being extinguished.

This is mature team metacognition: the ability to detect that the current strategy is failing, to hold that detection against the emotional investment in the current approach, and to make the structural change even at social cost.

Immature teams get stuck in failing strategies. They lack the metacognitive clarity to see what they're doing isn't working, or they see it but can't override the momentum of their current approach.

## Intent as Permission to Improvise

The deepest function of communicating intent is captured in the functions table Klein derives from his research:

**Functions intent communication serves:**
- **Promote independence**: Team members can evaluate their own progress without constant guidance
- **Improve performance**: Reduce need for clarifications that interrupt action
- **Detect deviations**: Allow team members to catch errors in advance by understanding what was expected
- **Enable error-catching**: Team members can use their own experience to notice planning errors
- **Promote improvisation**: React to local conditions without waiting for permission
- **Recognize opportunities**: Spot opportunities not part of the original plan
- **Set priorities**: Make trade-off decisions autonomously
- **Continue beyond outcomes**: Proceed to next task without waiting for the next order

When you communicate *why*, subordinates can adjust *how* and *when* without losing alignment. You give them permission to improvise. Without the *why*, improvisation becomes deviation — action without anchor, action that may violate the purpose even while following the letter of the instructions.

The Goeben disaster is the canonical failure of this. The letter of "do not fight superior forces" was honored. The purpose — destroy the Goeben — was abandoned. Troubridge was following orders while betraying intent.

Effective teams internalize the *why* deeply enough that their improvisation serves the purpose even in situations the instructions never anticipated. This is only possible when intent is clearly communicated — and when both parties extend themselves to bridge the gap between what can be specified and what must be understood.

# Nonlinear Problem Solving: Leverage Points, Ill-Defined Goals, and the Structure of Real Problems

## The Failure of Stage Models

Every problem-solving course teaches some version of the four-stage model:
1. Define the problem
2. Generate a course of action
3. Evaluate the course of action
4. Carry out the course of action

Klein argues this model is not merely imperfect — it is **wrong in its structural assumptions** for most real problems. It assumes goals are defined before solutions are generated. It assumes a clean linear progression from problem to solution. It assumes the "problem" is fixed throughout.

Real problems don't work this way.

"Most natural goals seem to be ill defined... Most of the studies of problem solving and decision making have centered on tasks with well-defined goals... Because all the problems have correct answers, there is no ambiguity in these studies."

In the lab: x + 7 = 12; solve for x. Clear goal. Single correct answer. Linear.

In the field: Write a good story. Solve a city's parking problem. Determine whether to attack or search-and-rescue at a burning building where the occupancy is unknown and the structure is failing. Multiple, contested goals. No clear correct answer. Experts disagree on what counts as success.

The stage model advice "define the goal first" traps problem-solvers at step one when goals are genuinely ambiguous — which is to say, most of the time. It mistakes a laboratory convenience for a cognitive law.

## The Nonlinear Problem-Solving Model

Klein's alternative framework identifies four **interactive processes** that occur simultaneously and in any order:

**1. Problem Detection**: An anomaly is noticed. An expectation is violated. An opportunity appears. This doesn't happen once at the start; it recurs throughout problem-solving as new information surfaces.

**2. Problem Representation**: The gap is identified, the opportunity is framed, goals are set, solvability is assessed. This includes optional diagnosis (constructing a causal story about how the current state arose) and optional forecasting (projecting how the situation will evolve). This is the largest activity in most complex problem-solving.

**3. Option Generation**: Either recognized action (knowing what to do because the situation is familiar) or constructed action (building a novel approach from leverage points). Recognition comes first; construction happens when recognition fails.

**4. Evaluation**: Mental or physical simulation of the proposed action; checking for pitfalls; deciding to execute, modify, or abandon. Evaluation can reveal new goals or barriers, which feeds back to problem representation. It can discover opportunities that didn't appear in the original framing.

These processes don't run in sequence. They run **in parallel, constrained by each other**. Evaluation changes the problem representation. Problem representation determines what options are generated. Option generation surfaces new aspects of the problem that weren't initially visible. The system is genuinely nonlinear.

## Leverage Points: What Makes Small Things Powerful

"A leverage point is a small difference that makes a large difference, a small change that can turn a situation around."

Leverage points are not context-free. What counts as a leverage point depends on the problem-solver's capabilities, the situation's constraints, and the goals being pursued. Rock climbing holds illustrate this:

A quarter-inch ridge might be a viable handhold on easy terrain for a rested, strong climber on dry rock. The same quarter-inch ridge is not a hold for a tired climber in rain on vertical faces above weak rock. A crack too narrow for a full hand might accommodate a bent finger — making it a hold for someone who knows how to use it.

Holds are not properties of rocks; they're **perception-action pairs**. The same physical feature is an affordance for one climber and not for another.

Leverage points work the same way. The "impossible crossing" on a military map was marked impossible because the standard test (full platoon crossing) failed. An Israeli commander studying that map noted: "Here, where it is marked 'Impossible.' That's where we cross." The test's failure didn't rule out crossing one or two tanks at a time. His deeper understanding of how the official records were compiled — what assumptions they baked in — let him see a viable path where the map showed a wall.

"Experts know how the official records are compiled... They know when the steps have to be followed and when to make exceptions."

## The Physician and the Bubbles: Constructive Problem Solving

A physician needed to intubate a neonate with a massive cystic hygroma blocking the trachea. The standard approach (tracheostomy on a quarter-inch-wide infant trachea) posed high infection risk and was technically extremely difficult.

The physician recalled a case he'd heard about involving a barbed-wire injury: an emergency tech had "inserted a tube where he saw bubbles." The analogy was far-fetched — adult accident victim versus neonate, emergency tech versus physician, trauma versus birth. But the physician extracted the *essence*: bubbles indicate where air is moving; air movement indicates airway access.

The leverage point: bubbles. But in the infant's case, there were no spontaneous bubbles. The physician's innovation: compress the infant's chest to force air out through any gap, locate bubbles emerging between the cysts, thread a laryngoscope through that gap.

The neonate turned pink. The procedure worked.

This is **constructive problem solving** — not retrieval of a solution from memory, but creative recombination of elements from a far-fetched analogy. The physician wasn't following a protocol. He was using an insight from one domain (bubbles = airway) and adapting it to fundamentally different constraints (force the air out rather than wait for natural breathing).

Several things make this case instructive:

First, the analogy wasn't a full-scenario match. The physician didn't think "this case reminds me of that entire barbed-wire situation." He retrieved a **fragment** — a specific feature (bubbles marking the airway) — and built from there.

Second, the adaptation required understanding the mechanism, not just the surface procedure. He knew *why* bubbles indicated airway access. That mechanistic understanding is what made it possible to adapt: if you don't have natural bubbles, create them.

Third, the leverage point had to be *constructed* before it could be used. The physician had to do something (compress the chest) to reveal the leverage point (bubbles) before he could exploit it.

## The Philadelphia Landing: Vulnerability Detection as Expertise

An airline crew is preparing to land in Philadelphia. A pilot traveling as a jumpseat observer notices several risk factors accumulating:

1. Worn brakes and tires (almost at replacement limit)
2. Extra 9,000 pounds of fuel aboard (reason unknown)
3. Thrust reverser light flickering (may not slow aircraft on landing)
4. Deteriorating weather in Philadelphia (moderate winds, rain)
5. Air traffic control assigned the **shortest available runway**

The observer — experienced, attentive — sees each factor and synthesizes them into an assessment of composite risk. The stopping distance is a function of: brake condition, tire condition, aircraft weight, thrust reverser function, runway length, and surface conditions. Each factor reduces the margin. Together, they create a potentially catastrophic situation.

He asks about the runway selection. The question prompts the captain to request a longer runway. The risk is avoided.

Two people had the same information. The observer saw the **choke point** — the leverage point working *against* you. The captain appeared to process each factor in isolation, without integrating the composite picture.

This is the dark side of leverage point recognition: expert perception of **vulnerabilities** before they become crises. The observer wasn't doing anything magical. He was running a mental model of stopping distance with multiple degraded inputs and noticing that the margins were collapsing.

The key capability: **small margins compound**. When brake condition is marginal AND tire condition is marginal AND weight is high AND thrust reverser is uncertain AND the runway is short AND the surface is wet — no single factor is alarming, but the combination is potentially fatal. Novices process each factor separately. Experts integrate across them.

This is also preventive rather than reactive expertise. The observer didn't wait for the landing to go wrong. He detected the vulnerability at the point where it could still be addressed — before commitment to the approach, before the runway assignment became fixed.

## The Apollo 13 Analysis: Where Problem-Solving Effort Actually Goes

Klein analyzed 73 problem-solving and decision-making episodes from the Apollo 13 crisis — the oxygen tank explosion and subsequent return to Earth.

The distribution of effort tells a striking story:

| Process | Episodes | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Problem Detection | 20 | Usually obvious (alarms); hardest case was recognizing LEM could be lifeboat |
| Problem Representation | 31 | **Largest activity** — forming situation awareness |
| Option Generation | 18 | Constructing novel courses of action |
| Diagnosis | 10 | Critical when present (e.g., battery malfunction was pre-existing) |
| Forecasting | 15 | Predicting consumable depletion, trajectory deviation |
| Goal Revision | 5 | Mission to rescue; shift from exploration priority to survival priority |
| Formal Option Comparison | 4 | Only four true comparisons among 73 episodes |

"Most of the problem solving did not require either diagnoses or the generation of a novel course of action. Forming an accurate problem representation was the most common activity for this incident."

This inverts conventional wisdom. Problem-solving training focuses on generating options and evaluating them. But the largest cognitive demand in the Apollo 13 crisis was **understanding what was happening** — building accurate problem representations. The heroic innovation (using the LEM as a lifeboat, finding ways to reduce power consumption, calculating reentry trajectory without Earth's horizon) required accurate problem representation before anything else was possible.

The formal option comparisons that did occur are instructive for their rarity and character:

1. **Shut reactant valves** (stop O₂ loss but lose lunar mission): Necessary, essentially no real choice
2. **Direct vs. indirect abort**: Direct was infeasible (engine damage); indirect was obvious
3. **Sleep before power-down**: Mental simulation of risk (losing a crew member during sleep from CO₂) vs. fatigue errors; chose power-down first
4. **Burn strategy** (36-hour fast vs. 20+-hour slow): Compared worst cases — consumables tight but manageable vs. jettisoning service module with unknown heat shield risk. Chose slow burn, keeping service module.

None of these involved scoring options against criteria. The fourth involved comparing worst-case scenarios via mental simulation — which risk was more catastrophic if the assumption was wrong?

The leverage points that enabled survival:
- **Moon as alignment marker**: Can't see Earth's horizon on the night side for reentry angle; but the moon's edge is visible against Earth's limb; calculate when moon disappears = confirm entry angle
- **LEM as lifeboat**: The pivot that reframed the entire problem from "repair the command module" to "use the LEM for life support"
- **Excess LEM capacity**: The difference between current reentry power requirements and available LEM power defined a solvable subproblem

## The Telemarketing Company: How Goals Evolve During Problem-Solving

A parent company with franchisees was exploring centralizing telemarketing to reduce the burden on individual franchisees. The president was lukewarm — the investment seemed large relative to the operational benefit.

As the team simulated the telemarketing center operations — thinking through how it would actually work, who would staff it, how scripts would work, how scheduling would operate — the president noticed something: the same infrastructure that would serve franchisees could *also* be used for direct catalog sales to the public.

The goal shifted. Not "solve the franchisee burden" but "launch a new revenue stream that also solves the franchisee burden."

The evaluation of one course of action revealed a higher-value opportunity that hadn't been visible at the start. This is the nonlinear property that the stage model can't accommodate: evaluation produces goals, not just verdicts on options.

Klein calls this **goal elevation** — discovering during problem-solving that a bigger or better goal is available. It's not possible to instruct problem-solvers to "define goals before generating options" and then permit goal elevation. The instruction presupposes a linear process that blocks the behavior it's supposedly guiding.

## The AI Problem: Why Search Is Not Problem-Solving

Artificial intelligence approaches to problem-solving treat the problem space as containing all possible states, and solution as search through that space using heuristics (means-ends analysis, subgoal decomposition).

Klein identifies four failures of this framework for real problem-solving:

**Problem space doesn't exist**: Humans don't generate full problem spaces except for artificial combinatorial puzzles. Complex problems defy enumeration — too many variables, too many possible states, too many interactions.

**Search misses noticing**: The AI model assumes you move through a defined space. Real problem-solving involves *noticing* things — recognizing opportunities, spotting leverage points — that weren't part of the original representation.

**Means-ends analysis misses opportunities**: The strength of means-ends analysis is finding the remaining difference and reducing it. But this means you only look for obstacles to remove, never for resources to exploit. Leverage points are resources, not obstacle-removers.

**Goals radically shift**: The means-ends model handles goal reformulation as "remove unnecessary constraints." But the telemarketing case shows goal *elevation* — a completely different goal becoming available during problem-solving, not just a less constrained version of the original.

"Computational approaches try to reduce thinking to searching. Instead of searching through a problem space, we notice opportunities that let us make progress, even if those opportunities do not correspond to the obstacles we are trying to eliminate."

The implication for any intelligent system: problem-solving cannot be reduced to search. It requires the ability to *notice* — to detect affordances, leverage points, and opportunities that weren't part of the initial problem representation. This is a form of perceptual skill, built from experience with similar domains, not a form of computational search.

## Planning as Problem-Solving: What Plans Are For

Klein and colleagues studied planning teams in military settings (Roving Sands, USS Kittyhawk, USS Mt. Whitney) and found that the standard conception of planning — as a process for generating instructions to execute — is incomplete.

Plans serve multiple functions simultaneously:

- **Coordinating team actions**: Making shared commitments explicit
- **Developing shared situation awareness**: The planning process itself builds understanding
- **Generating expectancies**: What we expect to happen when we execute
- **Supporting improvisation**: A baseline from which to deviate intelligently
- **Detecting inconsistencies**: Incompatibilities surface during planning that would otherwise surface during execution (at higher cost)
- **Establishing time horizons**: Forcing thinking ahead
- **Shaping thought and promoting learning**: Perhaps the most important — planning teaches people about the domain

This last function is crucial and counterintuitive: "Sometimes planners engage in lengthy, detailed preparations that quickly become obsolete, yet they continue with the same process, again and again; it appears that the function is to help them all learn more about the situation and to calibrate their understanding."

The plan is not the output. The *planning process* is the output. The plan is a temporary artifact that captures the current best understanding and enables coordination — but its value comes primarily from the learning and alignment it produces, not from the instructions it contains.

This reframes evaluation of planning: a plan that becomes obsolete quickly is not necessarily a failure. A planning process that produces deep shared understanding and enables intelligent improvisation — even if the specific plan becomes obsolete the moment conditions change — is a success.

The environmental forcing function shapes what kind of plans work:
- **Stable environments**: Precise, complex, integrated plans succeed
- **Dynamic environments**: Modular plans enable rapid improvisation without cascading failures when one element changes
- **Time pressure and uncertainty**: Harder to build integrated plans; modular plans are safer defaults

# Analogical Reasoning and Stories: The Infrastructure of Expert Cognition

## Stories as Causal Packages

"We similarly organize the cognitive world — the world of ideas, concepts, objects, and relationships. We link these up into stories."

Klein's claim is not merely that stories are useful for communication. Stories are the **natural medium for causal reasoning**. They encode cause-and-effect in a form that preserves interaction effects, context-dependency, and multi-factor relationships that formal rules and procedures cannot capture.

"A good story is a package of different causal relationships — what factors resulted in what effects."

The contrast with procedures is exact:
- A procedure says: "When condition A, do action B."
- A story says: "Under these specific conditions, with these background factors operating simultaneously, this cause produced this effect — and here's what we noticed along the way."

Stories preserve what experiments often strip out: the *interaction effects*, the context-dependencies, the unexpected complications, the partial solutions that led somewhere unexpected. They record that under *these conditions* (not just any conditions) *these causes* (working simultaneously, not independently) produced *this result*.

"In its way, a story is also a report of an experiment, linking cause and effect. It says, 'Under these conditions, this is what happens.'"

## The Pneumopericardium: One Story, Many Lessons

Klein gives a story that functions as a teaching device for multiple simultaneous lessons:

A nurse observing a neighboring baby noticed subtle color changes over several hours. She mentioned this to the baby's primary nurse. The baby then suddenly turned deep blue-black; heart rate appeared to hold at 80 beats per minute.

The first nurse knew immediately: pneumopericardium — an air sac around the heart, paralyzing it. The primary nurse shouted for an X-ray, assuming a collapsed lung, pointing to the monitor showing what appeared to be a steady heartbeat.

The first nurse contradicted: "It's the heart; there's no heartbeat." She listened with a stethoscope and began compressions. She told the arriving chief neonatologist: "It's a pneumopericardium. I know it. Stick the heart." He did. The air was released. The baby survived.

The post-event understanding: the monitor records electrical impulses, not mechanical pumping. A heart can have electrical activity (showing on the monitor) while being mechanically paralyzed (no circulation). The monitor was showing a false signal.

What lessons are embedded in this story?

1. **Equipment blindness**: Machines can display misleading data; know their failure modes
2. **Intervention risk**: Life support equipment can cause the very conditions it's meant to prevent (ventilators causing pneumopericardium by forcing air into the pleural space)
3. **Expert perception**: The first nurse had seen pneumopericardium before (in a fatal case); she recognized the pattern
4. **Permission narrative**: When to break protocol, push back against authority, make a fuss — and what culture will permit this
5. **Data interpretation**: The most obvious reading of a monitor (steady signal = steady heartbeat) can be catastrophically wrong

A procedure could encode some of this: "If pneumopericardium is suspected, check for discrepancy between monitor and stethoscope." But the procedure strips out: what does pneumopericardium *look like* before it's obvious? How does a nurse know when to push back against a more senior clinician? What does the monitor *feel like* as a trusted instrument before you know it can lie?

The story carries all of this simultaneously, in a form that's memorable, transferable, and able to be extracted differently by different listeners based on their own experience and concerns.

## What Makes Stories Work

Klein identifies the features of stories that make them effective for learning and knowledge transfer:

- **Drama**: Unexpected things that compel attention; violation of expectancy creates engagement
- **Empathy**: We identify with agents, so we absorb their perspective along with their experience
- **Wisdom**: Lessons that emerge with repeated telling, deepening on each pass
- **Plausibility**: Elements that are believable; anomalies that get explained
- **Consistency**: Elements that fit together; no unexplained contradictions
- **Economy**: Complete without excessive detail; what's included is there for a reason
- **Uniqueness**: Not open to multiple contradictory interpretations
- **Ending**: Resolution of main themes that signals what the story was about

These features explain why stories work better than abstractions for knowledge transfer. A rule ("check that the heart monitor matches stethoscope readings") has none of these properties. A story about a baby whose heart had stopped while the monitor showed activity has all of them.

The drama is the near-death and the confident nurse overriding a superior. The empathy is with both nurses — one certain, one confused; the resolution vindicates the experienced over the procedural. The wisdom deepens: the more you know about neonatal medicine, the more layers of the story you can access.

## Troubleshooting as Successive Story-Building

Klein's analysis of expert troubleshooters reveals that they work by building and refining stories — not by systematically testing components.

A classic case: a fuse keeps blowing in summer but not winter. Electricians find nothing wrong on inspection.

A master troubleshooter asks how the room is used. Summer: walkway. Winter: storage.

He has the homeowner rearrange to the summer configuration, then gets on his hands and knees to examine the worn carpet pathway. He finds a protruding nail puncturing the electrical wire.

The explanation: in summer, people walking the path step on the nail repeatedly, creating intermittent shorts. In winter, no traffic, no shorts.

Strategy: find the key change between two conditions; use that to direct search.

This is **successive story-building**: an initial story (something electrical changes seasonally) directs investigation (what changes in the room seasonally?). The investigation finds a key difference (traffic pattern). A new story forms (nail causes intermittent shorts when stepped on). The story is testable and confirmed.

Klein's AT&T study of software troubleshooters found the same pattern. Best troubleshooters built successive stories, each directing them to check specific components. When a story failed, they gathered new information and revised the story. The process was simultaneous knowledge-building and information-gathering — each influencing the other.

This matches Klein's nonlinear problem-solving model: problem representation and option generation are interleaved, each informing the other, with story-building as the mechanism that integrates them.

## The Critical Decision Method: Extracting Stories

Because experts cannot articulate their knowledge directly ("experience and intuition" is the typical answer to "how do you know?"), Klein and Beth Crandall developed the **Critical Decision Method** — a structured approach to extracting expertise through stories.

The approach rests on a simple observation: while experts can't answer "what are your rules?", they *can* tell stories about specific difficult cases. And those stories, carefully analyzed, reveal the perceptual patterns and reasoning that constitute expertise.

**Four-Pass Interview Structure:**

**Pass 1 — Brief version**: Get the story quickly. Does this case have good potential for analysis? Which parts warrant deeper probing?

**Pass 2 — Full timeline**: Pin down the sequence. Diagram knowledge transformations. Note what the expert knew at each stage and when they learned it. Catch inconsistencies in the timeline — they often point to tacit knowledge the expert is skipping over.

**Pass 3 — Thought process probes**:
- What triggered the assessment change?
- What alternative goals were available at this point?
- What other options could you have chosen?
- Hypotheticals: "If information X hadn't arrived, what would you have done?"
- Cohen's probe: "Imagine that didn't happen. Why not? What could explain such a thing?" — this surfaces hidden assumptions that explain why the expert discounted certain possibilities

**Pass 4 — Novice vulnerability analysis**: At each choice point, could a novice get confused? What mistakes might they make? Why? This directly elicits the expert's understanding of the knowledge gap — what *they* see that novices don't.

The NICU nurses example: asked how they detected sepsis, they said "experience and intuition." Crandall had them tell stories of specific detections. Each story tied to different cues. Across stories, a master list of cues emerged — nearly half of which were not in the medical literature, some of which were opposite to adult sepsis patterns (adults become more irritable; premature babies become *less* irritable, losing their normal responsiveness). A baby that always cries when weighed suddenly not crying? Danger sign.

The knowledge was real, specific, and teachable. It just couldn't be accessed by asking "what do you know?" It was accessible only through stories.

## Analogical Reasoning: Using Prior Cases to Navigate Novel Problems

Stories become analogues when they're applied to new situations. The power of analogical reasoning comes from a profound property of prior cases:

"If we can find an analogy with which we feel comfortable, we can use it because it reflects the full set of causal factors, even the ones we cannot yet identify. The analogy also reflects the interactions between causal factors, interactions we cannot specify, so it lets us make a prediction that reflects factors whose existence we do not know and whose properties we do not know. That is the power of analogical reasoning."

This is the key insight: the analogue carries hidden causal factors and their interactions. You don't need to know what they are to benefit from them. The prior case acts as a proxy experiment — if the conditions are similar enough, the outcome will be similar even for reasons you can't articulate.

"There is a delicate balance to using analogues. If we know a great amount, we do not need the analogues; we can just figure out the formulas. If we know very little, analogical reasoning may be as likely to get us into trouble as to help us. Analogical reasoning seems to help most when we are in between: we know something about the area but not enough for a satisfactory analysis."

This calibration is crucial and often missed. Analogical reasoning is most valuable in the middle zone of knowledge: when you understand enough to select a relevant analogue and adjust for known differences, but not enough to derive the answer from first principles.

## The Mechanics of Analogical Reasoning: B-1 Aircraft Examples

Klein illustrates analogical reasoning through an engineer's task: estimate the reliability of components for the B-1 bomber before it's built.

**Example 12.1 — Scaling (Simple)**:
B-1 duct system compared to the FB-111 predecessor. The analogue is straightforward; the adjustment is proportional: scale upward by the size difference. Same causal factors operating at different scale.

**Example 12.2 — Causal Adjustment (Complex)**:
B-1 hydraulic system (4,000 psi) compared to B-52 (3,000 psi). The identified causal factor: higher pressure → increased wear → lower reliability. The adjustment: reduce the B-52 reliability data by one-third.

This is transparent reasoning that other experts can check. Someone might disagree (new materials might compensate for the pressure increase) — but they can see the logic and adjust it. The method is communicable.

**Example 12.3 — Rejecting an Identical Analogue (Most Instructive)**:
The C-5A cargo aircraft uses an auxiliary power unit that is physically nearly identical to the B-1's. A naive approach would use C-5A reliability data directly.

The engineer rejected it entirely.

Reason: the operational contexts are completely different. Bombers scramble — rapid startup, all systems activated simultaneously, sharp maneuvers to avoid enemy action. Cargo aircraft operate smoothly and gradually. The *demand pattern* on the APU is fundamentally different, in ways that would invalidate any adjustment. No proportional factor could bridge this gap.

"Engineer's judgment: reject the analogue entirely, despite physical identity."

Physical similarity is irrelevant. Causal dynamics are everything. This requires domain knowledge — understanding *why* components fail, not just *that* they fail.

**Example 12.5 — The Wrong Analogue (Failure Case)**:
When designing the JSTARS aircraft (surveillance platform, flies low and slow near battle lines), designers chose the AWACS as the analogical model for self-defense requirements. Both are slow, large aircraft.

What they missed:
- AWACS has dedicated fighter escort; JSTARS does not
- AWACS crew monitors air picture; JSTARS crew monitors ground (no air defense capacity)
- AWACS is a secure platform; JSTARS is "a giant kick-me sign" — slow, predictable, emitting radar signals
- AWACS operates far from the threat; JSTARS operates near it

The designers underestimated self-defense requirements. The error was corrected only during Desert Storm by ensuring no enemy fighters were nearby and providing fighter escort.

The analogue was selected on surface similarity (big slow aircraft). The critical causal factors that differentiated the cases (escort arrangements, crew air-defense capacity, distance from threat) were overlooked. This is the exact failure mode analogical reasoning is designed to prevent — and it happened anyway because the analogue selection wasn't subjected to causal scrutiny.

## Movie Theater Attendance: Validating Analogical Reasoning

Klein conducted a striking empirical test of analogical reasoning with the Little Art theater in Yellow Springs, Ohio.

The task: predict attendance for 35 future movies from a database of 10 years of historical data (coded by genre, nationality, critical reception, etc.).

Results:
- 17 random citizens: correlation +.17 (barely above chance)
- Jennie (theater manager, no database): correlation +.31
- Klein and colleague (amateurs with the database): correlation +.45

The key finding: Klein's team outperformed the expert theater manager despite having less domain knowledge. The database provided candidate analogues; Klein and his colleague applied analogical reasoning with hard historical data.

"Success came from the database, not from expertise."

The method was: find the most similar prior movie (matching on genre, origin, critical tone, etc.), use its attendance as the baseline prediction, adjust for known differences. Simple analogical reasoning grounded in empirical cases outperformed intuition.

But note the limit: if the database were fabricated or estimated rather than based on hard historical data, accuracy dropped catastrophically (correlation fell from ~.45 to +.36 in tests with invented baseline data). "The method requires grounding in empirical cases." The
