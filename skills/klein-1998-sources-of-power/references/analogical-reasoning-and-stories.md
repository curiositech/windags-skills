# Analogical Reasoning and Stories: The Infrastructure of Expert Cognition

## Stories as Causal Packages

"We similarly organize the cognitive world — the world of ideas, concepts, objects, and relationships. We link these up into stories."

Klein's claim is not merely that stories are useful for communication. Stories are the **natural medium for causal reasoning**. They encode cause-and-effect in a form that preserves interaction effects, context-dependency, and multi-factor relationships that formal rules and procedures cannot capture.

"A good story is a package of different causal relationships — what factors resulted in what effects."

The contrast with procedures is exact:
- A procedure says: "When condition A, do action B."
- A story says: "Under these specific conditions, with these background factors operating simultaneously, this cause produced this effect — and here's what we noticed along the way."

Stories preserve what experiments often strip out: the *interaction effects*, the context-dependencies, the unexpected complications, the partial solutions that led somewhere unexpected. They record that under *these conditions* (not just any conditions) *these causes* (working simultaneously, not independently) produced *this result*.

"In its way, a story is also a report of an experiment, linking cause and effect. It says, 'Under these conditions, this is what happens.'"

## The Pneumopericardium: One Story, Many Lessons

Klein gives a story that functions as a teaching device for multiple simultaneous lessons:

A nurse observing a neighboring baby noticed subtle color changes over several hours. She mentioned this to the baby's primary nurse. The baby then suddenly turned deep blue-black; heart rate appeared to hold at 80 beats per minute.

The first nurse knew immediately: pneumopericardium — an air sac around the heart, paralyzing it. The primary nurse shouted for an X-ray, assuming a collapsed lung, pointing to the monitor showing what appeared to be a steady heartbeat.

The first nurse contradicted: "It's the heart; there's no heartbeat." She listened with a stethoscope and began compressions. She told the arriving chief neonatologist: "It's a pneumopericardium. I know it. Stick the heart." He did. The air was released. The baby survived.

The post-event understanding: the monitor records electrical impulses, not mechanical pumping. A heart can have electrical activity (showing on the monitor) while being mechanically paralyzed (no circulation). The monitor was showing a false signal.

What lessons are embedded in this story?

1. **Equipment blindness**: Machines can display misleading data; know their failure modes
2. **Intervention risk**: Life support equipment can cause the very conditions it's meant to prevent (ventilators causing pneumopericardium by forcing air into the pleural space)
3. **Expert perception**: The first nurse had seen pneumopericardium before (in a fatal case); she recognized the pattern
4. **Permission narrative**: When to break protocol, push back against authority, make a fuss — and what culture will permit this
5. **Data interpretation**: The most obvious reading of a monitor (steady signal = steady heartbeat) can be catastrophically wrong

A procedure could encode some of this: "If pneumopericardium is suspected, check for discrepancy between monitor and stethoscope." But the procedure strips out: what does pneumopericardium *look like* before it's obvious? How does a nurse know when to push back against a more senior clinician? What does the monitor *feel like* as a trusted instrument before you know it can lie?

The story carries all of this simultaneously, in a form that's memorable, transferable, and able to be extracted differently by different listeners based on their own experience and concerns.

## What Makes Stories Work

Klein identifies the features of stories that make them effective for learning and knowledge transfer:

- **Drama**: Unexpected things that compel attention; violation of expectancy creates engagement
- **Empathy**: We identify with agents, so we absorb their perspective along with their experience
- **Wisdom**: Lessons that emerge with repeated telling, deepening on each pass
- **Plausibility**: Elements that are believable; anomalies that get explained
- **Consistency**: Elements that fit together; no unexplained contradictions
- **Economy**: Complete without excessive detail; what's included is there for a reason
- **Uniqueness**: Not open to multiple contradictory interpretations
- **Ending**: Resolution of main themes that signals what the story was about

These features explain why stories work better than abstractions for knowledge transfer. A rule ("check that the heart monitor matches stethoscope readings") has none of these properties. A story about a baby whose heart had stopped while the monitor showed activity has all of them.

The drama is the near-death and the confident nurse overriding a superior. The empathy is with both nurses — one certain, one confused; the resolution vindicates the experienced over the procedural. The wisdom deepens: the more you know about neonatal medicine, the more layers of the story you can access.

## Troubleshooting as Successive Story-Building

Klein's analysis of expert troubleshooters reveals that they work by building and refining stories — not by systematically testing components.

A classic case: a fuse keeps blowing in summer but not winter. Electricians find nothing wrong on inspection.

A master troubleshooter asks how the room is used. Summer: walkway. Winter: storage.

He has the homeowner rearrange to the summer configuration, then gets on his hands and knees to examine the worn carpet pathway. He finds a protruding nail puncturing the electrical wire.

The explanation: in summer, people walking the path step on the nail repeatedly, creating intermittent shorts. In winter, no traffic, no shorts.

Strategy: find the key change between two conditions; use that to direct search.

This is **successive story-building**: an initial story (something electrical changes seasonally) directs investigation (what changes in the room seasonally?). The investigation finds a key difference (traffic pattern). A new story forms (nail causes intermittent shorts when stepped on). The story is testable and confirmed.

Klein's AT&T study of software troubleshooters found the same pattern. Best troubleshooters built successive stories, each directing them to check specific components. When a story failed, they gathered new information and revised the story. The process was simultaneous knowledge-building and information-gathering — each influencing the other.

This matches Klein's nonlinear problem-solving model: problem representation and option generation are interleaved, each informing the other, with story-building as the mechanism that integrates them.

## The Critical Decision Method: Extracting Stories

Because experts cannot articulate their knowledge directly ("experience and intuition" is the typical answer to "how do you know?"), Klein and Beth Crandall developed the **Critical Decision Method** — a structured approach to extracting expertise through stories.

The approach rests on a simple observation: while experts can't answer "what are your rules?", they *can* tell stories about specific difficult cases. And those stories, carefully analyzed, reveal the perceptual patterns and reasoning that constitute expertise.

**Four-Pass Interview Structure:**

**Pass 1 — Brief version**: Get the story quickly. Does this case have good potential for analysis? Which parts warrant deeper probing?

**Pass 2 — Full timeline**: Pin down the sequence. Diagram knowledge transformations. Note what the expert knew at each stage and when they learned it. Catch inconsistencies in the timeline — they often point to tacit knowledge the expert is skipping over.

**Pass 3 — Thought process probes**:
- What triggered the assessment change?
- What alternative goals were available at this point?
- What other options could you have chosen?
- Hypotheticals: "If information X hadn't arrived, what would you have done?"
- Cohen's probe: "Imagine that didn't happen. Why not? What could explain such a thing?" — this surfaces hidden assumptions that explain why the expert discounted certain possibilities

**Pass 4 — Novice vulnerability analysis**: At each choice point, could a novice get confused? What mistakes might they make? Why? This directly elicits the expert's understanding of the knowledge gap — what *they* see that novices don't.

The NICU nurses example: asked how they detected sepsis, they said "experience and intuition." Crandall had them tell stories of specific detections. Each story tied to different cues. Across stories, a master list of cues emerged — nearly half of which were not in the medical literature, some of which were opposite to adult sepsis patterns (adults become more irritable; premature babies become *less* irritable, losing their normal responsiveness). A baby that always cries when weighed suddenly not crying? Danger sign.

The knowledge was real, specific, and teachable. It just couldn't be accessed by asking "what do you know?" It was accessible only through stories.

## Analogical Reasoning: Using Prior Cases to Navigate Novel Problems

Stories become analogues when they're applied to new situations. The power of analogical reasoning comes from a profound property of prior cases:

"If we can find an analogy with which we feel comfortable, we can use it because it reflects the full set of causal factors, even the ones we cannot yet identify. The analogy also reflects the interactions between causal factors, interactions we cannot specify, so it lets us make a prediction that reflects factors whose existence we do not know and whose properties we do not know. That is the power of analogical reasoning."

This is the key insight: the analogue carries hidden causal factors and their interactions. You don't need to know what they are to benefit from them. The prior case acts as a proxy experiment — if the conditions are similar enough, the outcome will be similar even for reasons you can't articulate.

"There is a delicate balance to using analogues. If we know a great amount, we do not need the analogues; we can just figure out the formulas. If we know very little, analogical reasoning may be as likely to get us into trouble as to help us. Analogical reasoning seems to help most when we are in between: we know something about the area but not enough for a satisfactory analysis."

This calibration is crucial and often missed. Analogical reasoning is most valuable in the middle zone of knowledge: when you understand enough to select a relevant analogue and adjust for known differences, but not enough to derive the answer from first principles.

## The Mechanics of Analogical Reasoning: B-1 Aircraft Examples

Klein illustrates analogical reasoning through an engineer's task: estimate the reliability of components for the B-1 bomber before it's built.

**Example 12.1 — Scaling (Simple)**:
B-1 duct system compared to the FB-111 predecessor. The analogue is straightforward; the adjustment is proportional: scale upward by the size difference. Same causal factors operating at different scale.

**Example 12.2 — Causal Adjustment (Complex)**:
B-1 hydraulic system (4,000 psi) compared to B-52 (3,000 psi). The identified causal factor: higher pressure → increased wear → lower reliability. The adjustment: reduce the B-52 reliability data by one-third.

This is transparent reasoning that other experts can check. Someone might disagree (new materials might compensate for the pressure increase) — but they can see the logic and adjust it. The method is communicable.

**Example 12.3 — Rejecting an Identical Analogue (Most Instructive)**:
The C-5A cargo aircraft uses an auxiliary power unit that is physically nearly identical to the B-1's. A naive approach would use C-5A reliability data directly.

The engineer rejected it entirely.

Reason: the operational contexts are completely different. Bombers scramble — rapid startup, all systems activated simultaneously, sharp maneuvers to avoid enemy action. Cargo aircraft operate smoothly and gradually. The *demand pattern* on the APU is fundamentally different, in ways that would invalidate any adjustment. No proportional factor could bridge this gap.

"Engineer's judgment: reject the analogue entirely, despite physical identity."

Physical similarity is irrelevant. Causal dynamics are everything. This requires domain knowledge — understanding *why* components fail, not just *that* they fail.

**Example 12.5 — The Wrong Analogue (Failure Case)**:
When designing the JSTARS aircraft (surveillance platform, flies low and slow near battle lines), designers chose the AWACS as the analogical model for self-defense requirements. Both are slow, large aircraft.

What they missed:
- AWACS has dedicated fighter escort; JSTARS does not
- AWACS crew monitors air picture; JSTARS crew monitors ground (no air defense capacity)
- AWACS is a secure platform; JSTARS is "a giant kick-me sign" — slow, predictable, emitting radar signals
- AWACS operates far from the threat; JSTARS operates near it

The designers underestimated self-defense requirements. The error was corrected only during Desert Storm by ensuring no enemy fighters were nearby and providing fighter escort.

The analogue was selected on surface similarity (big slow aircraft). The critical causal factors that differentiated the cases (escort arrangements, crew air-defense capacity, distance from threat) were overlooked. This is the exact failure mode analogical reasoning is designed to prevent — and it happened anyway because the analogue selection wasn't subjected to causal scrutiny.

## Movie Theater Attendance: Validating Analogical Reasoning

Klein conducted a striking empirical test of analogical reasoning with the Little Art theater in Yellow Springs, Ohio.

The task: predict attendance for 35 future movies from a database of 10 years of historical data (coded by genre, nationality, critical reception, etc.).

Results:
- 17 random citizens: correlation +.17 (barely above chance)
- Jennie (theater manager, no database): correlation +.31
- Klein and colleague (amateurs with the database): correlation +.45

The key finding: Klein's team outperformed the expert theater manager despite having less domain knowledge. The database provided candidate analogues; Klein and his colleague applied analogical reasoning with hard historical data.

"Success came from the database, not from expertise."

The method was: find the most similar prior movie (matching on genre, origin, critical tone, etc.), use its attendance as the baseline prediction, adjust for known differences. Simple analogical reasoning grounded in empirical cases outperformed intuition.
