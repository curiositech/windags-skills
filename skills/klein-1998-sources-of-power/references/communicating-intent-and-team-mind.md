# Communicating Intent and the Team Mind: Distributed Cognition Under Pressure

## The Fundamental Challenge of Coordination

When Klein asks a veteran assistant (Barbara Law) to edit a manuscript, she does more than correct prose. She finds number discrepancies with a prior study, realizes the sponsor (Jim Banks) recently saw that prior study, infers Klein wouldn't want Banks to discover mismatches, and holds up the draft until the discrepancies are resolved — without being asked.

She accomplished this by knowing Klein's priorities, past concerns, and long-term goals well enough to simulate what he would want in a situation he hadn't anticipated. She could "read his mind."

This is the ideal of coordination: subordinates who understand intent well enough to make good decisions in situations the principal couldn't foresee. It requires two parties to extend themselves: the person delegating must communicate *why*, not just *what*; the person receiving must imagine what the other person really wants when situations don't fit the instructions.

The alternative — complete specification of all relevant details in advance — is impossible. "Usually we cannot specify all these details in advance. If you are doing a favor for me, I have to rely on your ability to read my mind and imagine how I would have made all the choices. I do not believe I could anticipate all the relevant details to tell you."

## The Seven Facets of Intent

Klein's research identified seven components of effective intent communication. Together they create a picture complete enough that subordinates can operate independently and improvise appropriately:

1. **Purpose** (higher-level goals) — the big picture of why this matters
2. **Objective** (desired outcome image) — what success looks like concretely
3. **Sequence of steps** (the plan) — how to proceed under normal conditions
4. **Rationale** (reasoning behind the plan) — why this plan and not another
5. **Key decisions** (contingencies and priorities) — what to watch for and how to respond
6. **Antigoals** (unwanted outcomes) — what NOT to do, which situations to avoid
7. **Constraints and considerations** — relevant context the subordinate may not have

Karl Weick's distillation of effective intent communication: "Here's what I think we face. Here's what I think we should do. Here's why. Here's what we should keep our eye on. Now, talk to me."

This is not a long document. It's a brief statement of situation, intention, reasoning, and critical uncertainties — followed by an invitation to respond. The art is giving *enough* to enable autonomy without so much that the main points get buried.

"The more details you pack in, the more you obscure your main points. However, if you leave out an important consideration, you run the risk that the person will become confused at a critical decision point."

## The Goeben Disaster: When Intent Fails

At the start of World War I, the German battleship Goeben was in the Mediterranean. Churchill ordered British naval forces: "Hunt down the Goeben but do not at this stage be brought to action against superior forces."

Churchill's intent: destroy the Goeben. The warning about superior forces was directed at the Austrian battle fleet.

Admiral Troubridge's interpretation: The Goeben is larger than any of my ships. It could be considered a "superior force." To avoid engaging without safety guarantees, he should regroup.

The Goeben escaped through the Dardanelles. The Ottoman Empire entered the war on the German side. Russian shipping was bottled up. This contributed to the Russian Revolution. The British Mandate in the Near East was shaped by these events.

One ambiguous phrase, one interpretive failure. Churchill failed to communicate the *intent* (destroy the Goeben at all costs). The order, taken literally, supported Troubridge's decision. The intent would have demanded the opposite.

## The Fatal Tug-of-War: Conflicting Intents

A surgeon is operating and orders a lowering of the patient's blood pressure. The anesthesiologist receives the instruction but interprets it through her own standing intent: keep vital signs stable.

When blood pressure drops, the anesthesiologist increases BP-raising medication. The surgeon sees BP rising, orders more pressure-lowering medication. The cycle continues until the patient dies.

Neither party was irrational. Each was following their own intent, which was incompatible with the other's. The surgeon never explained *why* he wanted lower pressure. The anesthesiologist had no way to know her standing intent was in conflict. Both were pursuing what they believed was correct.

This illustrates why intent communication must include *rationale* — not just what to do but why. "Lower blood pressure" is an instruction; "lower blood pressure because we need to reduce bleeding at the surgical site, which takes priority over normal vital signs during this phase" is intent. The second allows the anesthesiologist to understand when her standing goals should yield.

## Marvin's Mouse: The Trap of Specifying Means Without Rationale

Marvin's computer mouse fails. He wants an exact replacement from the same manufacturer. He writes a purchase request including the old purchase order number and the part number.

The front office receives the request and interprets the old purchase order as indicating a preferred vendor — not a required specific model. They order the closest price-equivalent mouse from that vendor. It's incompatible with his machine.

Marvin provided complete procedural specification. He specified the means in detail. What he didn't provide was rationale: why he wanted this specific item (compatibility with his specific hardware configuration). The front office lacked the technical knowledge to infer the compatibility requirement from the documentation he provided.

The fix is simple: "I need an inexpensive mouse that works with [specific machine]." This communicates intent (hardware compatibility is the constraint) rather than means (this specific product from this specific vendor). Anyone receiving that request can make sensible decisions when the specified means aren't available.

## The Prussian Principle: Training for Independent Judgment

After the Napoleonic wars, the Prussian Army undertook a systematic reform. One celebrated episode captures the cultural shift:

A major excused a tactical blunder by explaining that he was following orders. Prince Frederick Charles responded: "His Majesty made you a major because he believed you would know when **not** to obey his orders."

The Prussian military culture trained officers to understand intent deeply enough to know when literal compliance would violate the underlying purpose. They were trained *not* to follow orders blindly — but to follow the intent behind the orders, even when that required departure from the letter of the instruction.

This stands in stark contrast to the organizational structures that emerged in the 20th century, which often trained compliance rather than judgment. The problem: compliance works only when instructions are correct. In dynamic, unpredictable situations, instructions become obsolete before they're fully executed. Only intent survives contact with a changing reality.

## The Research Gap: Intent in Practice

Klein and colleagues analyzed 35 real Commander's Intent statements from military operations:

- Only 14 (40%) mentioned purpose at all
- Only 5 (14%) included antigoals
- Only 13 (37%) addressed key decisions
- Average expert rating: below midpoint, closer to "very ineffective" than "very effective"

Shattuck (1995) asked battalion commanders how they'd want subordinates to respond to unexpected developments, then asked company commanders how they would respond. **Only 34% match** between what commanders wanted and what subordinates said they'd do.

This isn't because people are incompetent. It's because communicating intent is genuinely difficult — it requires making implicit reasoning explicit, anticipating what subordinates might misunderstand, and finding the minimum sufficient statement that enables autonomy without creating ambiguity.

## Machines Need to Communicate Intent Too

On a B-727 at 30,000 feet, a passenger accidentally kicked the rudder control blade. The Flight Management System compensated automatically. Pilots noticed nothing — the system didn't signal that it was compensating.

Eventually the system reached its limits and handed back control to pilots who had no idea anything had been wrong. They were startled by the sudden change in aircraft behavior, initially misdiagnosed it as an engine problem, and the aircraft entered a dangerous descent before recovery.

Wiener (1989) captured the pilot's experience: "What is it doing? Why is it doing that? What is it going to do next?"

These three questions capture exactly what intent communication should answer. A system that can't answer them — whether the system is an automated aircraft or a human subordinate — leaves the human partner unable to coordinate effectively.

Designing for intent communication in automated systems is hard because the system must signal its state and reasoning at the right moments, at appropriate levels of detail, without creating data overload that drowns the signal. But the alternative — a system that acts without communicating its logic — creates exactly the fragile coordination seen in the flight example.

## The Team Mind: What Teams Can Do That Individuals Cannot

"Team decision making includes properties that we might never predict if we study only individuals — for example, the ability of a team to come up with ideas that are beyond the skills of any single team member."

Klein's research reveals that advanced teams have a qualitatively different cognitive character from collections of individuals. The team mind is not additive. It has properties that emerge from interaction.

### Four Features of Team Development

**1. Team Competencies**: Individual skill levels, shared routines, automaticity in coordination. Without sufficient competency, team members consume all their attention on basic tasks and have nothing left for team-level awareness.

**2. Team Identity**: Understanding of who the team is, what its boundaries are, what it controls and doesn't control. Immature teams ask "just tell me my job." Mature teams ask "what's our overall situation?" Members identify with team outputs, not just individual assignments. They think: "I might need to compensate for others, ask for help, pitch in for team goals even if I abandon my own tasks."

**3. Team Cognition**: Shared situation awareness — do members understand the same situation? Shared time horizon — how far ahead does the team look? Ability to manage uncertainty across distributed knowledge.

**4. Team Metacognition**: The ability to monitor the team's own performance and adjust. Can the team notice it's on the wrong track? Can it change strategy when the current strategy is failing — even when that change is emotionally difficult?

### The Wildland Firefighters: What Excellent Teams Look Like

Klein studied U.S. Forest Service wildland firefighting teams that routinely assembled thousands of firefighters in days, on complex terrain, under extreme conditions. These teams produced remarkably smooth, competent performance under conditions that would paralyze most organizations.

Why they excel — five factors that are individually common but rarely appear together:

1. **Continuous experience**: Fires every season; many cases per year; the adversary (fire behavior) doesn't change its tactics unpredictably
2. **Year-round sharpness**: Core members fight fires in both hemispheres, maintaining practice through the off-season
3. **Stable teams**: Core command teams work together every fire season for decades — contrasting with military 3-5 year rotation cycles that constantly reset relationships
4. **Promotion from within**: Leaders know from experience what it's like to be led; crews trust leader competence
5. **Dense networks**: A closely-knit community; commanders have worked together on large fires before; trust and communication patterns already established

The behavioral signatures: calm in the face of complexity; decisions made quickly with fragmentary information (where to set a firebreak?); command staff meetings that are blunt, efficient, and ego-robust (criticism delivered and received without defensive collapse); sensitivity to morale; disagreement expressed privately unless the stakes are critical.

One detail that Klein finds particularly striking: even during major crises, experienced teams are **simultaneously training successors** — investing in the development of the next generation. This reflects a time horizon and team identity that transcends the immediate situation.

### The Crisis Management Disaster: What Immature Teams Look Like

Klein contrasts the wildland firefighters with crisis management teams he observed in exercises. The contrast is stark.

**The Reaction Time Blindness case**: A crisis manager ordered "double the guards at sector H." Thirty-one minutes later, terrorists invaded sector H.

The crisis manager was shocked: "The order had no effect." But:
- 10 minutes: relay order, brief sector H chief, discuss hostage situation
- 2+ hours minimum: sector chief lines up extra staff, calls people at home, arranges transport
- 5-10 minutes: delay from attack start until anyone can call headquarters

The window available was 31 minutes. The time required was 2.5+ hours. He was trying to control events with a 2:1 disadvantage in response time. The analogy: an infant reaching for a moving ball — reaction time so slow the ball is gone before the hand arrives.

"The team didn't know its own reaction time."

This is a profound form of ignorance. The team was making decisions with a completely miscalibrated understanding of its own capabilities.

**The Caucus Case**: A different crisis manager, burned by previous coordination failures, instituted regular all-hands calls to keep everyone informed. He called for a caucus every 9 minutes (timed by an observer). Just as the security force made phone contact with external units, the leader would yell "Caucus!" — and they'd hang up.

The manager then let the caucus run without agenda, didn't announce when it was over, and berated the security force for not completing calls that his own caucuses had interrupted.

Intended communication actually destroyed coordination. The tool designed to create alignment created fragmentation.

### The Preconscious Failure: Three People, Three Mental Models

A B-727 simulation involved a fuel management problem. Three crew members made a decision together. In the post-flight debrief:
- Copilot (who was flying the plane): "We had two generators"
- Captain: "One and a half" (one good, one on standby)
- Flight Engineer: "One" (standby takes 1-2 minutes to activate; not available for landing)

All three had made the same decision with different mental models of the critical system state. **The team never noticed they disagreed.** The most dangerous answer belonged to the person actually flying the aircraft, and he had low confidence in it — but never surfaced his uncertainty.

This is the team's preconscious in action: what team members know but haven't voiced, ideas in parallel that never surface, thoughts that remain at a preconscious level while the team proceeds on misaligned assumptions.

The fix is explicit verification at critical decision points: "Before we commit to this, let's make sure we're seeing the same situation. How many generators do we have?" Immature teams assume shared understanding. Mature teams verify it.

### The Metacognitive Shift: When Teams Can Change Themselves

During a massive northern California forest fire, a command team was deploying all available resources but making no progress. Fires were not being extinguished.

A metacognitive moment: the team convened and realized they were "fighting fires, not putting fires out." Their current strategy had them responding to active fires, but the fires were overwhelming their capacity to suppress them individually. The solution was to stop fighting every fire, list all fires, prioritize by which ones could be extinguished with available resources, and work through the list systematically.

This required abandoning fires some crews had been protecting. It required telling firefighters their work was being deprioritized. Some friendships were permanently damaged. The decision was emotionally costly.

And it worked. For the first time, fires were actually being extinguished.

This is mature team metacognition: the ability to detect that the current strategy is failing, to hold that detection against the emotional investment in the current approach, and to make the structural change even at social cost.

Immature teams get stuck in failing strategies. They lack the metacognitive clarity to see what they're doing isn't working, or they see it but can't override the momentum of their current approach.

## Intent as Permission to Improvise

The deepest function of communicating intent is captured in the functions table Klein derives from his research:

**Functions intent communication serves:**
- **Promote independence**: Team members can evaluate their own progress without constant guidance
- **Improve performance**: Reduce need for clarifications that interrupt action
- **Detect deviations**: Allow team members to catch errors in advance by understanding what was expected
- **Enable error-catching**: Team members can use their own experience to notice planning errors
- **Promote improvisation**: React to local conditions without waiting for permission
- **Recognize opportunities**: Spot opportunities not part of the original plan
- **Set priorities**: Make trade-off decisions autonomously
- **Continue beyond outcomes**: Proceed to next task without waiting for the next order

When you communicate *why*, subordinates can adjust *how* and *when* without losing alignment. You give them permission to improvise. Without the *why*, improvisation becomes deviation — action without anchor, action that may violate the purpose even while following the letter of the instructions.

The Goeben disaster is the canonical failure of this. The letter of "do not fight superior forces" was honored. The purpose — destroy the Goeben — was abandoned. Troubridge was following orders while betraying intent.

Effective teams internalize the *why* deeply enough that their improvisation serves the purpose even in situations the instructions never anticipated. This is only possible when intent is clearly communicated — and when both parties extend themselves to bridge the gap between what can be specified and what must be understood.
