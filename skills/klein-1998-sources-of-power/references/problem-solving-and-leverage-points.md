# Nonlinear Problem Solving: Leverage Points, Ill-Defined Goals, and the Structure of Real Problems

## The Failure of Stage Models

Every problem-solving course teaches some version of the four-stage model:
1. Define the problem
2. Generate a course of action
3. Evaluate the course of action
4. Carry out the course of action

Klein argues this model is not merely imperfect — it is **wrong in its structural assumptions** for most real problems. It assumes goals are defined before solutions are generated. It assumes a clean linear progression from problem to solution. It assumes the "problem" is fixed throughout.

Real problems don't work this way.

"Most natural goals seem to be ill defined... Most of the studies of problem solving and decision making have centered on tasks with well-defined goals... Because all the problems have correct answers, there is no ambiguity in these studies."

In the lab: x + 7 = 12; solve for x. Clear goal. Single correct answer. Linear.

In the field: Write a good story. Solve a city's parking problem. Determine whether to attack or search-and-rescue at a burning building where the occupancy is unknown and the structure is failing. Multiple, contested goals. No clear correct answer. Experts disagree on what counts as success.

The stage model advice "define the goal first" traps problem-solvers at step one when goals are genuinely ambiguous — which is to say, most of the time. It mistakes a laboratory convenience for a cognitive law.

## The Nonlinear Problem-Solving Model

Klein's alternative framework identifies four **interactive processes** that occur simultaneously and in any order:

**1. Problem Detection**: An anomaly is noticed. An expectation is violated. An opportunity appears. This doesn't happen once at the start; it recurs throughout problem-solving as new information surfaces.

**2. Problem Representation**: The gap is identified, the opportunity is framed, goals are set, solvability is assessed. This includes optional diagnosis (constructing a causal story about how the current state arose) and optional forecasting (projecting how the situation will evolve). This is the largest activity in most complex problem-solving.

**3. Option Generation**: Either recognized action (knowing what to do because the situation is familiar) or constructed action (building a novel approach from leverage points). Recognition comes first; construction happens when recognition fails.

**4. Evaluation**: Mental or physical simulation of the proposed action; checking for pitfalls; deciding to execute, modify, or abandon. Evaluation can reveal new goals or barriers, which feeds back to problem representation. It can discover opportunities that didn't appear in the original framing.

These processes don't run in sequence. They run **in parallel, constrained by each other**. Evaluation changes the problem representation. Problem representation determines what options are generated. Option generation surfaces new aspects of the problem that weren't initially visible. The system is genuinely nonlinear.

## Leverage Points: What Makes Small Things Powerful

"A leverage point is a small difference that makes a large difference, a small change that can turn a situation around."

Leverage points are not context-free. What counts as a leverage point depends on the problem-solver's capabilities, the situation's constraints, and the goals being pursued. Rock climbing holds illustrate this:

A quarter-inch ridge might be a viable handhold on easy terrain for a rested, strong climber on dry rock. The same quarter-inch ridge is not a hold for a tired climber in rain on vertical faces above weak rock. A crack too narrow for a full hand might accommodate a bent finger — making it a hold for someone who knows how to use it.

Holds are not properties of rocks; they're **perception-action pairs**. The same physical feature is an affordance for one climber and not for another.

Leverage points work the same way. The "impossible crossing" on a military map was marked impossible because the standard test (full platoon crossing) failed. An Israeli commander studying that map noted: "Here, where it is marked 'Impossible.' That's where we cross." The test's failure didn't rule out crossing one or two tanks at a time. His deeper understanding of how the official records were compiled — what assumptions they baked in — let him see a viable path where the map showed a wall.

"Experts know how the official records are compiled... They know when the steps have to be followed and when to make exceptions."

## The Physician and the Bubbles: Constructive Problem Solving

A physician needed to intubate a neonate with a massive cystic hygroma blocking the trachea. The standard approach (tracheostomy on a quarter-inch-wide infant trachea) posed high infection risk and was technically extremely difficult.

The physician recalled a case he'd heard about involving a barbed-wire injury: an emergency tech had "inserted a tube where he saw bubbles." The analogy was far-fetched — adult accident victim versus neonate, emergency tech versus physician, trauma versus birth. But the physician extracted the *essence*: bubbles indicate where air is moving; air movement indicates airway access.

The leverage point: bubbles. But in the infant's case, there were no spontaneous bubbles. The physician's innovation: compress the infant's chest to force air out through any gap, locate bubbles emerging between the cysts, thread a laryngoscope through that gap.

The neonate turned pink. The procedure worked.

This is **constructive problem solving** — not retrieval of a solution from memory, but creative recombination of elements from a far-fetched analogy. The physician wasn't following a protocol. He was using an insight from one domain (bubbles = airway) and adapting it to fundamentally different constraints (force the air out rather than wait for natural breathing).

Several things make this case instructive:

First, the analogy wasn't a full-scenario match. The physician didn't think "this case reminds me of that entire barbed-wire situation." He retrieved a **fragment** — a specific feature (bubbles marking the airway) — and built from there.

Second, the adaptation required understanding the mechanism, not just the surface procedure. He knew *why* bubbles indicated airway access. That mechanistic understanding is what made it possible to adapt: if you don't have natural bubbles, create them.

Third, the leverage point had to be *constructed* before it could be used. The physician had to do something (compress the chest) to reveal the leverage point (bubbles) before he could exploit it.

## The Philadelphia Landing: Vulnerability Detection as Expertise

An airline crew is preparing to land in Philadelphia. A pilot traveling as a jumpseat observer notices several risk factors accumulating:

1. Worn brakes and tires (almost at replacement limit)
2. Extra 9,000 pounds of fuel aboard (reason unknown)
3. Thrust reverser light flickering (may not slow aircraft on landing)
4. Deteriorating weather in Philadelphia (moderate winds, rain)
5. Air traffic control assigned the **shortest available runway**

The observer — experienced, attentive — sees each factor and synthesizes them into an assessment of composite risk. The stopping distance is a function of: brake condition, tire condition, aircraft weight, thrust reverser function, runway length, and surface conditions. Each factor reduces the margin. Together, they create a potentially catastrophic situation.

He asks about the runway selection. The question prompts the captain to request a longer runway. The risk is avoided.

Two people had the same information. The observer saw the **choke point** — the leverage point working *against* you. The captain appeared to process each factor in isolation, without integrating the composite picture.

This is the dark side of leverage point recognition: expert perception of **vulnerabilities** before they become crises. The observer wasn't doing anything magical. He was running a mental model of stopping distance with multiple degraded inputs and noticing that the margins were collapsing.

The key capability: **small margins compound**. When brake condition is marginal AND tire condition is marginal AND weight is high AND thrust reverser is uncertain AND the runway is short AND the surface is wet — no single factor is alarming, but the combination is potentially fatal. Novices process each factor separately. Experts integrate across them.

This is also preventive rather than reactive expertise. The observer didn't wait for the landing to go wrong. He detected the vulnerability at the point where it could still be addressed — before commitment to the approach, before the runway assignment became fixed.

## The Apollo 13 Analysis: Where Problem-Solving Effort Actually Goes

Klein analyzed 73 problem-solving and decision-making episodes from the Apollo 13 crisis — the oxygen tank explosion and subsequent return to Earth.

The distribution of effort tells a striking story:

| Process | Episodes | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Problem Detection | 20 | Usually obvious (alarms); hardest case was recognizing LEM could be lifeboat |
| Problem Representation | 31 | **Largest activity** — forming situation awareness |
| Option Generation | 18 | Constructing novel courses of action |
| Diagnosis | 10 | Critical when present (e.g., battery malfunction was pre-existing) |
| Forecasting | 15 | Predicting consumable depletion, trajectory deviation |
| Goal Revision | 5 | Mission to rescue; shift from exploration priority to survival priority |
| Formal Option Comparison | 4 | Only four true comparisons among 73 episodes |

"Most of the problem solving did not require either diagnoses or the generation of a novel course of action. Forming an accurate problem representation was the most common activity for this incident."

This inverts conventional wisdom. Problem-solving training focuses on generating options and evaluating them. But the largest cognitive demand in the Apollo 13 crisis was **understanding what was happening** — building accurate problem representations. The heroic innovation (using the LEM as a lifeboat, finding ways to reduce power consumption, calculating reentry trajectory without Earth's horizon) required accurate problem representation before anything else was possible.

The formal option comparisons that did occur are instructive for their rarity and character:

1. **Shut reactant valves** (stop O₂ loss but lose lunar mission): Necessary, essentially no real choice
2. **Direct vs. indirect abort**: Direct was infeasible (engine damage); indirect was obvious
3. **Sleep before power-down**: Mental simulation of risk (losing a crew member during sleep from CO₂) vs. fatigue errors; chose power-down first
4. **Burn strategy** (36-hour fast vs. 20+-hour slow): Compared worst cases — consumables tight but manageable vs. jettisoning service module with unknown heat shield risk. Chose slow burn, keeping service module.

None of these involved scoring options against criteria. The fourth involved comparing worst-case scenarios via mental simulation — which risk was more catastrophic if the assumption was wrong?

The leverage points that enabled survival:
- **Moon as alignment marker**: Can't see Earth's horizon on the night side for reentry angle; but the moon's edge is visible against Earth's limb; calculate when moon disappears = confirm entry angle
- **LEM as lifeboat**: The pivot that reframed the entire problem from "repair the command module" to "use the LEM for life support"
- **Excess LEM capacity**: The difference between current reentry power requirements and available LEM power defined a solvable subproblem

## The Telemarketing Company: How Goals Evolve During Problem-Solving

A parent company with franchisees was exploring centralizing telemarketing to reduce the burden on individual franchisees. The president was lukewarm — the investment seemed large relative to the operational benefit.

As the team simulated the telemarketing center operations — thinking through how it would actually work, who would staff it, how scripts would work, how scheduling would operate — the president noticed something: the same infrastructure that would serve franchisees could *also* be used for direct catalog sales to the public.

The goal shifted. Not "solve the franchisee burden" but "launch a new revenue stream that also solves the franchisee burden."

The evaluation of one course of action revealed a higher-value opportunity that hadn't been visible at the start. This is the nonlinear property that the stage model can't accommodate: evaluation produces goals, not just verdicts on options.

Klein calls this **goal elevation** — discovering during problem-solving that a bigger or better goal is available. It's not possible to instruct problem-solvers to "define goals before generating options" and then permit goal elevation. The instruction presupposes a linear process that blocks the behavior it's supposedly guiding.

## The AI Problem: Why Search Is Not Problem-Solving

Artificial intelligence approaches to problem-solving treat the problem space as containing all possible states, and solution as search through that space using heuristics (means-ends analysis, subgoal decomposition).

Klein identifies four failures of this framework for real problem-solving:

**Problem space doesn't exist**: Humans don't generate full problem spaces except for artificial combinatorial puzzles. Complex problems defy enumeration — too many variables, too many possible states, too many interactions.

**Search misses noticing**: The AI model assumes you move through a defined space. Real problem-solving involves *noticing* things — recognizing opportunities, spotting leverage points — that weren't part of the original representation.

**Means-ends analysis misses opportunities**: The strength of means-ends analysis is finding the remaining difference and reducing it. But this means you only look for obstacles to remove, never for resources to exploit. Leverage points are resources, not obstacle-removers.

**Goals radically shift**: The means-ends model handles goal reformulation as "remove unnecessary constraints." But the telemarketing case shows goal *elevation* — a completely different goal becoming available during problem-solving, not just a less constrained version of the original.

"Computational approaches try to reduce thinking to searching. Instead of searching through a problem space, we notice opportunities that let us make progress, even if those opportunities do not correspond to the obstacles we are trying to eliminate."

The implication for any intelligent system: problem-solving cannot be reduced to search. It requires the ability to *notice* — to detect affordances, leverage points, and opportunities that weren't part of the initial problem representation. This is a form of perceptual skill, built from experience with similar domains, not a form of computational search.

## Planning as Problem-Solving: What Plans Are For

Klein and colleagues studied planning teams in military settings (Roving Sands, USS Kittyhawk, USS Mt. Whitney) and found that the standard conception of planning — as a process for generating instructions to execute — is incomplete.

Plans serve multiple functions simultaneously:

- **Coordinating team actions**: Making shared commitments explicit
- **Developing shared situation awareness**: The planning process itself builds understanding
- **Generating expectancies**: What we expect to happen when we execute
- **Supporting improvisation**: A baseline from which to deviate intelligently
- **Detecting inconsistencies**: Incompatibilities surface during planning that would otherwise surface during execution (at higher cost)
- **Establishing time horizons**: Forcing thinking ahead
- **Shaping thought and promoting learning**: Perhaps the most important — planning teaches people about the domain

This last function is crucial and counterintuitive: "Sometimes planners engage in lengthy, detailed preparations that quickly become obsolete, yet they continue with the same process, again and again; it appears that the function is to help them all learn more about the situation and to calibrate their understanding."

The plan is not the output. The *planning process* is the output. The plan is a temporary artifact that captures the current best understanding and enables coordination — but its value comes primarily from the learning and alignment it produces, not from the instructions it contains.

This reframes evaluation of planning: a plan that becomes obsolete quickly is not necessarily a failure. A planning process that produces deep shared understanding and enables intelligent improvisation — even if the specific plan becomes obsolete the moment conditions change — is a success.

The environmental forcing function shapes what kind of plans work:
- **Stable environments**: Precise, complex, integrated plans succeed
- **Dynamic environments**: Modular plans enable rapid improvisation without cascading failures when one element changes
- **Time pressure and uncertainty**: Harder to build integrated plans; modular plans are safer defaults
