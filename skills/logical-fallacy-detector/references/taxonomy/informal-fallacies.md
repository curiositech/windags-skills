# Informal Fallacies Reference

Informal fallacies are flaws not in the logical *structure* of an argument but in the *content, relevance, or framing* of the premises. The argument may have a valid deductive form, yet still fail because it relies on irrelevant support, exploits ambiguous language, or smuggles in unjustified assumptions.

Organized into three categories: **Relevance**, **Presumption**, and **Ambiguity**.

---

## PART I: FALLACIES OF RELEVANCE

The premises do not actually bear on the conclusion. They distract, misdirect, or manipulate instead of providing logical support.

---

### 1. Ad Hominem (Attack on the Person)

**Definition:** Dismissing or supporting a claim by attacking or praising the *person* making it, rather than engaging with the argument itself.

Three major variants:

#### 1a. Abusive Ad Hominem
Attacks the person's character, intelligence, or worth.

**Examples:**
- "We shouldn't take her economic proposals seriously — she's clearly not very bright."
- "His critique of the policy is worthless; he's a known liar."

**Why it's problematic:** A person's character does not determine the truth of their claims. Even dishonest or unintelligent people can make correct arguments.

**How to spot it:** The response attacks the *speaker*, not the *argument*.

**Stronger alternative:** Engage with the specific claims made, citing evidence or counterarguments.

#### 1b. Circumstantial Ad Hominem
Dismisses a claim by pointing to the speaker's circumstances, affiliations, or self-interest.

**Examples:**
- "Of course the pharmaceutical company says this drug is safe — they have a financial stake in selling it."
- "You only support open borders because you're an immigrant yourself."

**Why it's problematic:** Bias is worth noting as a *caveat*, but it doesn't automatically invalidate an argument. The claim still needs to be evaluated on its merits. (Note: pointing out a conflict of interest is legitimate context — it becomes a fallacy only when it's used as a *reason to dismiss* the argument entirely.)

**Stronger alternative:** "Given their financial interest, we should scrutinize their evidence carefully. Looking at independent studies, the data shows..."

#### 1c. Tu Quoque (You Too / Appeal to Hypocrisy)
Deflects a criticism by pointing out that the critic does the same thing.

**Examples:**
- "You're telling me to eat healthier? You eat fast food every week!"
- "You accuse me of not listening, but you don't listen either!"

**Why it's problematic:** Even if the critic is a hypocrite, that doesn't make their criticism false. The argument and the arguer's behavior are separate questions.

**Stronger alternative:** Address the substance of the criticism separately from the person's behavior.

---

### 2. Appeal to Authority (Argumentum ad Verecundiam)

**Definition:** Treating someone's opinion as conclusive evidence simply because of their authority, prestige, or expertise — especially when that authority is outside their domain or the claim is contested.

**Examples:**
- "Einstein believed in a deterministic universe, so quantum indeterminacy must be wrong."
- "My doctor says vaccines cause autism, so it must be true."
- "A Nobel laureate in physics says this economic policy will work."

**Why it's problematic:** Authority is a *heuristic*, not proof. Experts can be wrong, especially outside their domain. Consensus matters more than individual authority.

**How to spot it:** The sole support for a claim is someone's credentials or status, without reference to evidence or reasoning.

**Stronger alternative:** Cite the evidence or reasoning the expert used, not just their endorsement.

**Note:** Appealing to genuine expert consensus in a relevant domain is *not* a fallacy — it is a reasonable epistemic shortcut. The fallacy arises when a single authority is treated as definitive, especially outside their field.

---

### 3. Appeal to Emotion (Argumentum ad Passiones)

**Definition:** Substituting emotional manipulation for logical reasoning — using fear, pity, flattery, envy, or other emotions to move someone to accept a conclusion.

**Examples:**
- "Think of the children who will suffer if we pass this policy!" (when the argument requires evidence of that suffering)
- "You're one of our smartest customers. Surely someone as perceptive as you can see the value in this upgrade."
- "If you don't donate now, these animals will die."

**Why it's problematic:** Emotional appeals are not inherently fallacious — they become fallacious when the emotion *substitutes for* rather than *accompanies* logical support. Moving someone to act on false or unsubstantiated emotional premises is manipulative.

**Stronger alternative:** Present the evidence, then note the emotional stakes: "Studies show X consequence — and that matters because it affects real people's lives."

---

### 4. Appeal to Nature (Naturalistic Fallacy)

**Definition:** Claiming something is good, safe, or correct simply because it is "natural," or bad because it is "unnatural."

**Examples:**
- "This supplement is completely natural, so it must be safe."
- "Homosexuality is unnatural, therefore it is wrong."
- "We should eat only raw foods — that's how humans evolved."

**Why it's problematic:** Nature is morally and epistemically neutral. Many natural things are harmful (arsenic, botulinum toxin, predation); many unnatural things are beneficial (antibiotics, eyeglasses, surgery).

**Stronger alternative:** Evaluate safety, health, or ethical value on the basis of evidence and reasoned principles, not naturalism.

---

### 5. Appeal to Tradition (Argumentum ad Antiquitatem)

**Definition:** Arguing that something is correct, good, or justified simply because it has been done or believed for a long time.

**Examples:**
- "We've always done performance reviews this way — there's no reason to change."
- "Marriage has traditionally been between a man and a woman."

**Why it's problematic:** Longevity does not establish correctness. Many longstanding practices have been wrong (slavery, bloodletting). Tradition provides evidence of durability, not truth or value.

**Stronger alternative:** Examine whether the original reasons for the practice still apply and whether evidence supports its continuation.

---

### 6. Bandwagon (Argumentum ad Populum / Appeal to Popularity)

**Definition:** Arguing that something is true, good, or desirable simply because many people believe or do it.

**Examples:**
- "Nine out of ten Americans support this policy — so it must be the right approach."
- "Everyone's investing in crypto right now. You'd be foolish not to."

**Why it's problematic:** Popularity is not a criterion of truth. Majorities have been collectively wrong throughout history (geocentrism, eugenics, etc.).

**Stronger alternative:** Present actual evidence for the claim; reference the population's reasoning rather than just their conclusion.

---

### 7. Red Herring (Ignoratio Elenchi)

**Definition:** Introducing irrelevant information to divert attention from the actual issue being debated.

**Examples:**
- "Why are you worried about my expense report when the whole department has been underperforming?"
- "Senator X is being questioned about tax policy, but what about his opponent's record on healthcare?"

**Why it's problematic:** The new topic, however compelling, does not address the original claim. It is a distraction tactic.

**How to spot it:** The response changes the subject or raises issues that are logically unconnected to the original claim.

**Stronger alternative:** Address the original question directly before raising additional considerations.

---

### 8. Straw Man (Straw Person)

**Definition:** Misrepresenting or exaggerating an opponent's position to make it easier to attack, then refuting the misrepresentation instead of the actual argument.

**Examples:**
- Actual claim: "We should reduce military spending by 5%."
  Straw man: "So you want to leave our country defenseless."
- Actual claim: "Children should have more free play time."
  Straw man: "You think children shouldn't have any structured education."

**Why it's problematic:** The original position is never actually addressed. The debate becomes unproductive because both sides argue past each other.

**How to spot it:** The rebuttal attacks a position the original speaker clearly did not hold. Look for hyperbole, extreme restatements, or scope distortion.

**Stronger alternative:** "If I understand correctly, your claim is [accurate restatement]. My response to that is..."

---

### 9. Appeal to Ignorance (Argumentum ad Ignorantiam)

**Definition:** Claiming something is true because it hasn't been proven false, or false because it hasn't been proven true.

**Examples:**
- "No one has disproven that ghosts exist, so they must be real."
- "Science can't prove consciousness is purely physical, so dualism must be correct."

**Why it's problematic:** Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, but neither is it positive evidence for the claim. The burden of proof lies with whoever asserts a positive claim.

**Note:** In legal contexts, the presumption of innocence ("innocent until proven guilty") is a deliberate policy choice — not a logical proof of innocence.

**Stronger alternative:** Acknowledge the uncertainty and note what evidence *would* be needed to settle the claim.

---

### 10. Genetic Fallacy

**Definition:** Evaluating a claim based solely on its origin — where it came from, who first proposed it, or how it arose — rather than on its merits.

**Examples:**
- "That idea was invented during the Cold War as propaganda — it can't be right."
- "This argument was originally made by racists, so we should reject it."

**Why it's problematic:** The origin of an argument does not determine its truth value. Good arguments can come from bad sources; bad arguments can come from good sources.

**Note:** If the origin reveals a systematic bias or error (e.g., a study funded by a conflicted party), that is relevant context — but it is a reason to scrutinize more carefully, not to automatically dismiss.

## PART II: FALLACIES OF PRESUMPTION

The argument assumes what it needs to prove, artificially constrains the options, or draws conclusions from insufficient evidence.

---

### 11. Begging the Question (Petitio Principii / Circular Reasoning)

**Definition:** The conclusion of the argument is assumed (often covertly) in one of its premises. The argument is circular — it uses the conclusion as a premise to support the conclusion.

**Examples:**
- "The Bible is true because it says so in the Bible."
- "This law is just because it's the law, and the law is just by definition."
- "Of course she's trustworthy — she's an honest person. How do I know she's honest? She'd never lie to me."

**Why it's problematic:** No new information is provided. The argument doesn't advance understanding; it restates the conclusion in different words.

**How to spot it:** Try to identify whether accepting the premises already requires accepting the conclusion. If yes, the argument is circular.

**Stronger alternative:** Provide independent evidence for the claim that doesn't presuppose it.

---

### 12. False Dilemma / False Dichotomy

**Definition:** Presenting only two options as if they were the only possibilities, when in fact other options exist.

**Examples:**
- "You're either with us or against us."
- "Either we implement this policy or the company will fail."
- "You can have security or freedom — not both."

**Why it's problematic:** The artificial binary excludes middle-ground positions, hybrid solutions, or entirely different framings of the problem.

**How to spot it:** "Either A or B" framing; claims that there is no middle ground when there clearly is.

**Stronger alternative:** "One option is X; another is Y; hybrid approaches or entirely different strategies may also be worth considering."

---

### 13. Hasty Generalization

**Definition:** Drawing a broad general conclusion from an insufficient, unrepresentative, or too-small sample.

**Examples:**
- "I've met three French people and they were all rude. French people are rude."
- "The last two hires from that university were poor performers. We should stop recruiting there."
- "Every time I've tried to park downtown it's been impossible. Downtown parking is a disaster."

**Why it's problematic:** Small or biased samples are unreliable guides to general patterns. Rare or extreme cases are overrepresented in personal experience.

**How to spot it:** Conclusions about "all," "most," "always," or "never" based on anecdote.

**Stronger alternative:** "In my experience X, though that may not be representative. Broader data would be needed to generalize."

---

### 14. Slippery Slope

**Definition:** Asserting that one event will inevitably lead through a chain of consequences to some extreme outcome, without establishing that the intermediate steps are likely or necessary.

**Examples:**
- "If we allow same-sex marriage, next people will want to marry animals."
- "If we regulate assault weapons, eventually the government will confiscate all guns and we'll live in a tyranny."
- "If I give you an extension on this assignment, everyone will expect extensions and academic standards will collapse."

**Why it's problematic:** The causal chain between the first step and the extreme outcome is asserted, not demonstrated. Each step in the chain requires justification.

**Note:** Not all slope arguments are fallacious. If you can provide evidence that the intermediate steps are genuinely likely, the argument becomes a legitimate empirical prediction ("gateway effect"). The fallacy is treating the chain as inevitable without evidence.

**Stronger alternative:** Either demonstrate why the intermediate steps are likely, or concede that the argument is speculative.

---

### 15. Loaded Question (Complex Question)

**Definition:** A question that presupposes the truth of something not yet established, framed so that any direct answer appears to concede the presupposition.

**Examples:**
- "Have you stopped cheating on your taxes?"
- "Why do young people today have no work ethic?"
- "When did you start believing you were better than everyone else?"

**Why it's problematic:** The question smuggles in an assumption. Answering "yes" or "no" both confirm the embedded claim.

**How to spot it:** The question cannot be answered without accepting a hidden premise.

**Stronger alternative:** Identify and challenge the embedded assumption: "I need to address the assumption in your question before I can answer it."

---

### 16. No True Scotsman

**Definition:** When a counterexample is offered to a universal claim, the original claim is modified post hoc by adding an ad hoc qualifier to exclude the counterexample, without providing principled grounds for the exclusion.

**Examples:**
- "No Scotsman would behave like that."
  "But Angus is a Scotsman and he did."
  "Well, no *true* Scotsman would."

- "Real entrepreneurs never give up."
  "Steve Jobs left his first company."
  "He wasn't a *real* entrepreneur then."

**Why it's problematic:** The category is redefined to immunize the original claim against refutation. No evidence could ever falsify the claim — a telltale sign of unfalsifiable reasoning.

**Stronger alternative:** Either provide principled criteria for category membership in advance, or accept that the counterexample challenges the original claim.

---

## PART III: FALLACIES OF AMBIGUITY

The argument exploits vagueness or multiple meanings to shift meaning and manufacture apparent support.

---

### 17. Equivocation

**Definition:** Using the same word or phrase in two different senses within the same argument, treating the two uses as equivalent.

**Examples:**
- "The sign said 'fine for parking here.' So I parked there — it's fine!"
- "Feathers are light. Light travels faster than sound. Therefore feathers travel faster than sound."
- "Evolution requires random mutation. But nothing in science is random — everything has a cause. Therefore evolution is false."

**Why it's problematic:** The argument shifts the meaning of a key term mid-argument. The conclusion only seems to follow because the word appears to be the same.

**How to spot it:** Find the key term that appears in both premises. Ask whether it means exactly the same thing in each use. If not, the argument has at least one hidden equivocation.

**Stronger alternative:** Define terms precisely and maintain consistent definitions throughout the argument.

---

### 18. Amphiboly

**Definition:** An argument that exploits grammatical ambiguity — a sentence or phrase that can be parsed in more than one way — to support a conclusion.

**Examples:**
- "The professor said on Monday he would give the exam." (Does the exam occur on Monday, or did the professor say it on Monday?)
- Oracle at Delphi: "If Croesus goes to war, a great empire will fall." (Which empire is unspecified.)
- "Visiting relatives can be boring." (Are the relatives visiting you, or are you visiting them?)

**Why it's problematic:** The argument exploits syntactic ambiguity. The two interpretations have different logical implications.

**Stronger alternative:** Rewrite the ambiguous sentence to make the intended meaning explicit.

---

### 19. Composition

**Definition:** Inferring that what is true of the *parts* of a whole must also be true of the *whole*.

**Examples:**
- "Every player on this team is excellent. Therefore this team is excellent."
- "Each tile in this mosaic is simple and plain. Therefore the mosaic is simple and plain."
- "Sodium is a reactive metal. Chlorine is a toxic gas. Therefore table salt (NaCl) is a reactive, toxic substance."

**Why it's problematic:** Wholes have emergent properties that differ from their parts. Aggregation can produce qualitatively different outcomes.

**Stronger alternative:** Evaluate the whole independently from its parts where emergent properties are relevant.

---

### 20. Division

**Definition:** Inferring that what is true of a *whole* must also be true of each of its *parts*.

**Examples:**
- "This is an excellent team, so every player must be excellent."
- "The United States is a wealthy nation. Therefore every American is wealthy."
- "Water is wet. Therefore each molecule of H₂O is wet."

**Why it's problematic:** Properties that belong to a collective or aggregate often cannot be attributed to individual members. "Wetness" is a macroscopic property that does not apply at the molecular level.

**Stronger alternative:** Treat collective and individual-level claims separately; provide evidence for each level.

## Notes on Application

**On distinguishing weak arguments from fallacies:** Not every bad argument has a specific fallacy name. Some arguments are simply unsupported, unclear, or strategically poor without fitting a named category. Reserve fallacy labels for clear pattern matches.

**On context-sensitivity:** Whether an argument commits a fallacy often depends on context. An appeal to authority is not fallacious when the authority is genuinely expert, the domain is relevant, and the consensus is robust. A slippery slope is not fallacious if there is real evidence the slope exists. Always evaluate the specific instance.

**On intent:** Fallacies can be committed unconsciously (habits of thought) or deliberately (rhetorical manipulation). In interpersonal and relationship contexts, assume unconscious error before malicious intent. Focus on the structure of the argument, not the character of the person.
