# Research and Positioning

Use this before brand, design-family, or content decisions.

## Research Inputs

Collect and record:

- product category and primary user jobs
- direct competitors, adjacent competitors, and aspirational references
- visual references from real products, not only design galleries
- domain conventions users expect
- conventions worth breaking
- trust, compliance, and credibility requirements
- content strategy and editorial opportunities
- conversion or workflow metrics

If the project is serious, use a design archive sidecar and a competitive map
sidecar when the environment permits authorized agent work.

## Competitive Map

Map the field spatially instead of listing competitors. Useful axes:

- expressive brand vs operational utility
- consumer warmth vs enterprise authority
- editorial depth vs conversion speed
- data density vs whitespace
- premium craft vs approachable clarity
- technical depth vs mainstream simplicity

The design direction should occupy defensible white space, not imitate the
median competitor.

## TypeUI and Design-Family Selection

TypeUI is useful as a design-system inspiration source because it treats a
portable skill file as a design contract. Do not blindly pull a style. Evaluate
whether a family fits the product:

- Paper: editorial, calm, artifact-driven
- Bento: modular product storytelling and dashboards
- Refined: premium but restrained
- Dramatic: high-impact launches and portfolios
- Sleek: dense precision and operational tools
- Professional: commerce and conversion
- Contemporary: current startup energy
- Flat: performance-first, direct, low ornament

Record the chosen family, why it fits, and which traits are explicitly rejected.

## Brand Direction

Choose:

- brand promise
- voice and vocabulary
- visual nouns and metaphors
- typography stance
- color temperature and contrast strategy
- imagery strategy
- motion role
- interface density
- trust signals

Do not use fake customer quotes, fake awards, invented logos, or generic claims.
When proof is unavailable, write precise, modest copy.

## Visual Archive Minimum

For small projects, inspect at least 12 relevant examples.

For serious commercial work, inspect 30 to 100 examples before the first major
visual direction. For category-defining work, use a long-running archive plan
with checkpoints.

For each example, capture:

- URL and date
- product category
- palette
- typography
- layout system
- interaction style
- content model
- what to borrow
- what to avoid

## Failure Modes

- Treating a UI kit as a brand.
- Choosing colors before knowing the category and audience.
- Copying the current trend cluster and calling it differentiation.
- Ignoring mobile competitors when the product will be used on phones.
- Designing a landing page when the user asked for an app.
- Writing a blog without a real editorial angle.
