# Annotated Speech Examples

A reference for the `forensic-speech-structure` skill. Load this when the user
asks for examples, wants to see the structure in action, or needs a model to
adapt for their own context.

Each example is annotated with:
- Section markers and labels
- Notes on rhetorical choices and ordering
- Devices used (named)
- Strengths and areas for improvement

---

## Example 1: Legal Closing Argument (Fictional)

**Context:** Criminal defense. Defendant accused of theft. Evidence is circumstantial.
Jury audience. Approximately 8 minutes.

---

**[EXORDIUM]**

> "Ladies and gentlemen of the jury, twelve days ago you walked into this courtroom
> strangers to Marcus Webb. Today, you know something about him that almost no one
> else in this room does: you know the full story. Every exhibit. Every witness.
> Every inconsistency. You have seen what the prosecution chose to show you —
> and what it chose to leave out."

*Annotation:*
- Opens by **elevating the jury's authority** — a credibility-transfer technique
  that invests them in the verdict
- Closes the Exordium with a **tension-setting contrast** ("chose to show / chose
  to leave out") that signals the argument to come without stating it
- No "today I will prove" language — instead, it creates anticipatory tension
- *Device*: anaphora ("Every exhibit. Every witness. Every inconsistency.")

---

**[NARRATIO]**

> "Marcus Webb is a warehouse supervisor. He has worked the same shift at Hadley
> Logistics for nine years. On the night of November 14th, he clocked in at 6 PM,
> ate dinner in the break room at 9:30, and clocked out at 6:10 AM. The prosecution
> says that somewhere in those twelve hours, he stole $4,000 in merchandise from a
> locked storage room. They say the evidence proves it. Let's look at that evidence."

*Annotation:*
- Establishes the **human baseline** — nine years, routine, predictability
- Uses **chronological ordering** to make the accusation feel like an interruption
  of a perfectly ordinary night, not a revelation
- The phrase "let's look at that evidence" is a pivot line that bridges into Confirmatio
- Deliberately brief (under 90 seconds) — facts are not in dispute; the interpretation is
- *Device*: cataloguing specifics ("clocked in at 6 PM, ate dinner... clocked out")
  builds verisimilitude

---

**[CONFIRMATIO]**

*Argument 1 (second-strongest): Physical access was impossible*

> "The prosecution's own witness — their own security contractor — testified that
> the storage room uses a key fob system. There are only three fobs. The manager
> had one. The overnight supervisor had one. The third was logged as 'inactive' in
> the building's access system. Marcus Webb was never issued a fob. Not that night.
> Not ever. The prosecution did not produce a fourth fob. They cannot explain how
> Marcus entered that room."

*Argument 2 (weakest, buried in middle): No evidence places him at the scene*

> "The prosecution points to a single security camera still image — grainy, taken
> from 40 feet away — and says 'that's Marcus.' Their own expert said the image
> quality was, and I quote, 'insufficient for facial identification.' Yet here we
> are. That image is the foundation of their case. A foundation of blur."

*Argument 3 (strongest, last): The actual timeline exonerates him*

> "Finally: timing. The theft, according to the prosecution, occurred between 10 PM
> and midnight. You have the break room log. Marcus Webb signed in at 9:28 PM. You
> have testimony from two colleagues — neither of whom knew each other outside work,
> neither of whom knew they'd be called to testify together — who place Marcus at
> that table until at least 11 PM. Two independent witnesses. One unambiguous log.
> This is not reasonable doubt. This is impossible doubt."

*Annotation:*
- Arguments ordered: second-strongest → weakest → strongest (**anti-climax middle**)
- Each argument follows Claim → Evidence → Impact
- The phrase "a foundation of blur" is an **epigram** — short, memorable, devastating
- "Two independent witnesses. One unambiguous log. This is not reasonable doubt. This
  is impossible doubt." — *device*: **tricolon** building to an **antithesis** climax
- Note on weakness: the second argument (image quality) relies on hedged expert
  testimony — it would be stronger with a competing expert witness. A good opposing
  counsel will exploit this. Could be tightened or repositioned.

---

**[REFUTATIO]**

> "The prosecution will tell you in their closing that the colleagues are friends
> covering for each other. But their own investigation showed these two men did not
> socialize outside work — the prosecution's investigator testified to that. They
> will tell you Marcus had motive because he was passed over for promotion. Having
> a reason to be unhappy is not a reason to commit a crime. If it were, half the
> workforce of America would be in handcuffs. And they will tell you: 'who else
> could have done it?' Ladies and gentlemen, that is not evidence. That is
> the sound of a case with no case left."

*Annotation:*
- Uses **anticipatory refutation** — addressing the opponent's argument before
  they make it (steals their thunder, signals confidence)
- Addresses three likely objections in descending urgency
- The motive rebuttal uses **reductio ad absurdum** briefly — risky but effective
  here because it lands before any one takes it too seriously
- Final line is an **epigram** — "the sound of a case with no case left" — meant
  to be the jury's internal monologue when the prosecutor rises
- *Improvement note*: The third refutation ("who else?") is the weakest — it
  refutes an absence of evidence without providing a counter-theory. In a stronger
  closing, you'd offer at least a gesture toward the alternative.

---

**[PERORATIO]**

> "You walked into this courtroom twelve days ago as strangers to Marcus Webb.
> You leave knowing that the prosecution has not proven, beyond a reasonable doubt,
> that he did this. Not one piece of physical evidence. Not one. No fob. No image
> that holds up. A timeline that doesn't fit. And two witnesses who, by every
> measure, had no reason to lie.
>
> The law gives you one verdict when the case is not proven: not guilty. That is
> not a technicality. That is the system working. That is justice.
>
> Give Marcus Webb justice."

*Annotation:*
- **Callbacks to Exordium**: opens with the exact phrase ("twelve days ago...
  strangers to Marcus Webb") — creates structural closure and emotional resonance
- **Summary without repetition**: restates the key failures of the prosecution's
  case in compressed, punchy form — not a recitation of the argument, but its essence
- **Amplificatio**: "That is not a technicality. That is the system working." —
  elevates the personal verdict to a civic act
- **Call to action**: "Give Marcus Webb justice." — specific, imperative, three words
- *Device*: tricolon + anaphora in the summary ("No fob. No image that holds up.
  A timeline that doesn't fit.")
- The final sentence is a **short, declarative imperative** — as it should always be

## Example 2: Policy Debate Speech (School Safety)

**Context:** High school policy debate. Affirmative constructive speech. Resolution:
"The federal government should mandate armed resource officers in all public schools."

Note: This is an illustrative debate example — the argument is presented as a model
of debate structure, not as an endorsement of the policy.

---

**[PLAN]**

> "The affirmative team proposes the following plan: The Department of Education
> shall require that all public elementary, middle, and high schools maintain at
> least one trained, armed school resource officer during school hours. Officers
> shall complete a minimum of 40 hours of de-escalation and school-crisis training
> annually. Funding: $1.2 billion annually through reallocation of existing Homeland
> Security community grant programs."

*Annotation:*
- A policy debate plan must be **specific and enforceable**: agency (Dept. of Ed),
  mandate (at least one officer), qualifications (40 hours, named type), and
  funding mechanism
- Vague plans invite solvency takeouts ("Your plan doesn't actually do anything")
- The funding mechanism pre-empts a common negative objection

---

**[INHERENCY]**

> "Status quo fails. Right now, only 58% of U.S. public schools have any form of
> armed security presence, according to the National Center for Education Statistics,
> 2023. In rural districts — where response times average 11 minutes — that number
> drops to 31%. Market forces and local budgets will not solve this. States have
> tried voluntary programs for fifteen years. Coverage has barely moved. Federal
> mandate is the only lever that changes this."

*Annotation:*
- Inherency answers the question: "Why won't the status quo fix itself?"
- Numbers are specific and sourced — critical in policy debate
- The historical argument ("fifteen years of voluntary programs") is a powerful
  inherency argument: it shows the problem isn't lack of awareness, it's lack of
  mechanism
- *Improvement*: the 31% rural statistic is the most powerful — it could be
  developed more before moving on

---

**[HARMS]**

> "The harms are magnitude and probability. 93 children and staff were killed in
> school shootings between 2018 and 2023. The Secret Service's 2023 Threat Assessment
> found that in 93% of school attacks, the attacker was known to someone at the school —
> suggesting early intervention points. More: the RAND Corporation's review found
> that school resource officers reduce the probability of a shooting escalating to
> mass-casualty level by 32%. Current absence of consistent officer presence is a
> structural gap the data says costs lives."

*Annotation:*
- Policy debate harms require: **magnitude** (how big?), **probability** (how likely?),
  and ideally **reversibility** (can we undo the harm if we don't act?)
- Stacking authoritative sources (Secret Service, RAND) builds logos quickly
- The 93% figure pivots from harms toward solvency — efficient but creates a structural
  bleed between sections that an alert negative team will exploit

---

**[SOLVENCY]**

> "Our plan solves. The RAND review establishes a 32% reduction in escalation when
> a trained officer is present. Applied to current incidence rates, that's
> statistically 8–12 lives per year saved at minimum. The de-escalation training
> requirement is drawn directly from programs piloted in Colorado and Virginia that
> reduced use-of-force incidents by officers by 60% while maintaining response
> capability. Our plan isn't guesswork — it's a scaled version of what already works."

*Annotation:*
- Solvency must show the **mechanism**: why does this plan fix the identified harm?
  Here: officer presence → reduced escalation (RAND) and training → reduced officer
  harm (Colorado/Virginia pilots)
- **Quantified impact**: "8–12 lives per year" — necessary in policy debate
- "Scaled version of what already works" is a **stock phrase** for solvency advocates —
  it grounds speculation in empirical precedent
- *Note on weakness*: this speech does not preempt the obvious negative argument
  (disparate impact of armed officers on students of color). A strong negative team
  will run this. The affirmative needs a prepared response in the 1AR.

## Example 3: Personal Persuasive Speech — Proposing a Plan to a Reluctant Partner

**Context:** Informal but high-stakes. One partner wants to relocate for a job.
The other is resistant. No formal debate structure — uses TED-style adapted
with classical scaffolding underneath.

---

**[EXORDIUM — Story hook]**

> "Do you remember when we moved here? We'd loaded everything we owned into a
> van that was one blown tire from disaster. You were terrified. I was terrified.
> And two years later, this city is home. I want to tell you about another move —
> and why I think it could do the same thing again."

*Annotation:*
- Uses **shared memory** as the ethos and pathos opening — the speaker is not
  claiming authority, they're claiming shared history
- The "one blown tire" detail is vivid and funny — it lowers defenses before
  the argument begins
- The final clause ("I want to tell you about another move") is a thesis that
  is also an invitation — appropriate for an intimate audience where aggression
  would backfire

---

**[NARRATIO — Context]**

> "Worthington is offering me the senior design lead position. It's a 40% salary
> increase. It's the kind of work I've been trying to get to for eight years. The
> position is in Austin. I know what that means for you — your job, your mom,
> the apartment we finally got right. I'm not pretending those things aren't real."

*Annotation:*
- The **proleptic concession** ("I know what that means for you") is critical for
  a resistant audience — it signals the speaker is not steamrolling
- Facts are laid out plainly, without exaggeration ("40% salary increase" instead
  of "massive raise")
- The phrase "the apartment we finally got right" is a small, specific, pathos-loaded
  detail that says: I see what you'd be giving up

---

**[CONFIRMATIO — Three arguments]**

*Argument 1: Financial*
> "Financially: a 40% salary increase puts us in a position to pay off the
> car loan in a year and still add $800 a month to savings. We've been talking
> about buying for three years. Austin changes that timeline from 'someday'
> to 'two years from now.'"

*Argument 2: Career trajectory (strongest)*
> "For my career, this is the role. Not a role — the role. Every conversation
> I've had with people I respect in this field has pointed to senior design lead
> as the credential that opens every other door. If I don't take this, I don't
> know when another one appears. I've been waiting eight years for one."

*Argument 3: Your concerns are addressed*
> "I've looked at remote options for your firm's Austin client list — they have
> two. I've also talked to two people at Worthington who made this exact move
> with partners in similar situations. Both found work within four months.
> I'm not asking you to quit your job. I'm asking you to give us the time to
> find yours."

*Annotation:*
- For an intimate audience, the ordering shifts: financial (removes anxiety),
  career (the core ask), then addressing concerns (the partner's position)
- The "eight years" figure is a **personal authority** claim — it establishes
  investment, not entitlement
- The last argument in the Confirmatio is specifically designed for this audience:
  addressing the partner's concern *as an argument for the move*, not just a
  concession — a subtle but powerful technique
- *Device*: "Not a role — the role" is **diacope** (repetition with intervening words)

---

**[REFUTATIO — Addressed as acknowledgments, not combat]**

> "I know the hardest part is your mom. Austin is a flight, not a drive. That's
> real. I've been thinking about whether we could commit to visiting every six
> weeks for the first year — budget it in explicitly. That's more visits than we
> averaged last year. And if it's not enough, we talk about it and adjust.
> I'm not asking you to trade your family for my career. I'm asking whether
> we can build something that holds both."

*Annotation:*
- In an intimate, high-stakes personal context, **the Refutatio must be relational,
  not combative** — you are not defeating an opponent, you are showing a partner
  that their concerns have weight
- The flight-to-visit calculation ("more visits than we averaged last year") is a
  **quantified concession** — it makes the abstract loss concrete and then manages it
- "I'm not asking you to trade your family for my career" directly names the
  fear the partner likely hasn't voiced — this kind of **explicit fear-naming**
  builds trust
- *Improvement*: this section could include more space for the partner to respond.
  In a real conversation, you'd pause here.

---

**[PERORATIO]**

> "We moved here scared. It worked. I'm scared about this too. But I believe
> in us doing hard things. I'm asking you to trust that belief one more time —
> with your eyes open, with your concerns heard, and with me committed to making
> sure this works for both of us. Not just for me."

*Annotation:*
- **Callback to Exordium**: "We moved here scared. It worked." — closes the loop
- **Ethos of vulnerability**: "I'm scared about this too" — disarms any sense
  that the speaker is cavalier about the ask
- Call to action is relational, not transactional: "trust that belief" — frames
  the decision as an act of their shared identity, not an economic calculation
- *Device*: **tricolon** in the penultimate sentence ("with your eyes open, with
  your concerns heard, with me committed...") slows the pace before the final
  declarative close
- Final sentence lands the only thing that matters: "Not just for me."

## Example 4: Professional Presentation — Proposing a New Initiative

**Context:** Mid-level manager presenting to executive leadership.
Recommending a company switch to a new vendor for CRM software.
15-minute slot. Audience: skeptical but open.

---

**[EXORDIUM]**

> "Last quarter, our sales team spent an average of 2.3 hours per week resolving
> data sync errors in Salesforce. That's 90 hours per rep per year. Across our 24
> reps, that's 2,160 hours — the equivalent of one full-time employee doing nothing
> but fixing broken software. I'd like to talk about what we could do with that
> employee instead."

*Annotation:*
- Opens with **a specific, quantified problem** — no soft setup, no "thank you
  for having me"
- The **aggregation move** (per rep → across 24 reps → equivalent to one FTE) is
  a classic executive-audience technique: make the abstract concrete and costly
- The closing question ("what we could do with that employee instead") creates
  forward motion — the audience is now curious, not defensive
- *Device*: progressive quantification for emphasis

---

**[NARRATIO]**

> "We've been on Salesforce for six years. For the first three, it served us well.
> Our team was 12 reps, our product line was simpler, and the integrations held.
> Since 2023, we've added 12 reps, two product lines, and a new order management
> system. The integration layer hasn't kept up. IT has logged 47 sync error tickets
> in the last six months. Our Customer Success scores dropped 8 points in Q3 —
> the quarter we added the second integration."

*Annotation:*
- Narrative structure: **past success → growth → breaking point** — positions the
  change as a response to success, not a failure of the status quo
- This framing matters for an executive audience: you are not criticizing a past
  decision, you are noting that conditions have changed
- The correlation ("Q3 — the quarter we added the second integration") is not
  stated as causation — appropriate epistemic honesty that builds credibility
- 47 tickets and -8 NPS points are **specific, auditable numbers** — executives
  respond to data they can verify

---

**[CONFIRMATIO]**

*Argument 1: Cost savings*
> "HubSpot's enterprise tier costs $38,000 per year for our seat count.
> We currently pay $52,000 for Salesforce. That's a $14,000 annual saving
> before we count the integration engineering costs, which IT estimates at
> $18,000 last year alone. Total potential savings: $32,000 annually."

*Argument 2: Fit with current stack*
> "HubSpot's native integration with NetSuite — our OMS — is maintained by
> HubSpot's engineering team, not ours. That eliminates the sync error problem
> at its source. Pilot data from two comparable companies in our space —
> both available as references — showed sync errors drop to near zero within
> 60 days of migration."

*Argument 3: Team adoption (strongest)*
> "I surveyed the sales team last week. Unprompted, seven of 24 reps named the
> data sync problem as their top productivity blocker. When shown a demo of
> HubSpot's interface, 19 of 24 said it was 'easier to use' than current Salesforce.
> We can build the best tech stack in the world — it fails if the people using it
> are working around it instead of with it. Our team is ready to move."

*Annotation:*
- Classic three-argument structure for executive audiences: **financial → technical
  → human/adoption** — executives often block on adoption risk; saving it for last
  means the financial and technical case is already made before the hardest question
- Survey data is used as **primary research** — shows the presenter did the work
  and didn't just rely on vendor materials
- "We can build the best tech stack..." is a **concession move** that
  pre-empts the "but we need to know it will be adopted" objection by turning
  it into a confirmation

---

**[REFUTATIO]**

> "The main concern I've heard in pre-meetings is migration risk. Valid. We have
> 6 years of Salesforce data. I'm proposing a phased migration: pilot with four
> reps in January, full team in Q2 if the pilot succeeds. The migration vendor
> I've scoped, DataSync Pro, has completed 200+ Salesforce-to-HubSpot migrations;
> they guarantee data integrity or they re-run the migration at no cost.
> If the pilot fails, we've learned something and spent $8,000 instead of $38,000."

*Annotation:*
- Begins by **naming the objection explicitly** ("The main concern I've heard") —
  signals that the speaker was listening before the meeting, not just pitching
- The **phased approach** is a classic risk-mitigation structural move in business
  cases — it lowers the stakes of approval
- "If the pilot fails... $8,000 instead of $38,000" is a **loss-framing inversion**:
  you are quantifying the cost of being wrong, which is lower than the cost of
  doing nothing
- Vendor guarantee is specific and auditable — not just "they're good"

---

**[PERORATIO]**

> "We started this conversation with 2,160 hours of wasted productivity. A phased
> migration this quarter gives us a path to reclaim them. Financial savings of
> $32,000 annually. A tech stack that our team will actually use. I'm asking for
> approval to run the pilot in January. If it works — and the data says it will —
> we move in Q2. If it doesn't, we've spent $8,000 learning something important.
> That's a decision I'm confident asking you to make."

*Annotation:*
- **Callback to opening number** ("2,160 hours") — closes the loop, rewards
  audience memory of the opening
- **Compressed summary**: three bullets in three sentences (savings, fit, adoption)
- **Specific ask**: "approval to run the pilot in January" — not "consider this"
  or "explore options" — executives want to know exactly what they are approving
- Final line ("That's a decision I'm confident asking you to make") transfers
  decision-making authority gracefully while expressing speaker confidence —
  **a closing technique appropriate for proposals to leadership**
- *Device*: conditional structure ("If it works... If it doesn't...") models rational
  risk analysis in real time, reinforcing logos in the final moment

## Summary: What Makes These Examples Work

| Example | Key Strength | Improvement Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| Legal closing | Structural closure (callback); strong tricolon | Refutatio's third point needs a counter-theory |
| Policy debate | Specific, sourced, quantified throughout | Solvency bleeds into harms; needs cleaner sectioning |
| Personal persuasion | Relational refutatio; fear-naming technique | Could allow more space for response before Peroratio |
| Professional presentation | Quantified everything; phased risk structure | Could add a brief case study of a successful migration |
