# Bad-Faith Tactics: Deep Reference

This reference provides detailed definitions, mechanisms, examples, and response guidance for each major bad-faith rhetoric pattern. It supplements the quick-reference list in SKILL.md.

## 1. Motte-and-Bailey

### Definition
A rhetorical maneuver in which someone argues for a controversial, difficult-to-defend position (the "bailey") but, when challenged, retreats to a trivially defensible position (the "motte") — then resumes arguing the bailey as if no retreat occurred. Named by philosopher Nicholas Shackel after medieval castle architecture: the motte (a heavily fortified tower) is easy to defend; the bailey (a desirable courtyard) is what the occupant actually wants to hold.

### Mechanism
1. Speaker advances a strong, specific, or controversial claim (bailey).
2. Opponent challenges the claim with evidence or counterargument.
3. Speaker reframes their position as merely the weaker, obvious version (motte): "I never said X, I only said Y."
4. Opponent, having "misunderstood," looks aggressive or unfair.
5. Debate resets; speaker returns to bailey.

### Examples

**Political:** A politician argues for broadly dismantling regulatory agencies (bailey). When challenged: "I simply believe government should be efficient — surely we can agree on that?" (motte). After the controversy passes, the policy agenda continues.

**Workplace:** A manager argues that the team's work culture needs a complete overhaul (bailey). When met with pushback: "All I'm saying is we should try to improve communication" (motte). Later, restructuring proposals re-emerge.

**Relational:** "You're always prioritizing your friends over me" (bailey). When contested: "I just meant I'd like more quality time with you" (motte). The behavior pattern that prompted the accusation continues to be referenced.

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
A good-faith version: genuine clarification or course correction. If someone says "I think I overstated that — what I actually mean is X," without returning to the original claim, this is not motte-and-bailey. The key marker is **recidivism**: returning to the stronger claim after weathering the challenge.

### Suggested Responses
- Name the shift explicitly: "Earlier you said [bailey]. Now you're saying [motte]. Which position are we discussing?"
- Ask for commitment: "Can you clarify which version of this you're defending? I want to make sure I'm addressing what you actually mean."
- Decline to chase: "I'll respond to the stronger version of your claim, since that's what seemed most important to you earlier."

## 2. Moving Goalposts

### Definition
Changing the criteria for success, proof, or resolution after the original criteria have been satisfied or substantially met. The effect is to make it impossible to ever "win" the argument or prove the point.

### Mechanism
1. Party A sets a standard ("If you can show me X, I'll accept it").
2. Party B meets the standard.
3. Party A introduces a new, higher, or different standard.
4. Cycle repeats, exhausting Party B.

### Examples

**Political:** "Show me one peer-reviewed study." (Study provided.) "That's just one study; I need a consensus." (Consensus established.) "Scientific consensus has been wrong before."

**Workplace:** "Bring me three client testimonials and I'll consider the proposal." (Done.) "We'd also need to see a market analysis." (Done.) "The timing isn't right."

**Relational:** "If you really trusted me, you wouldn't check my phone." (Stops checking.) "You're just doing it because I asked. If you really trusted me, it wouldn't have been an issue in the first place."

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
Legitimate standard-raising happens when new information genuinely changes what evidence is needed, or when earlier criteria were under-specified. The distinction: good-faith revision is transparent about *why* the criteria changed and what the new standard is. Goal-post moving involves retroactive invalidation of already-met standards.

### Suggested Responses
- Document the original criteria in writing when possible.
- Name the pattern: "The standard we agreed on was X. You've now changed it to Y. Can you explain what changed?"
- Set a closing condition: "If I provide Y, what would that mean for the decision?" — get commitment before investing effort.

## 3. Whataboutism

### Definition
A deflection technique that responds to a criticism or concern by pointing to a separate, allegedly equivalent wrongdoing — effectively saying "but what about them?" It avoids accountability by redirecting attention and implying moral equivalence where none may exist.

### Mechanism
1. Party A raises a specific concern or criticism.
2. Party B responds not by addressing it, but by raising an unrelated or tangentially related criticism of Party A or a third party.
3. The original concern is never engaged.

### Examples

**Political:** "The administration's handling of the crisis has been inadequate." "What about what the previous administration did?"

**Workplace:** "Your report contained significant factual errors." "What about the Johnson team's presentation last quarter — that had errors too."

**Relational:** "You forgot our anniversary." "What about when you didn't come to my work event in February?"

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
Pointing to context or relevant comparators can be legitimate ("Is this criticism applied consistently?"). The distinction: good-faith comparison is offered *in addition to* engaging the original concern, not *instead of* it. Whataboutism functions as a full substitute for response.

### Suggested Responses
- Acknowledge, then redirect: "That may be worth discussing separately. Can we finish with the original question first?"
- Name the pattern: "I notice we've moved from [X] to [Y]. I want to stay with [X]."
- Separate the issues: "Two things can both be true. I'm asking about this specific situation."

## 4. Gish Gallop

### Definition
A technique named after creationist debater Duane Gish: flooding an opponent with a high volume of arguments — often weak, misleading, or tangential — too quickly for the opponent to refute each one adequately. The quantity creates the illusion of overwhelming evidence, and any failure to address every point is framed as conceding it.

### Mechanism
1. Speaker introduces 10–20 arguments in rapid succession.
2. Opponent can reasonably address 2–3 in the available time.
3. Speaker cites the unaddressed points as "proof" the opponent has no answer.

### Examples

**Political:** A 3-minute speech that contains 15 distinct factual claims, historical references, and policy objections — impossible to rebut in the allotted response time.

**Workplace:** An email listing 18 concerns about a colleague's project, each requiring separate research and documentation to address.

**Relational:** "You're never on time, you don't listen, you don't remember important dates, you're dismissive of my feelings, you prioritize work over us, and you never apologize first."

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
Raising multiple legitimate concerns at once is not inherently a gallop — complex situations have complex dimensions. The distinction: good-faith multi-point critique is organized, traceable, and allows time/space for response. A gallop is designed to overwhelm, not to be answered.

### Suggested Responses
- Triage openly: "There are many points here. Let me address what seems most central: [X]. Can we work through them one at a time?"
- Refuse the implicit frame: "I'm not able to address all of this at once — that's not a concession, it's a constraint of conversation."
- Ask for prioritization: "Of these concerns, which would you most like to focus on?"

## 5. Tone Policing

### Definition
Dismissing or invalidating an argument based on the *manner* of its expression rather than its *substance*. It redirects focus from what is being said to how it is being said, effectively making the speaker responsible for both making the point and managing the listener's comfort.

### Mechanism
1. Speaker raises a concern, potentially with visible emotion.
2. Respondent comments on the tone: "You're too emotional to discuss this rationally."
3. Original concern is sidelined while the speaker defends or moderates their tone.
4. The substantive issue is never addressed.

### Examples

**Political:** "Your anger about this policy is understandable, but until you can discuss it calmly, I'm not going to engage."

**Workplace:** "I'd be happy to consider your feedback when it's delivered professionally."

**Relational:** "I can't have a conversation with you when you're crying. Come back when you've calmed down."

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
There are real situations where communication style affects comprehension and safety. A good-faith version: "I want to hear you — can we take a short break so we can both think clearly?" The distinction: good-faith appeals to tone still honor the underlying content and commit to returning to it. Tone policing uses tone as a permanent veto.

### Suggested Responses
- Separate the issues: "I understand my tone may have been intense. The substance of what I said still stands. Can we address that?"
- Name the pattern: "I notice we're now discussing how I said it rather than what I said."
- Acknowledge, without capitulating: "I'll try to be clearer. But the concern itself hasn't changed."

## 6. Sealioning

### Definition
A pattern of persistent, seemingly civil, good-faith inquiry used to exhaust and delegitimize the target. The sealion professes only to want information or dialogue, but the questions are never-ending, the bar for satisfactory answers keeps shifting, and the framing implies the burden of proof falls entirely on the person being questioned.

### Mechanism
1. Target makes a claim or expresses a view.
2. Sealion responds with a question: "Can you prove that?"
3. Target answers.
4. Sealion asks a follow-up question, often ignoring the substance of the answer.
5. Eventually, any frustration from the target is framed as proof of unreasonableness.

### Examples

**Political/Online:** Responding to every factual assertion about climate data with "Can you link me to a primary source?" then "How do you know that methodology is valid?" indefinitely.

**Workplace:** After every proposal, demanding comprehensive documentation, then questioning every assumption in the documentation, without ever offering a counter-view.

**Relational:** "Can you explain why that upset you?" → (Explanation given.) → "But why would that specifically bother you?" → (Explained again.) → "I just don't understand what you want me to do differently."

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
Genuine inquiry is curious, engages with answers, and is self-limiting. A sealion's questions are recursive and never acknowledge the answers they receive. Good-faith questioners update their position; sealions don't.

### Suggested Responses
- Name the pattern: "I've answered this question several times in different ways. I'm not going to keep doing that."
- Set a limit: "I'm willing to explain this once more, clearly. After that, I'm going to assume you've heard me."
- Decline engagement: "I don't think further explaining is going to be useful here."

## 7. DARVO

### Definition
An acronym coined by psychologist Jennifer Freyd: **Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender**. A three-phase response to confrontation in which the accused denies the behavior, attacks the credibility or motives of the confronter, and repositions themselves as the real victim.

### Mechanism
1. **Deny**: "I never did that / said that / meant that."
2. **Attack**: "You're being paranoid / oversensitive / manipulative."
3. **Reverse Victim and Offender**: "I'm actually the one who's been hurt here. You're the one causing damage."

### Examples

**Political:** Confronted with documented behavior: "That's completely false." (Attack:) "This is a coordinated smear campaign." (Reverse:) "I've been victimized by these attacks for years."

**Workplace:** Confronted with a complaint about exclusionary behavior: "I never excluded anyone." "You're trying to make me look bad." "I've actually been the target of a hostile work environment."

**Relational:** "You said you'd be home by 7 and didn't text." → "I did text! You're imagining things." → "You're always attacking me for everything." → "I'm the one who's been feeling neglected in this relationship."

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
Genuine defense of a false accusation can involve denial and counter-assertion. The distinguishing markers of DARVO: the attack on credibility rather than the facts, and the rapid pivot to self-as-victim. A good-faith response addresses the specific concern; DARVO dismantles the confronter's standing to raise concerns.

### Suggested Responses
- Name the sequence without accusation: "I want to talk about [X]. I notice we're now talking about my motives and about your feelings of being hurt. Can we stay with the original concern first?"
- Decline the attack phase: "I'm not asking in order to attack you. I'm asking because [specific concern]."
- Validate before continuing: "I hear that you also feel hurt. I'd like to talk about that too. First, can we address [X]?"

## 9. Strategic Ambiguity

### Definition
Deliberately using vague, imprecise, or undefined language so that any specific interpretation of what was said can be denied afterward. The speaker can claim to have been misunderstood without ever having been clear in the first place.

### Mechanism
1. Speaker makes a statement open to multiple interpretations.
2. The listener acts on one interpretation.
3. Speaker reframes, claiming a different interpretation was always intended.

### Examples

**Political:** "We need to deal with the problem of illegal immigration" — vague enough to mean anything from enforcement to reform, allowing the speaker to claim any position after the fact.

**Workplace:** "Let's see how things develop before we make any decisions" — can mean "we'll promote you" or "we're letting you go" depending on outcome.

**Relational:** "I wasn't happy about that" — can mean mild displeasure or serious anger, giving plausible deniability on the intensity of the complaint.

### Suggested Responses
- Ask for specifics: "Can you be more precise about what you mean by [term]?"
- Restate your interpretation before acting: "I'm hearing this as X. Is that right?"
- Request commitment: "Can you put that in writing so we're working from the same understanding?"

## 10. Emotional Flooding

### Definition
Introducing intense emotional content — anger, distress, guilt, or crisis — that overwhelms the topic under discussion and makes rational engagement impossible. May be conscious or unconscious; the mechanism and effect are the same.

### Mechanism
1. A topic is raised for discussion.
2. The other party escalates emotionally, shifting focus to the emotional state itself.
3. The original topic is displaced; energy is consumed managing the emotional situation.

### Examples

**Political:** Town hall question about policy is met with passionate, tearful appeals to personal narrative that make substantive response feel callous.

**Workplace:** Performance review raises a concern; employee becomes visibly distressed, requiring reassurance, shifting the meeting away from the concern.

**Relational:** Partner raises a concern about finances; the other breaks down crying about past trauma, making further conversation feel cruel.

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
Genuine emotional responses are not inherently manipulative. The distinction: good-faith emotion doesn't require the other party to permanently abandon the topic; it may request a pause but commits to return. Flooding that is used recurrently to prevent any engagement with a specific topic is the pattern of concern.

### Suggested Responses
- Respond to the emotion first: "I can see this is really affecting you. Let's take a moment."
- Name the need to return: "I want to make sure we come back to [X] — is it okay if we do that later today?"
- Recognize protective function: In relational contexts, flooding may be protective shutdown. See `references/playbooks/relational-safety-responses.md`.

## 11. False Equivalence

### Definition
Treating two things as equally valid, equally harmful, or equally culpable when they differ significantly in kind, scale, frequency, or context. Creates the appearance of balance while obscuring meaningful difference.

### Mechanism
1. Party A raises a concern about a specific behavior.
2. Party B equates it with a different, often less serious, behavior: "Both sides do it."
3. The original asymmetry is flattened; accountability is diffused.

### Examples

**Political:** Comparing one party's rhetoric with another's as if scale, frequency, and consequence were identical.

**Workplace:** "Every department misses deadlines sometimes" — in response to a pattern of significant, costly delays in one department.

**Relational:** "You've done things that hurt me too" — without engaging whether the specific harm being discussed is analogous.

### Suggested Responses
- Interrogate the comparison: "In what way are these equivalent? Let's look at scale, frequency, and context."
- Acknowledge without collapsing: "I can accept that both things happened. They don't seem equivalent to me for these reasons..."
- Name the function: "Pointing to something else doesn't address what I'm asking about."

## 12. Appeal to Intent

### Definition
Using "I didn't mean it that way" as a complete and final defense against criticism of impact — placing intent above effect, and implicitly requiring the affected party to disregard their experience.

### Mechanism
1. Party A raises concern about the effect of Party B's words or actions.
2. Party B asserts good intent as a full defense: "I didn't mean it that way."
3. The impact on Party A is treated as moot, or Party A is implicitly blamed for misinterpreting.

### Examples

**Workplace:** "That comment felt dismissive." "I wasn't dismissing you at all — I'd never do that on purpose."

**Relational:** "When you said that in front of everyone, I felt humiliated." "I was just joking around — you know I love you."

**Political:** Confronted with the effect of a policy: "The intention was to help people."

### Distinguishing from Good Faith
Clarifying intent is legitimate and often important. The distinction: a good-faith appeal to intent *accompanies* acknowledgment of impact: "I didn't mean to hurt you — and I can see that you are hurt. I'm sorry for that effect." Appeal to intent as a *substitute* for acknowledging impact is the pattern of concern.

### Suggested Responses
- Separate intent from impact: "I believe you didn't mean to. And the effect was still [X]. Both can be true."
- Ask for acknowledgment: "I hear you about your intent. Can you also acknowledge how it landed?"

## 13. Selective Attention (Weakmanning)

### Definition
Engaging only with the weakest, most easily dismissed element of a multi-part argument — or with a poorly expressed version of a position — while ignoring the stronger points. The inverse of steelmanning.

### Mechanism
1. Party A makes a complex argument with multiple points, or makes one strong point expressed imperfectly.
2. Party B focuses only on the weakest or most awkward version.
3. By defeating the easy target, Party B implies the whole argument is refuted.

### Examples

**Political:** Responding to a complex policy proposal by fixating on one flawed example the proponent used, rather than the core argument.

**Workplace:** Dismissing a project proposal because the projected ROI figure was imprecise, without engaging the overall strategic rationale.

**Relational:** "You said I 'never' listen — that's not true, I listened yesterday." (Ignoring the broader concern about a pattern.)

### Suggested Responses
- Redirect: "The [weak point] was imprecise — let me restate that. The core argument is [X]. Can we discuss that?"
- Name the function: "I notice you've addressed [peripheral point] but not [main point]. What do you think about [main point]?"

## 14. Minimization

### Definition
Systematically downplaying the significance, severity, or legitimacy of a concern to discourage its pursuit. Often involves direct invalidation of the other person's emotional response or judgment.

### Mechanism
1. Party A raises a concern.
2. Party B reduces its standing: "You're overreacting." / "That's not that big a deal." / "You're too sensitive."
3. Party A must now defend the legitimacy of feeling the concern before the concern itself can be addressed.

### Examples

**Workplace:** "That comment in the meeting was really demeaning." "You're reading way too much into it — they didn't mean anything by it."

**Relational:** "I felt scared when you raised your voice." "I barely raised my voice — you always catastrophize."

**Political:** "People are genuinely suffering from this policy." "There are always people who struggle with change."

### Suggested Responses
- Hold the ground: "Whether it seems big to you or not, it was significant to me. Can we discuss what I actually experienced?"
- Name the pattern: "I've heard 'you're overreacting' several times. I'd like us to look at the actual concern."

## 15. Stonewalling

### Definition
Complete refusal to engage: shutting down conversation, going silent, leaving the room, or repeatedly deflecting without response.

### Critical Context: Bad Faith vs. Protective Shutdown

**Bad-faith stonewalling** is a power move: used to punish, to avoid accountability, or to exhaust the other party. It is deliberate and often accompanied by contempt or dismissiveness.

**Protective shutdown** is a dysregulation response: the nervous system becomes overwhelmed and the person literally cannot continue to engage productively. Often seen in people with anxious or avoidant attachment, trauma histories, or high emotional reactivity. Stonewalling in this mode is not a tactic — it's a physiological state.

John Gottman's research identifies stonewalling as a predictor of relationship breakdown — but also notes that the majority of stonewallers are flooded, not strategic.

### How to Distinguish
- Bad-faith stonewalling: often sudden, without explanation, and maintained even after time passes; may be tied to enforcement patterns (silence as punishment).
- Protective shutdown: often preceded by signs of physiological flooding (heart rate, agitation); person is often genuinely unable to process; they may want to engage but cannot.

### Suggested Responses
See `references/playbooks/relational-safety-responses.md` for full scripts.

- Offer time: "I can see you're not able to talk right now. Can we come back to this in an hour?"
- Name the need: "I need us to discuss this. Can we pick a time?"
- In bad-faith cases: set a consequence, not a demand.

## Additional Tactics

### Ad Hominem
Attacking the person making an argument rather than the argument itself. "Of course you'd say that" / "You're just biased" / "What do you know about it?"

**Response:** "Let's set aside who's saying it. Is the argument itself valid?"

### Burden-Shifting
Placing all responsibility for proof on the person raising a concern, often to a standard that is impossible to meet. "You can't prove I said that." "You'd have to show me definitive evidence."

**Response:** "I'm not in court — I'm raising a concern. I'd like to discuss it, not litigate it."

### False Dilemma
Presenting only two options when more exist. "Either you're with me or you're against me." "We can do it my way or we can fail."

**Response:** "Are those the only two options? What about [third option]?"

### Negging via Framing
Embedding a dismissive or belittling assumption inside a seemingly neutral question. "Even someone without experience in this field might want to consider..." / "Have you looked into whether there are simpler approaches?"

**Response:** "I notice that question assumes [X]. Can we set that aside?"
