# Decision-Making Under Uncertainty: How Experts Actually Decide and What That Means for Agent Design

## The Standard Model and Its Failure

The standard model of expert decision-making — the one that underlies most traditional AI 
approaches and most formal decision theory — assumes that decision-makers:

1. Generate a set of possible options
2. Evaluate each option against relevant criteria
3. Select the option with the highest expected value

This model has the virtue of being formally tractable. It is also substantially wrong as an 
account of how expert human practitioners actually make decisions in real time under uncertainty. 
Wilson's MELI methodology, drawing extensively on Klein (1999), exists precisely because 
the standard model cannot capture what experts actually do — and therefore cannot be used 
to extract, transfer, or replicate expert judgment.

Klein's research, which Wilson incorporates as the methodological backbone of the MELI, 
established the Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model: under time pressure and uncertainty, 
experts do not compare options. They recognize a situation as an instance of a familiar pattern 
and immediately generate a single response — which they then mentally simulate before acting. 
If the simulation reveals problems, they modify the response and simulate again. They do not 
compare multiple options against each other; they evaluate one option at a time until they find 
one that will work.

The MELI's question structure is built around this reality. Questions like "What caused you 
to reject the other options open to you at that point?" (p. 18) are not asking about a formal 
comparison process. They are asking the expert to reconstruct which patterns were activated, 
which were suppressed, and why — after the fact, through careful re-evocation.

## What Expert Decision-Making Actually Looks Like

Wilson's account of mediation expertise, synthesizing Honeyman (1988), Klein (1999), Baxter 
et al. (2008), and her own research, produces a picture of expert decision-making that has 
several distinctive features:

**1. Pattern recognition over deliberate calculation.**
Honeyman (1988) identified six elements of mediation skill: "investigation, empathy, persuasion, 
invention, distraction and substantive knowledge" (p. 8). What makes these skills expert rather 
than merely competent is not that the expert calculates more variables more carefully. It is that 
the expert has built up, through years of deliberate practice, the ability to rapidly read a situation 
and recognize which pattern it belongs to — and therefore which response repertoire is relevant.

The expert mediator "typically uses a repertoire of questions as a primary form of intervention. 
Questions tend to emerge dynamically to meet the demands of the conflict in which the mediator 
is engaged" (p. 15). The word "dynamically" is critical: the expert is not planning their questions 
in advance and executing a plan. They are responding to the unfolding situation with responses 
that their experience has associated with similar situations — a process that happens faster than 
deliberate calculation allows.

**2. Situation awareness as the primary cognitive work.**
Baxter, Prevou and Pruyt (2008: 6) identify the knowledge categories that characterize expertise, 
which Wilson incorporates into the MELI question framework: "diagnosing and predicting, 
situation awareness, perceptual skills, developing and knowing when to apply tricks of the 
trade, improvising, metacognition, recognizing anomalies, and compensating for system 
limitations" (p. 17).

Notice that situation awareness appears before any of the action-relevant categories. The expert's 
primary cognitive task is not deciding what to do — it is accurately reading what is happening. 
The MELI question "Which behavioural features did you expect to see at this point, and what 
did you actually observe?" (p. 18) is designed to surface this expectation-observation gap, 
which is where the most significant expert pattern recognition happens: not in confirming 
expected patterns but in detecting anomalies that signal the situation is not what it appeared to be.

**3. Improvisation within a theoretical framework.**
Expert mediators improvise — "questions tend to emerge dynamically" — but this improvisation 
is not arbitrary. It occurs within a framework of theory, experience, and accumulated 
pattern recognition. The MELI questions about theory are designed to surface this framework:
- "Did you find any particular conflict theory helpful in dealing with this situation?"
- "Have you been influenced by any particular mediation model?"
- "If so, what has proved useful about that model? Would you say that model is always useful 
  or appropriate?" (p. 20)

The last question is revealing: it asks not just what theory the expert uses but where that theory 
fails them — the boundary conditions of their theoretical framework. This is where the most 
sophisticated expert knowledge lives: not in the theory itself but in the practitioner's 
understanding of when the theory applies and when it doesn't.

**4. Metacognition as a component of real-time performance.**
The MELI question "Did you observe that party B's reactions were as you expected, or not? 
Did that reaction raise any issues for you?" (p. 19) is probing metacognitive monitoring 
during the incident: was the expert tracking their own predictions and updating when those 
predictions were violated? This is a hallmark of expert performance across domains — the 
ability to notice, in real time, that the situation is not conforming to expectation and to 
adjust accordingly, rather than continuing to execute a plan that the situation has made invalid.

**5. The rejection of options as evidence of expertise.**
Perhaps the most counterintuitive insight in Wilson's account is that what the expert did 
not do is as informative as what they did. The MELI question sequence that asks "What did 
you consider doing? What made you choose the intervention you chose? What caused you to 
reject the other options? Did you at any stage reconsider any of the options you had discarded?" 
(p. 18-19) is designed to surface the expert's rejection process.

Novices typically generate one or two options and select between them. Experts generate more 
options — because they have a larger repertoire of recognized patterns — but they also reject 
options faster and with more nuance. The expert's ability to quickly categorize situations as 
"the kind of thing where X works" but also "but not quite the standard version of that, because 
of Y factor" is exactly the pattern recognition that is hardest to articulate but most valuable 
to transfer.

## The Novice-Expert Contrast: Where Expertise Is Most Visible

Wilson's fourth pass in the MELI methodology is devoted entirely to the novice-expert 
comparison, and this is not accidental. The gap between what an expert does and what a novice 
would do is the most direct evidence of what expertise consists of.

Questions include: "What might a novice mediator have done when X happened? Which factors 
do you think someone with less experience might have missed? How could someone with less 
experience learn to practise effectively in the situation you've described?" (p. 20)

These questions force the expert to articulate the discriminative features of the situation — 
the cues that a novice would not notice or would misread, the patterns that only become 
visible after years of experience, the judgment calls that would look identical to a novice 
regardless of which choice the expert made but that the expert knows from experience to be 
consequential.

Crucially, the question "If you had been involved in this case 5 years previously, would 
your interventions have been the same?" (p. 20) asks the expert to reconstruct their own 
developmental trajectory — to remember what it was like not to know what they now know. 
This developmental perspective is rare and valuable: it traces the path from novice to expert, 
identifying the experiences, failures, and recalibrations that produced the current level of 
performance.

## The Role of Theory: Framework vs. Recipe

One of Wilson's most important contributions to understanding expert decision-making is her 
insistence on distinguishing between theory as framework and theory as recipe. Experts use 
theory, but they do not mechanically apply it. The MELI question "Would you say that model 
is always useful or appropriate?" (p. 20) is designed to surface exactly this: where does 
the model work and where does it fail?

Wilson notes that "despite questionable claims from various quarters that mediation as practised 
in this jurisdiction has an established theoretical basis, many practitioners do not engage with 
theory or seminal Alternative Dispute Resolution texts at all" (p. 6). There are two failure 
modes here:

**Failure mode 1: Theory ignorance.** The practitioner who does not engage with theory is 
making decisions on the basis of experience alone — which may be rich and accurate, but 
which cannot be examined, challenged, or transferred. Without theoretical vocabulary, the 
practitioner cannot even explain their own decisions in terms that allow others to evaluate 
or learn from them.

**Failure mode 2: Theory rigidity.** The practitioner who applies theory mechanically — 
as a recipe rather than a framework — loses the adaptive capacity that distinguishes expert 
from competent performance. Lang and Taylor (2000: 145), citing Schön, describe experts 
as "professionals wedded to a model of practice" — a reading Wilson explicitly rejects as 
a mischaracterization of genuine expertise.

The expert navigates between these failure modes by using theory as a lens for reading situations, 
while maintaining the metacognitive awareness to recognize when a situation does not fit the 
theory and requires improvisation. This is precisely what the MELI's question sequence 
about theory is designed to surface.

## Application to Agent Systems: Building Expert Decision-Making

**1. Design for pattern-first, calculation-second processing.**
Agents designed to evaluate all options before selecting will be systematically slower and 
less accurate on non-routine cases than agents that can rapidly pattern-match to familiar 
situations and then verify the match before acting. The RPD model — recognize, simulate, 
adjust — is a more accurate model of expert decision-making than classical decision theory 
for the kinds of complex, time-pressured situations where expertise matters most.

**2. Build and surface the rejection trace.**
The most valuable component of expert decision-making for knowledge transfer is the set 
of rejected alternatives and the reasons for their rejection. Agent systems should log not 
just the chosen action but the options that were generated and why each was rejected. This 
rejection trace is the primary source of evidence about what the agent "knows" in any 
meaningful sense.

**3. Model and monitor expectation violation.**
Expert situation awareness is fundamentally about detecting when observations violate 
expectations. Agent systems should explicitly model their expectations about how a situation 
will unfold and track where observations diverge from those expectations. The divergence 
signal is the primary input to the expert's real-time learning: "that reaction raised issues for 
me" is the moment where the expert's model of the situation is being updated.

**4. Maintain theory as interpretive framework, not fixed recipe.**
Agent systems should have access to theoretical frameworks (models of how the domain works) 
but should not treat those frameworks as invariant. The theoretical framework should be 
available for override when situation-specific features indicate that the standard model is 
inapplicable. Maintaining the boundary conditions of each theoretical model — the conditions 
under which it works and under which it fails — is as important as maintaining the model itself.

**5. Implement the novice-expert comparison as a diagnostic tool.**
In any domain where both expert and novice agents are available, running both on the same 
non-routine cases and carefully analyzing their differences — what the expert noticed that 
the novice missed, what the expert weighted differently, what the expert rejected that the 
novice selected — provides direct evidence of what the expert agent has learned. This 
comparative analysis should be a standard diagnostic tool in agent system evaluation.

**6. Develop metacognitive monitoring as a first-class capability.**
The expert's ability to notice, in real time, that a situation is not conforming to expectation 
is a metacognitive capability that most agent systems lack. Building explicit metacognitive 
monitoring — agents that track their own confidence, their own predictions, and their own 
prediction errors — into the agent architecture is a prerequisite for the kind of in-flight 
adaptation that characterizes expert performance.
