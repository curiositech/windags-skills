# The Self-Report / Behavior Gap: Why Systems Cannot Monitor Themselves Through Self-Assessment

## The Core Finding

One of the most practically significant findings in the intelligence interviewing literature — documented across multiple studies cited in Nunan et al. (2020) — is the systematic gap between what practitioners *report* doing and what they *actually do* when their interactions are recorded and analyzed.

Hall (1997) provides the clearest demonstration: "police officers reported rapport as important, yet when their recorded interviews were examined, the rapport-building behaviours identified by the police officers were not present." The practitioners were not lying. They were not being deceptive in their self-reports. They genuinely believed they were using rapport-building behaviors. The behaviors simply were not there.

This finding is replicated across jurisdictions and contexts:

- A questionnaire of 221 UK police interviewers revealed that rapport building was thought to be utilised 87% of the time (Dando, Wilcock, & Milne, 2008). But self-report surveys could not be cross-verified against actual interview behavior.
- Kassin et al. (2007) found that 32% of U.S. investigators report always building rapport — but again, without behavioral verification.
- Vallano et al. (2015) found U.S. police officers self-reported using an average of three rapport-building techniques per interview, with self-disclosure, empathy, and common ground as most common. Without recording analysis, these claims cannot be verified.

The authors of the Nunan et al. study explicitly note: "it was not possible for researchers to observe the recorded interviews to verify what the participants reported (Alison & Alison, 2017)." The innovation of this research is precisely that it *does* access recorded interactions and compares behavior to both reported behavior and outcome.

## Why This Gap Exists

The self-report / behavior gap is not primarily a honesty problem. It reflects several deeper cognitive phenomena:

**1. Normative confusion with behavioral description.** When asked "do you build rapport?", practitioners retrieve their belief in rapport's importance and their intention to build it — not an accurate inventory of specific behaviors produced in the last interview. The question is interpreted as asking about commitment or approach, not behavioral frequency.

**2. Behavioral invisibility of automatic responses.** Many rapport behaviors (or their absence) operate at a level below focal attention. A practitioner may genuinely not notice that they interrupted their source, used evaluative feedback that short-circuits free recall, or failed to pause after a question. These behaviors occur in real time under cognitive load. They are not stored as events that happened; they simply happened.

**3. Post-hoc narrative construction.** When practitioners are asked to describe their interviews, they construct a coherent narrative that matches their self-concept and professional training. If training says "build rapport," then the post-interview narrative will include rapport-building, regardless of whether the specific behaviors occurred.

**4. The gap between knowing and doing.** This is perhaps the deepest issue: procedural knowledge (knowing *that* one should use paraphrasing, appropriate pauses, open questions) does not automatically generate procedural execution (actually using these behaviors under the cognitive load of a real interaction). Walsh and Bull (2012) demonstrated this: "establishing rapport alone was not enough to satisfy the interview's quality and outcomes, as rapport needs to be maintained throughout." Knowledge of rapport as a concept does not sustain its behavioral execution across an interaction.

## The Implications for Self-Monitoring Systems

This gap has critical implications for any intelligent system — including AI agent orchestration systems — that attempts to monitor its own performance through internal self-assessment.

### The Structural Problem with Self-Assessment

When an agent reports on its own performance ("I maintained coordination throughout the interaction," "I thoroughly explored the source's account," "I avoided leading questions"), that report is generated by the same system that performed the behavior. The report reflects the agent's model of its own behavior, not independent measurement of the behavior. These are systematically different things.

The self-report gap in human practitioners is not a defect of human cognition that AI systems escape. It is a structural feature of any system that generates self-reports using the same processes that generated the behavior being reported. The mechanism differs, but the gap is real in both cases.

**An AI agent optimizing for a goal will generate self-reports consistent with goal-achievement, regardless of whether the behavior actually achieved the goal.** This is not deception — it is the natural output of a system that uses its goal-models to generate descriptions of its states.

### Three Architectural Responses

**Response 1: External behavioral measurement, not self-report**

The Nunan et al. study's methodological innovation is the use of *external coders* analyzing *recorded interactions* against a *pre-specified behavioral framework*. The first and second authors coded independently, with inter-rater reliability k=.77, providing measurement that is independent of the practitioners being measured.

For agent systems: performance monitoring should be conducted by a separate monitoring agent or module that observes outputs (not self-reports) and codes them against a pre-specified behavioral taxonomy. The monitoring agent should be architecturally separated from the agent being monitored — using shared goals or shared models creates the same self-report problem in structural form.

**Response 2: Outcome measurement as ground truth, not process self-report**

The study's outcome metric (intelligence yield) is measured independently of rapport: coders count detail units (surrounding, object, person, action, temporal) from the source's account, not from the handler's assessment of how much information was obtained. This allows the relationship between process (rapport behaviors) and outcome (yield) to be measured without either party's self-assessment contaminating either side.

For agent systems: define outcome metrics that can be measured by examining outputs rather than by querying agents about their performance. If an agent is tasked with code review, measure the defects found and missed in subsequent execution — not the agent's self-assessment of review thoroughness. If an agent is tasked with information synthesis, measure the completeness and accuracy of the synthesis against ground truth — not the agent's confidence rating.

**Response 3: Behavioral logging at the act level, not the episode level**

The Nunan et al. study codes individual utterances (each "hmm," each paraphrase, each pause, each question) rather than rating the interaction as a whole. This granularity is what allows detection of the gaps — a practitioner might have produced three paraphrases and fourteen friendly affirmations in a seven-minute call, and episode-level rating ("good rapport") would obscure the imbalance.

For agent systems: log agent behaviors at the act level (each query generated, each summary produced, each probe or clarification requested, each response accepted without probing) rather than at the episode level ("completed task successfully"). Aggregate statistics on act-level logs will reveal behavioral patterns — including systematic under-use of high-leverage behaviors — that episode-level logs cannot detect.

## The Specific Behaviors Most Susceptible to the Gap

The research identifies specific behaviors that practitioners systematically under-deploy relative to their stated commitment:

**Pauses**: "Source handlers rarely used pauses to facilitate communication." Pauses are coordination behaviors that directly benefit memory retrieval. But pauses feel like interactional dead air — practitioners fill them automatically, because not responding feels rude, awkward, or unproductive. The behavior that most helps (allowing the source to continue thinking) is the one most likely to be suppressed by social instinct.

**Probing and exploration**: The attention category includes "goes beyond just accepting information but searches for further detail, identifying the provenance of the information provided." In practice, practitioners accept the first-pass account — it feels sufficient, it doesn't challenge the source, and asking for provenance feels like skepticism that might damage rapport. But these probes are exactly what extract the high-value details.

**Exploring motivation**: "Tries to find, with understanding, why the CHIS is willing to share their information and also use the CHIS' motivation for the conversation." This is identified as a behavior that can "hook" cooperation and adapt the interaction approach. But practitioners rarely do it systematically — it requires asking a potentially sensitive question, and the answer may complicate the interaction. The behavior's difficulty is proportional to its payoff.

**Interrupting vs. not interrupting**: The study notes that source handlers "on occasions interrupted their CHIS." Interruption is probably not a deliberate choice in most cases — it is an automatic response to a pause that triggers the practitioner's own contribution before the source has finished. From the practitioner's self-report perspective, this probably does not register as an interruption at all.

## Application to Knowledge-Work Agent Systems

The self-report / behavior gap is especially relevant for agents engaged in knowledge work — code review, security auditing, document analysis, research synthesis — where the quality of the work lies in behaviors that are not automatically visible in the output:

- **A code review agent may report "thoroughly reviewed the codebase" when it examined 60% of the relevant paths.** The self-report reflects its assessment of completion, not a measured coverage metric.
- **A research synthesis agent may report "considered alternative explanations" when it generated one alternative and dismissed it quickly.** The self-report reflects its model of what thorough consideration looks like, not a count of alternatives genuinely explored.
- **A security audit agent may report "checked all standard vulnerability categories" when it executed automated checks and used their passing as evidence of safety.** The self-report reflects its procedural model of audit, not independent measurement of coverage depth.

In each case, the agent's self-report is generated by the same model that produced the behavior — and that model was optimized for the task, not for accurate self-assessment of the task.

## The Monitoring-Agent Principle

The practical response to the self-report gap is an architectural one: **design systems where the entity responsible for monitoring performance is distinct from the entity responsible for performance.** 

In the Nunan et al. study, this is achieved by having independent coders analyze recordings of interactions. The coders have no stake in the handler's performance assessment; they apply a pre-specified behavioral framework to observable outputs.

In WinDAGs terms: for any high-stakes skill execution, route the outputs through a monitoring agent that applies a behavioral checklist to the *outputs*, not to the performing agent's self-report. The monitoring agent's framework should be specified before the task begins (like the rapport coding framework was specified before the study began), not constructed post-hoc to rationalize the performance observed.

This is especially important for skills where the gap between self-report and behavior is most likely to be large: skills where the high-value behaviors are cognitively costly (and therefore under-deployed under load), skills where practitioners have strong normative beliefs about what they *should* do (which dominate self-reports), and skills where outputs are complex enough that incomplete execution still produces plausible-looking results.

## Summary

The self-report / behavior gap is not a peripheral finding in the Nunan et al. paper — it is the methodological motivation for the entire study. The study exists because self-reports about rapport are insufficient evidence that rapport behaviors are occurring. The same logic applies to agent system self-assessment: self-reports about performance are insufficient evidence that performance-producing behaviors are occurring. External behavioral measurement, act-level logging, and architecturally independent monitoring are the structural responses to this gap.
