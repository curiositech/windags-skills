# Automation Surprises: The Opacity Failure Mode in Agent Systems

## The Most Dangerous Way a System Can Fail

There is a failure mode more dangerous than a system that crashes. It is a system that continues to operate — that produces output, that completes its tasks, that appears to be functioning normally — while doing something other than what its operators believe it is doing.

This is the "automation surprise," identified and named by Sarter, Woods, and Billings in their foundational research (cited in Hoffman et al., 2002), and it represents one of the central warnings of the Cognitive Systems Engineering tradition. The automation surprise is not a malfunction. The system is doing exactly what it was designed to do. The problem is that the people (or agents, or systems) responsible for supervising it don't know what it was designed to do in the current situation — or they knew once but forgot, or they know in principle but have lost track of what situation the system currently believes it is in.

The result is a gap between the operator's mental model of system state and actual system state. This gap is invisible — there is no alarm, no error message, no indication that something has gone wrong — until the gap becomes large enough that its consequences are observable. By that point, significant damage has often already been done.

## Where Automation Surprises Come From

CSE research identifies several root causes, all of which map directly onto multi-agent system architectures:

**Mode confusion.** Complex automated systems operate in different modes — different states in which the same inputs produce different outputs, or in which different behaviors are active. When operators lose track of which mode the system is in, they lose the ability to predict system behavior. In aviation, this is responsible for a substantial category of accidents. Pilots issue commands that would be correct in Mode A, not realizing the aircraft has transitioned to Mode B, and receive responses they don't understand and can't interpret quickly enough.

In agent orchestration, mode confusion occurs when an agent's behavior changes based on internal state that isn't communicated to the orchestration layer or to other agents. The orchestrating agent sends a request that was appropriate for the agent's previous state, receives a response that was generated by a different state, and proceeds as if the response was what it expected.

**Silent assumption propagation.** An automated component makes an assumption — about the validity of its input, about the meaning of a symbol, about the current state of the environment — and encodes that assumption in its output without flagging it. The downstream system receives the output and treats it as unqualified data. The assumption propagates silently. Errors introduced by false assumptions compound at each step.

**Opacity of automated reasoning.** When a human operator asks "what is the system doing and why?", the answer should be accessible — either through display design, through explanation generation, or through explicit documentation of system behavior in the current state. When no such answer is accessible, the operator cannot maintain effective oversight. They cannot detect errors, cannot evaluate system performance, and cannot intervene appropriately.

**Vigilance degradation under automation.** When a system is performing well, its human (or AI) supervisors naturally reduce their vigilance — they pay less attention, check outputs less carefully, intervene less frequently. This is rational in the short run and dangerous in the long run. It means that when the system begins to diverge from correct behavior, the degradation is likely to go undetected for longer, allowing errors to compound before they are caught.

## The Vigilance Problem in Multi-Agent Coordination

The vigilance degradation effect is particularly acute in multi-agent systems where agents are coordinating without a central controller that understands everything that is happening. Hoffman et al. highlight this structural challenge: "the modern 'socio-technical' workplace is characterized by changing collaborative mixes of humans and machines." In such environments, no single agent — and no single human supervisor — has complete situational awareness. Awareness is distributed across the system.

This distribution of awareness is efficient and often necessary — no single node can hold all the relevant information. But it creates a systematic vulnerability: **shared situational awareness can fall below the level required for effective coordination without any individual agent recognizing that this has happened.**

Each agent knows what it knows. Each agent assumes the other agents know what they need to know. No agent is responsible for verifying that the overall picture is coherent. This is precisely the condition under which automation surprises emerge at the system level — not from the failure of any individual component, but from the accumulated gaps between what different components assume about each other.

## The Design Response: Transparency as Safety Infrastructure

The CSE response to automation surprises is not primarily about alarm design or monitoring dashboards, though those matter. The deeper response is **transparency of reasoning as a design requirement**, not a feature.

A system designed for transparency makes its current state, its current mode, its current assumptions, and the basis for its current outputs accessible to its supervisors — human or AI — in real time, in a form they can actually interpret. This is not about logging (though logging is necessary). Logging records what happened; transparency supports understanding in the moment when decisions are being made.

For AI agent systems, transparency requirements translate into concrete design choices:

**Agents should emit reasoning traces alongside outputs.** Not full chain-of-thought in every case — that can be expensive and overwhelming. But at decision points, at mode transitions, and whenever operating outside the normal envelope, agents should produce a compact representation of: what situation they recognized, what strategy they selected, what key assumptions they are making, and what confidence they have in their output.

**Orchestration layers should track and expose agent state, not just agent output.** An orchestrating agent that only knows what outputs downstream agents have produced cannot maintain effective oversight. It needs to know what state those agents are in — what they believe about the current situation, what strategies they are currently pursuing, what contingencies they are prepared for.

**Mode transitions should be explicit events in the coordination protocol.** When an agent changes its operating mode — when conditions have pushed it into a different strategy regime — this should be communicated as an explicit event to any agent or orchestration layer that depends on its behavior. Silent mode transitions are a primary source of coordination failures.

**Build anomaly detection into the coordination layer.** The orchestration layer should actively compare expected system behavior (based on stated strategies and assumptions) against observed system behavior (based on outputs and state updates). Discrepancies should trigger investigation, not be silently swallowed.

## Confidence Communication and Its Limits

A common response to the opacity problem is to require agents to communicate confidence scores alongside their outputs. This is useful — an agent that says "I'm 40% confident in this output" is providing information that an orchestrating agent can use. But CSE research warns against treating confidence scores as a complete solution for several reasons:

**Agents may not know when they don't know.** An agent operating outside its training distribution may produce confident-looking outputs because its confidence calibration was trained on in-distribution data. The situations where confidence is most likely to be wrong are exactly the situations where the agent is least likely to know it should be uncertain.

**Confidence is one-dimensional; uncertainty is not.** A single confidence score conflates many different types of uncertainty: uncertainty about the input, uncertainty about the interpretation, uncertainty about the relevant strategy, uncertainty about the consequences of the recommended action. These different types of uncertainty have different implications for downstream reasoning, and collapsing them into a single number loses that information.

**Confidence communication can reduce vigilance.** Paradoxically, providing confidence scores can make the automation surprise problem worse if it leads supervisors to reduce their independent scrutiny of high-confidence outputs. High confidence should trigger scrutiny, not relieve it, in novel or high-stakes situations.

The CSE research tradition points toward a richer form of uncertainty communication: not just "how confident am I?" but "what am I uncertain about, specifically, and what would resolve that uncertainty?" This structured uncertainty representation gives the downstream agent and the orchestration layer actionable information, not just a number.

## Practical Design Checklist for Automation Surprise Prevention

Drawing on CSE principles, the following design questions should be asked of every agent in a multi-agent system:

1. **Can the agent communicate its current mode or operating state?** Are mode transitions visible to the orchestration layer?

2. **Can the agent flag outputs that were produced under unusual conditions?** Does it know when it is operating outside its normal envelope?

3. **Does the agent communicate key assumptions embedded in its outputs?** Can downstream agents access and validate these assumptions?

4. **Is the agent's reasoning accessible to its supervisors?** At decision points, can the orchestration layer understand why the agent did what it did?

5. **Does the agent provide structured uncertainty information, not just a confidence score?** Does it identify what it is uncertain about and what would resolve the uncertainty?

6. **Are there mechanisms to detect when distributed situational awareness has fallen below the coordination threshold?** Is anyone — or any system — responsible for maintaining the overall coherent picture?

The goal is not perfect transparency — that is unachievable and would be overwhelming if achieved. The goal is **interpretable operation**: a system that behaves in ways its supervisors can understand, predict, and verify, even in degraded conditions, even in novel situations, even at the moments when understanding matters most.
