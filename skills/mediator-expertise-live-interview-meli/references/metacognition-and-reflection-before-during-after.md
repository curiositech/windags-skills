# Metacognition in Expert Practice: Reflection Before, During, and After Action

## The Schön Problem: What Reflective Practice Actually Requires

Donald Schön's concept of the "reflective practitioner" (1983) has become so widely 
adopted in professional education that it functions almost as an unchallengeable axiom: 
good practitioners reflect on their practice. Wilson's treatment of this concept is 
unusually critical, and her critique is substantive rather than dismissive. Understanding 
what she objects to, and why, reveals something important about the relationship between 
reflection and expertise.

Lang and Taylor (2000: 145), drawing on Schön, describe experts as "professionals wedded 
to a model of practice" who believe "that individuals can acquire a body of knowledge that 
yields an effective and competent solution when applied to a problem" (p. 10). On this 
reading, experts are the problem: they have solidified into rigidity, applying a known 
framework without genuine responsiveness to the specific situation.

Wilson's objection is not to reflection itself but to the artificial dichotomy this creates 
between experts and reflective practitioners. Her argument has two components:
