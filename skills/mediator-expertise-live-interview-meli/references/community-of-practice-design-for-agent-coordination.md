# Community of Practice Theory: What It Teaches About Agent Coordination Without Central Control

## What Is a Community of Practice?

Wilson defines a community of practice as "a group of individuals participating in a similar 
practice, and focused on practical solutions" (Baxter, Prevou and Pruyt, 2008: 4, cited p.2). 
The concept, developed more fully by Lave and Wenger and drawn on extensively in 
professional development literature, describes the informal, practice-centered social 
structures through which much professional knowledge actually lives and is transmitted.

A community of practice is explicitly not a formal organization. It does not have a hierarchy 
of command, a certified curriculum, or a centralized quality assurance mechanism. What it 
has is shared practice — the common activity that brings practitioners together — and 
the informal mechanisms through which knowledge circulates within that shared activity.

Wilson's interest in communities of practice arises from a practical observation: "Mediators 
often lack opportunities to discuss their work in depth within a group setting because of 
their relatively isolated role; where mediators do take part in group meetings, such gatherings 
are often taken up with discussions about business and regulatory matters. At a time of 
considerable change in the field, case discussion can be lost amongst other necessary, 
but less instructive, material" (p.2).

The community of practice *exists* — mediators do meet — but the conditions for genuine 
knowledge transfer within that community are frequently absent. The formal agenda 
crowds out the informal practice discussion. The political dynamics of "adversaries with 
commonality" create incentives for withholding rather than sharing. The absence of 
structured elicitation means that the tacit knowledge which should circulate stays tacit.

## The Revans-Vince Tension

Wilson introduces an important tension in the theory of learning communities through 
the juxtaposition of two frameworks:

**Revans (1982)**: Action learning involves "comrades in adversity" — people who 
share difficulties and learn together from those shared difficulties. The learning community 
is characterized by solidarity, mutual support, and shared commitment to improvement.

**Vince (2004)**: Some members of learning communities are better characterized as 
"adversaries with commonality" — people who share practice but also compete in ways 
that generate complex, difficult interpersonal dynamics. These dynamics can produce 
"learning inaction": the appearance of collaborative learning without the substance.

Wilson notes that Yeadon-Lee's reading of Vince "suggests that some aspects of 
interpersonal relationships experienced by people who attempt to learn from one another 
can sometimes be complex and difficult" (p.6-7).

These two frameworks are not mutually exclusive. Within any community of practice, 
both dynamics may operate simultaneously. Some members are genuine comrades in 
adversity; others are adversaries with commonality; and the same person may operate 
differently in different contexts. The point is that the Revansnian assumption — that 
shared difficulty automatically creates solidarity and productive learning — is naive. 
The political economy of the learning community must be explicitly managed.

## Learning Inaction as a Failure Mode

"Learning inaction" (Vince, 2008: 1) deserves sustained attention as a concept. It describes 
the situation where a group produces the *form* of learning activity (meetings, discussions, 
shared accounts of practice) without the *substance* (genuine knowledge transfer, honest 
acknowledgment of failure, updating of practice based on what is learned).

Learning inaction in communities of practice is typically produced by:

1. **Competition without trust**: When members compete directly for clients, referrals, 
   or status, sharing genuine expertise is a competitive disadvantage. The rational 
   strategy is to share enough to appear collaborative while protecting the knowledge 
   that actually distinguishes excellent from mediocre practice.

2. **Norm enforcement**: Professional communities develop norms about what can be 
   said. Admitting failure, acknowledging uncertainty, or questioning established practice 
   are often implicitly discouraged. The result is that the knowledge most needed — 
   about edge cases, failures, and difficult situations — is precisely what doesn't circulate.

3. **Status protection**: Practitioners with high status have the most to lose from candid 
   accounts of their practice. Senior practitioners who acknowledge uncertainty or describe 
   cases that went badly risk their reputation. The MELI's design (interviewing someone 
   who consented in advance, in a forum of peers, focused on a specific case rather than 
   general competence claims) is partly an attempt to reduce the status risk of genuine sharing.

4. **Institutional agenda crowding**: The formal business of the community (regulatory 
   compliance, administrative matters, training certification) expands to fill available 
   time, leaving no space for the informal practice discussion where knowledge actually 
   circulates.

The MELI is specifically designed to address learning inaction by creating a structured 
context in which genuine knowledge sharing is the explicit, consented purpose of the 
meeting, reducing the space for deflection, norm enforcement, and agenda crowding.

## Lederach and the Elicitive Turn

John Paul Lederach's work on elicitive training (1995) provides an important complementary 
framework. Lederach developed his elicitive approach in response to cross-cultural training 
experiences where North American conflict resolution frameworks were being taught to 
practitioners in other cultural contexts. He recognized that this approach was "subtly 
embedded with the unintended residue of imperialism [...] based on cultural assumptions 
which are essentially North American" (1995: 38, cited p.11).

The elicitive alternative draws on the knowledge that already exists within a community: 
"the existing knowledge and wisdom of those participating" (p.11). Rather than importing 
a framework and teaching it, elicitive training surfaces what the community already knows, 
makes it explicit, and creates conditions for it to be shared and refined.

Wilson argues that this approach is not just for cross-cultural settings: "there is no reason 
to restrict elicitive methods to settings specifically deemed cross-cultural; the professional 
development of mediators may be generally enhanced by a form of elicitive training" (p.11).

The principle generalizes: any community of practice contains significant expertise that 
is not yet explicit, not yet shared, and not yet recognized as expertise. Elicitive methods 
can surface this knowledge in ways that imported frameworks cannot.

## The Trust Architecture of Knowledge Sharing

Wilson emphasizes that "the unrehearsed nature of the MELI means that a degree of mutual 
trust is necessary between interviewer and interviewee" (p.15). This is not incidental — 
the trust relationship is what makes genuine elicitation possible.

"Exploring one's practice through a detailed interview process in the presence of peers 
is a somewhat risky venture, potentially endangering both 'face' and reputation" (p.7). 
The expert who agrees to a MELI is taking a real risk: they may reveal uncertainty, 
describe decisions that others disagree with, or show that their practice is less systematic 
than their professional identity implies.

The MELI's design elements that reduce this risk include:
- Consent and voluntary participation
- The norm that the purpose is educative rather than evaluative
- The explicit commitment that the interviewer will not check whether the account is 
  "orthodox" or compliant with a particular model
- The community-of-practice setting (peers, not supervisors or evaluators)
- The focus on a specific incident rather than general practice claims

Together, these create what Mason (2005) calls "safe uncertainty" — a context where it 
is acceptable not to know, to be uncertain, to have made imperfect decisions. This is the 
precondition for genuine knowledge sharing rather than performance of professional competence.

## Application to Intelligent Agent Systems

### Coordination Without Central Control

The community of practice model describes a form of coordination that is explicitly 
non-hierarchical. Members of a community of practice coordinate their practice not 
through a central authority that specifies what each member should do, but through 
shared exposure to each other's practice, shared understanding of what constitutes 
good work, and informal mechanisms for calibrating individual practice against community 
standards.

This is directly relevant to multi-agent system design. In WinDAGs and similar 
orchestration systems, coordination between agents can take two forms:

1. **Hierarchical**: A central orchestrator routes tasks to specialized agents and 
   integrates their outputs
2. **Community-of-practice style**: Agents share information about their situations, 
   approaches, and outcomes, and adjust their behavior in response to what they learn 
   from each other

The community-of-practice model suggests that agent systems may be more robust 
and adaptable when agents have genuine visibility into each other's reasoning processes 
— not just their outputs — and mechanisms for learning from each other's successes and 
failures.

### Learning Inaction in Agent Systems

Agent systems have their own versions of learning inaction:

**Confidence inflation**: Agents that are evaluated on confident, fluent outputs have 
incentives to produce confident outputs even when genuinely uncertain. The "political 
economy" of reward models may systematically disadvantage honest uncertainty 
expression.

**Skill silo effects**: Specialized agents optimized for their specific sub-task may not 
share information that would benefit the system as a whole, especially if their reward 
signal is based on sub-task performance rather than system-level outcomes.

**Documentation theater**: When agents are required to produce explanations, they 
will produce explanations. But as the theory-in-use / theory-espoused analysis shows, 
those explanations may be performances of reasoning rather than accurate accounts 
of the computational process that drove the decision.

The MELI framework suggests that agent system designers should:
- Design evaluation that rewards genuine system-level performance over sub-task 
  performance or confident output tone
- Create mechanisms for agents to express and record uncertainty rather than just 
  produce outputs
- Build structured "knowledge transfer" protocols between agent instances — the 
  equivalent of the four-pass elicitation process applied to capturing what a 
  well-performing agent is actually doing differently from a baseline

### Elicitive Knowledge Base Construction

Lederach's elicitive principle applied to agent knowledge systems: rather than building 
knowledge bases entirely from imported canonical sources (textbooks, established 
practitioner documentation, research papers), invest in eliciting knowledge from the 
practitioners who will use the system about what they actually need in their specific 
context.

This is both an argument for user research and a methodological claim about how that 
research should be conducted. Standard user research methods (surveys, focus groups, 
documentation review) are subject to the same theory-espoused problem as expert 
interviews. The MELI approach suggests that genuine elicitation requires:
- Anchoring in specific cases, not general descriptions
- Four-pass deepening rather than single-pass questionnaires
- Non-directive questioning that doesn't impose the researcher's framework
- Attention to anomalies and difficult cases rather than just typical ones

### Trust Architecture in Multi-Agent Systems

The trust conditions that make genuine knowledge sharing possible in communities of 
practice — consent, voluntary participation, clear purpose, freedom from evaluation, 
peer framing — have analogs in the design of information-sharing protocols between agents.

Agents that share information about their limitations, uncertainties, and decision-making 
processes contribute to system-level intelligence but may individually perform worse on 
narrow evaluations (because they are more honest about uncertainty). A system that 
incentivizes only output quality will systematically suppress the kind of inter-agent 
transparency that would improve overall system robustness.

The design implication: system-level evaluation metrics matter as much as component-level 
metrics. And the incentive architecture of the system should reward honest uncertainty 
expression and knowledge sharing, not just confident performance.
