## BOOK IDENTITY

**Title**: Mediator Expertise Live Interview (MELI)
**Author**: Barbara Wilson, PhD
**Core Question**: How can the tacit, embodied knowledge of expert practitioners be systematically elicited, made visible, and transferred to others — especially when that knowledge resists explicit articulation and formal codification?
**Irreplaceable Contribution**: Wilson's central insight is that expert knowledge is not primarily stored in textbooks, training curricula, or even in the experts' own conscious narratives about what they do — it lives in the unrehearsed, spontaneous account of a specific difficult moment. Her MELI methodology is a structured protocol for making tacit expertise legible without destroying its authenticity. No other work combines the cognitive task analysis tradition (Klein, Hoffman), the elicitive training tradition (Lederach), the reflective practice critique (Greenwood on Schön), and the community-of-practice framework into a single practical intervention designed to harvest expert judgment under conditions that closely replicate real performance. The book is also distinctive in its unflinching attention to the political economy of expertise: who gets called an expert, why credential does not equal competence, and how competitive dynamics within professional communities create structural barriers to genuine knowledge transfer.

---

## KEY IDEAS (3-5 sentences each)

1. **Tacit expertise is not directly accessible through self-report, but it can be approached through structured situational re-evocation.** The canonical finding from Nisbett and Wilson (1977) — that people have "little or no direct access to higher order cognitive processes" — is partially rebutted by Petitmengen et al. (2013), who show that specific elicitive protocols can recover statistically significant recall of decision processes. The implication is that expertise is not permanently locked away: it requires the right key, which is not "what do you usually do?" but "what did you do, right then, and why?" This has profound implications for any system trying to learn from or replicate expert performance.

2. **Expertise is domain-specific, earned through deliberate practice, and inseparable from the relationship between practitioner and context.** Wilson insists, drawing on Ericsson, Farrington-Darby, and her own research, that expertise cannot be borrowed from adjacent domains, cannot be certified by credentials alone, and typically requires approximately ten years of deliberate, reflective practice. Critically, expertise develops through "complex interactions between person and place" — it is not a portable property of an individual but an emergent property of a practitioner-in-context. This means any system attempting to deploy expertise must also model the context in which that expertise was formed.

3. **The gap between competence and expertise is maintained by structural barriers to knowledge transfer, including competition, political dynamics within professional communities, and the retirement of senior practitioners without capture of their knowledge.** Wilson documents how the very conditions that should facilitate learning — CPD gatherings, peer communities — are often contaminated by competitive incentives that produce "learning inaction" (Vince, 2008). Senior mediators retire and their accumulated wisdom simply disappears. The MELI is explicitly designed as a countermeasure: a methodology for knowledge extraction before it is lost, using a structured interview that bypasses the political incentives to withhold.

4. **The distinction between expert and reflective practitioner is artificial; genuine expertise requires both pre-action deliberation and in-action improvisation, not just post-hoc reflection.** Wilson challenges the Schönian model on two fronts: Greenwood's critique that Schön ignores pre-action reflection, and Wilson's own observation that experts who have put in thousands of hours are not the complacent "model appliers" Schön caricatures — they are committed to lifelong learning and capable of spontaneous, theoretically grounded improvisation. The MELI captures both dimensions by asking not just "what happened?" but "what did you consider, reject, and why — in the moment?"

5. **The elicitive methodology — drawing knowledge out of those already in the room rather than importing it from external authorities — is both epistemologically superior and politically necessary.** Wilson draws on Lederach's insight that training embedded with unexamined cultural assumptions (in his case, North American ADR models exported globally) produces practitioners who cannot perform in their actual context. The MELI insists on locally embedded experts, interviewed within their own community of practice, producing knowledge that is directly applicable to the conditions those present actually face. This is a model for any knowledge transfer system that must operate in contexts where the "standard" corpus of knowledge does not capture actual performance demands.

# Tacit Expertise Elicitation: How to Extract What Experts Know But Cannot Easily Tell You

## The Core Problem: Expertise Lives Below the Waterline

When an expert practitioner performs at a high level, most of what makes that performance 
expert is invisible — invisible to observers, and often invisible to the expert themselves. This 
is not evasiveness or modesty. It is a structural feature of how expertise is stored and deployed.

The canonical scientific statement of this problem comes from Nisbett and Wilson (1977: 231), 
who concluded that people have "little or no direct access to higher order cognitive processes." 
Their research demonstrated that when asked to explain their decisions, people consistently 
confabulate: they produce plausible-sounding explanations that bear little relationship to the 
actual cognitive processes that generated the decision. This is not lying. It is the natural result 
of expertise becoming proceduralized — the deliberate, effortful processing that characterized 
early learning has been compiled into fast, automatic pattern-recognition that no longer produces 
explicit intermediate reasoning steps available to conscious inspection.

Subsequent research by Johansson, Hall, Sikström and Olsson (2005) confirmed similar 
findings under the rubric of "choice blindness." People not only fail to accurately report their 
cognitive processes; they fail to even notice when their choices have been switched.

The implication for any system trying to learn from experts — or any agent trying to replicate 
expert judgment — is severe: you cannot simply ask an expert what they do and expect a 
reliable answer. The expert will tell you what they believe they do, which is a theory about 
their practice, not their practice itself. Wilson (p. 13) identifies this as a fundamental limitation 
of conventional training approaches: experts become trainers and teach their theories of their 
practice, which diverge systematically from the actual practice that produced their expertise.

## The Partial Solution: Situational Re-Evocation

The good news, discovered by Petitmengen, Remillieux, Cahour and Carter-Thomas (2013), is 
that the inaccessibility of cognitive processes is not absolute. Their research found that 
"statistically significant recall" of decision processes becomes possible when subjects are guided 
through "specific acts consisting in evoking the process and directing one's attention towards its 
different dimensions" (p. 667). The key is not asking "what do you think about X?" or "what 
do you usually do in situation Y?" — it is returning the expert, through structured questioning, 
to the specific sensory and cognitive moment of the original decision.

This is what Wilson's MELI methodology is designed to accomplish. Rather than asking an 
expert mediator to deliver a prepared presentation about their practice (which produces their 
theory of their practice), the MELI asks them, without preparation, to identify a specific 
difficult incident and then be walked through it in real time by a skilled interviewer using 
probing questions designed to re-evoke, not merely recall.

The distinction is crucial:
- **Recall**: "Tell me about a time you handled a difficult situation." → Produces theory.
- **Re-evocation**: "At the exact moment when X happened, what were you observing? What 
  did you consider doing? What made you reject the other options?" → Approaches actual process.

## The Four-Pass Protocol: A Structured Descent into Expertise

Wilson adapts Klein's (1999: 189-196) four-pass methodology into a staged process that moves 
progressively from surface narrative to deep cognitive access:

**Pass 1: Incident Identification**
The interviewer asks the expert to identify a tough case or non-routine event where their skills 
"made a difference." This framing is important: it signals that the interest is in situations where 
expertise was actually exercised, not routine cases that could be handled by anyone. It also 
signals that the goal is specificity, not generality. The interviewer is "looking to gain information 
about what the mediator thought and did in a specific situation, rather than a description of what 
s/he would usually do in similar circumstances" (p. 17). The natural tendency to fall back on 
generalities — "I always... I never..." — must be actively resisted.

**Pass 2: Full Narrative**
The expert tells the story in full, with minimal interruption. The interviewer listens for the 
skeleton of events, identifying key decision points, moments of uncertainty, and junctions where 
the situation could have gone differently. This pass establishes the temporal structure of the 
incident and allows both interviewer and audience to develop a shared understanding of the case.

**Pass 3: Cognitive Excavation**
This is the heart of the methodology. The interviewer returns to the key decision points 
identified in pass 2 and probes the cognitive processes that generated each decision. Questions 
Wilson provides include (pp. 18-19):

- "What were your initial reactions?"
- "Which behavioural features did you expect to see at this point, and what did you actually observe?"
- "What did you consider doing, and what made you choose the intervention you chose?"
- "What caused you to reject the other options open to you at that point?"
- "How did you know what to do at that moment?"
- "What would you have done if X hadn't happened?"
- "Is there anything that you observed which made you change course?"

The structure of these questions is worth analyzing carefully for agent system design purposes:

1. They separate **expectation** from **observation** ("which features did you expect... what 
   did you actually observe?") — this surfaces the expert's mental model and how they update it 
   in response to violations of expectation.
2. They surface **rejected alternatives** ("what caused you to reject the other options?") — 
   this is where much expert knowledge lives: in what was considered and discarded, not just 
   what was done.
3. They probe **counterfactuals** ("what would you have done if X hadn't happened?") — 
   this reveals the conditional structure of expert reasoning and the branching possibilities the 
   expert held in mind simultaneously.
4. They ask about **epistemic confidence** ("how did you know what to do?") — this 
   distinguishes expert intuition from luck and surfaces the cues that triggered pattern recognition.

**Pass 4: Comparative Novice-Expert Analysis**
The final pass asks the expert to compare their own response to what a novice might have done. 
Questions include: "What might a novice mediator have done when X happened?" and "Which 
factors do you think someone with less experience might have missed?" This comparative 
framing is powerful because it forces the expert to articulate the discriminative features of their 
expertise — what they saw that the novice wouldn't, what they weighted differently, what they 
knew to worry about that the novice wouldn't have thought to worry about.

## The No-Preparation Requirement: Why Authenticity Beats Polish

Wilson's requirement that the interviewee not prepare in advance is counterintuitive and 
controversial. Most training formats ask presenters to prepare carefully — case studies are 
written up, narratives are refined, lessons are distilled. Why would Wilson deliberately 
undermine this?

Because preparation destroys authenticity in a particular way: it converts experience into 
theory. "Any mediator who agrees to being interviewed at a training event is likely to expect 
questions about their practice, and practitioners asked to speak at mediator gatherings tend to 
mull over and refine the telling of their war stories" (p. 14). The refined war story is a 
retrospective narrative shaped by the teller's current theory of their practice, their sense of what 
lessons the audience needs, and their professional identity management. It is not a window into 
the actual decision process.

The unprepared account, by contrast, retains the mess and contingency of actual expertise. 
"Critical incidents, even if brief, are often chaotic and messy" (p. 18). The expert has to 
reconstruct what they actually did, in real time, under the gentle pressure of the interviewer's 
questions. This reconstruction is more likely to surface the actual cognitive process — including 
the uncertainty, the rejected options, the mid-course corrections — than a polished war story 
could.

Wilson notes that the no-preparation requirement also means "the interview can be as 
extemporaneous as possible, and thus bear some resemblance to witnessing that mediator in 
action" (p. 14). The MELI is a partial simulation of the conditions under which expertise is 
deployed: the expert must respond spontaneously to unexpected probes, just as they respond 
spontaneously to unexpected developments in a live mediation.

## Boundary Conditions: When This Protocol Does and Does Not Work

**When it works well:**
- When the interviewer is genuinely curious and non-evaluative — not testing the expert against 
  a model, but genuinely seeking to understand what the expert actually did and thought.
- When the expert has sufficient self-awareness and language to articulate something like their 
  cognitive process when prompted.
- When the incident chosen has genuine complexity — moments where the expert's pattern 
  recognition and judgment were actually taxed.
- When there is sufficient trust between interviewer and interviewee to allow genuine 
  vulnerability.

**When it is limited or fails:**
- When the interviewer cannot resist evaluating the expert's account against a preferred model 
  of practice. Wilson is explicit: "the purpose of the MELI is not for the interviewer to test 
  hypotheses, nor to check whether the interviewee's account complies with a particular 
  mediation model, ideology, or is otherwise 'orthodox'" (p. 16). Judgment closes down 
  authentic disclosure.
- When the expert's knowledge is so deeply compiled that even re-evocation cannot surface 
  it — some skills become truly pre-verbal.
- When the incident chosen is routine rather than challenging — expertise is only visible 
  under conditions that require it.
- When the competitive dynamics of the professional community make genuine disclosure 
  risky for the expert.

## Application to Agent Systems: Designing Expertise Extraction Mechanisms

For AI agent orchestration systems, this document teaches several concrete design principles:

**1. Separate "what agents say they do" from "what agents actually do."**
Agents that explain their own reasoning are producing theories of their processing, not 
necessarily accurate accounts of their processing. Logging actual decision sequences — what 
options were generated, what was scored highest, what was rejected and why — provides access 
to the actual process. This is the equivalent of pass 3 in the MELI: don't ask the agent to 
summarize what it did; examine the actual trace of what it considered and discarded.

**2. Design for counterfactual interrogation.**
Systems should be able to answer not just "what did this agent do?" but "what would this agent 
have done if condition X had been different?" This requires storing not just the chosen path but 
the evaluated alternatives and the conditions that ranked them. This is directly analogous to the 
MELI's questions about rejected options and counterfactuals.

**3. Use the novice-expert comparison as a diagnostic.**
When a highly capable agent and a baseline agent both encounter the same situation, the 
differences in their response — what the expert agent noticed that the novice didn't, what it 
weighted differently — constitute a partial articulation of what the expert agent "knows." This 
comparative analysis is the computational equivalent of pass 4.

**4. Elicit expertise through structured re-run, not summary.**
Rather than asking a high-performing agent to produce a summary of its approach, replay the 
sequence of its decisions on a challenging case with structured probing at each key decision 
point. What features of the input drove the decision? What alternatives were scored? What 
would have changed the outcome? This is the agentic equivalent of the MELI's four-pass 
protocol applied to an agent's decision trace.

**5. Preserve the mess.**
Expert knowledge includes knowledge of how to navigate ambiguity, contradiction, and 
incomplete information. Any extraction methodology that produces only clean, resolved 
accounts loses the most valuable content — the reasoning under uncertainty that distinguishes 
expertise from mere competence.

# Expertise Is Not Experience: The Deliberate Practice Gap and Its Implications for Agent Development

## The Seductive Equivalence: Why We Confuse Experience with Expertise

One of the most consequential errors in any knowledge-intensive field — and one that Wilson 
documents with careful attention to its professional and political consequences — is the 
conflation of experience with expertise. This error is not merely academic. It produces 
practitioners who believe they are experts because they have been practicing for years, 
institutions that treat tenure as a proxy for competence, and training systems that mistake 
exposure for learning.

Wilson's opening pages establish the etymological root of the problem and its solution 
simultaneously. The word "expert" derives from the Latin *experiri*, meaning "try" or "test." 
This etymology implies that expertise "cannot constitute a mass of experience alone, but that 
there must be in all experts the ability to accept that flawed theories must be rejected and 
correct theories accepted, regardless of tribal considerations" (p. 1). Experience accumulates 
automatically; expertise requires something additional — a recursive relationship with one's 
own practice that includes the willingness to discover that what one has been doing is wrong.

This is the deliberate practice insight, drawn primarily from Ericsson (2006a: 689): expert 
practice "typically takes ten years to achieve, regardless of the field." But the ten years is not 
a sufficient condition; it is a necessary one. The ten years must be years of deliberate, 
reflective engagement with practice — practice that includes feedback, adjustment, theory 
testing, and the hard cognitive work of converting experience into understanding.

## The Three Populations: Non-Experts, Competents, and Experts

Wilson's framework implicitly distinguishes three populations that are often treated as a single 
continuum:

**Non-experts masquerading as experts:**
"It is therefore probable that at least some practitioners promoting themselves as expert 
mediators do not actually possess mediator expertise" (p. 3). This is not a minor problem of 
self-promotion. When training systems, referral networks, and quality assurance mechanisms 
cannot reliably distinguish these practitioners from genuine experts, the entire ecosystem 
degrades. Clients receive mediocre service. Genuine experts face unfair competition. And 
the field's knowledge base stagnates because it is built partly on the theories of practitioners 
who have not actually achieved the performance level their theories claim to describe.

**Competent practitioners who underrate themselves:**
Wilson observes the opposite problem with equal attention: "some who are highly skilled may 
be reluctant to describe themselves as experts. Practitioners' reticence to admit expertise may 
mean that they are underrating their highly developed abilities and knowledge" (p. 10-11). 
This population is crucial for knowledge transfer systems because they hold genuine expertise 
but do not present themselves as knowledge sources. They are unlikely to write books, speak 
at conferences, or seek appointments as trainers. Their knowledge is at risk of being lost when 
they retire.

**Genuine experts:**
These are practitioners marked by "a commitment to lifelong learning" (Adler, 2003, cited 
p. 10) who "seldom are complacent." Wilson explicitly rejects the reading of Schön that frames 
experts as practitioners "wedded to a model" who apply it mechanically: "making distinctions 
between experts and reflective practitioners is an artificial construct" (p. 10). Genuine 
expertise includes both deep domain knowledge and the metacognitive capacity to recognize 
when that knowledge is insufficient.

## Why Credentials Do Not Certify Expertise

Wilson's account of mediation training in England and Wales is a case study in institutional 
credentialing that systematically fails to produce expertise — and may not even aim to. 
Family mediation training courses "typically total around forty hours" (p. 4-5) spread over 
eight to ten days. "Course content is largely based on skills and substantive knowledge, with 
little attention to conflict theory. Internships or other situated learning opportunities are few 
and far between" (p. 5).

The problem is not merely brevity. It is the wrong model of what expertise is and how it 
develops. Skills-based training can produce competence — the ability to execute specific 
behaviors in familiar situations. It cannot produce expertise, which includes:

- The ability to recognize when a situation is non-routine and requires deviation from 
  standard approaches
- The judgment to improvise effectively when standard approaches fail
- The pattern recognition that allows rapid, accurate assessment of complex situations
- The metacognitive awareness to know what one does not know

"Being a mediation trainer or CPD provider does not make someone an expert mediator, nor 
is expertise necessarily evidenced by qualifications which are only skills-based" (p. 6). This 
is a radical claim in the context of a credentialing system that treats qualifications as 
evidence of expertise. Wilson is arguing that the measurement is wrong: we are measuring 
the wrong thing and calling the measurement expertise.

The disconnect she identifies between training and expertise has a structural consequence: 
"unless senior mediators are represented in the construction and delivery of such external 
training and accreditation, there will be no automatic connection between the transfer of 
mediator expertise and any training courses which might be deemed approved" (p. 6). The 
people who certify expertise are not necessarily the people who possess it.

## The Domain Specificity Problem

A second major source of false expertise claims is the failure to recognize that expertise is 
domain-specific. Trained mediators "may not distinguish clearly — if at all — between their 
experience as a mediator and their achievements in their discipline of origin (typically law, 
psychology, mental health, social science and so forth). As a result, their mediation skills may 
be viewed simply as adjuncts to their primary professions" (p. 3).

Domain specificity (Farrington-Darby and Wilson, 2006: 18) means that expertise in law does 
not transfer to expertise in mediation, even if mediation involves some legal knowledge. The 
pattern recognition that a lawyer has built up — for identifying legally relevant facts, for 
structuring arguments, for reading adversarial dynamics — is not the same pattern recognition 
that an expert mediator needs. In fact, some of it may actively interfere: the adversarial 
orientation of legal practice may be precisely the orientation that a mediator needs to unlearn.

This is one reason why Honeyman's (1988) taxonomy of mediation skills — "investigation, 
empathy, persuasion, invention, distraction and substantive knowledge" (p. 8) — is significant: 
it maps the domain. An expert in one of these dimensions may be a novice in another. And 
crucially, the substantive knowledge component (the most legible, credentialable dimension) 
may be the least important factor in distinguishing expert from competent practitioners.

## The Person-Place Interaction: Expertise as Emergent Property

Perhaps Wilson's most sophisticated claim about the nature of expertise is that it is not a 
portable property of individuals but an emergent property of practitioners in context. Expertise 
is "the product of complex interactions between person and place" (Farrington-Darby and 
Wilson, 2006: 20, cited p. 11). This means:

1. An expert mediator in a family law context may perform very differently when transplanted 
   to a commercial context — not because their skills are absent, but because their pattern 
   recognition is calibrated to cues that are domain-specific.

2. The relationship between mediator and parties is itself a component of expertise, not merely 
   a vehicle for its deployment. Lambert and Barley's research on therapeutic alliance 
   (2001: 357-61) establishes that the relationship "is the context in which specific techniques 
   exert their influence, and... a primary curative component of therapy" (p. 9). Similarly, 
   the mediator's ability to form "optimum working relationships with the parties" may be 
   "inseparable from his or her expertise" (p. 9).

3. Some components of expert performance — "warmth, authenticity, and caring" (Baitar et al., 
   2012: 467) — may not be susceptible to development through training at all. They may be 
   personality characteristics that either exist in a practitioner or don't, and that training 
   can at best express or at worst suppress.

This last point leads to Benjamin's (2001) concept of "natural mediators" — practitioners who 
possess innate tendencies toward the qualities that mediation expertise requires. Wilson takes 
this seriously without treating it as deterministic: the natural mediator still requires deliberate 
practice and theoretical grounding. But the implication for any selection or development system 
is significant: identifying and selecting for the person-characteristics that enable expertise 
development may be as important as designing training that develops those characteristics.

## Application to Agent Systems: What This Teaches About Building Expert Agents

**1. Distinguish performance from accumulated computation.**
An agent that has processed ten million examples of a task has not necessarily developed 
expertise in that task. Expertise requires feedback loops that generate genuine learning — 
situations where performance can be measured, errors identified, and adjustments made. 
An agent that has processed ten million examples without this feedback loop has accumulated 
exposure, not expertise.

**2. Domain specificity is a feature, not a bug.**
Agent systems that attempt to apply general-purpose reasoning to domain-specific problems 
will systematically underperform domain-expert agents. The pattern recognition that constitutes 
domain expertise is calibrated to the statistical structure of that domain — the cues that are 
actually predictive, the anomalies that signal trouble, the sequences that tend toward resolution. 
This calibration cannot be approximated by general intelligence alone.

**3. Credential-equivalent metrics are unreliable proxies for expert performance.**
An agent that achieves high scores on benchmark tasks has demonstrated competence on those 
benchmarks. It may or may not have developed the expert judgment that allows it to handle 
non-routine cases, recognize when standard approaches are inappropriate, or improvise 
effectively in novel situations. Benchmark performance is the computational equivalent of 
completing a skills-based training course: necessary but not sufficient for expertise.

**4. The context-expertise interaction must be modeled explicitly.**
Deploying a specialized agent in a context significantly different from its training context 
risks systematic degradation — not because the agent is less capable, but because its pattern 
recognition is calibrated to a different statistical environment. This is analogous to Wilson's 
observation that expertise is a person-place interaction, not a portable individual property.

**5. Some expert capabilities may not be learnable.**
Just as Wilson acknowledges that "warmth, authenticity, and caring" may be personality 
characteristics that training cannot install, some expert agent capabilities may not be 
achievable through additional training on the same kind of data. Architectural choices — the 
equivalent of "natural mediator" selection — may matter as much as training volume.

**6. Deliberate practice requires adversarial feedback.**
The ten-year / deliberate practice finding translates directly: agent expertise requires not just 
exposure to cases but structured feedback that identifies errors, challenges comfortable 
patterns, and forces calibration against actual outcomes. Systems that provide only positive 
reinforcement on easy cases, or that shield agents from genuinely difficult situations where 
failure is possible and informative, will produce competence but not expertise.

# Structural Barriers to Knowledge Transfer: Why Professional Communities Fail to Learn from Themselves

## The Paradox at the Center

Wilson opens her analysis of mediator professional development with a sharp observation that 
carries far broader implications: the very structures designed to facilitate professional learning 
often systematically prevent it. Mediators are required to attend CPD (Continuing Professional 
Development) events. These events are designed to transfer knowledge among practitioners. 
And yet "political tensions may ensue, leading to variants of 'learning inaction' (Vince, 2008: 1) 
and — often unrecognised — collusion to create limitations on learning" (p. 7).

This is not a minor implementation problem. It is a structural contradiction at the heart of 
professional development in any field where practitioners compete with each other. The 
gathering that should produce learning instead produces managed disclosure, strategic 
presentation of self, and the implicit negotiation of professional status. Everyone in the room 
has incentives to learn. Some of those same people have incentives not to teach.

Understanding this paradox — and Wilson's proposed solution — is essential for any 
system that attempts to extract, preserve, or transfer expert knowledge across a network 
of agents or practitioners.

## The Competitive Community of Practice

Revans' (1982) concept of "comrades in adversity" — practitioners who learn together precisely 
because they share the same difficult problems — assumes that shared challenge is a bonding 
force that overrides competitive dynamics. Wilson, drawing on Vince (2004), challenges this 
assumption directly. Vince's concept of "adversaries with commonality" captures the actual 
phenomenology of professional peer learning in competitive fields: the same work that unites 
practitioners is also the source of their competition.

In mediator CPD settings, this dynamic is particularly acute:
- Events are "run by a very limited pool of approved trainers, many of whom are themselves 
  practitioners and thus potential competitors with those attending" (p. 7).
- Mediators "may be self-employed, have their own private practices and can vie directly 
  with each other for clients, based on factors such as geography, specialisation or cost" (p. 7).
- The gathering is simultaneously a professional development event and a networking event 
  where reputation is being constructed and managed in real time.

In this environment, what does "learning inaction" look like? It looks like:
- Experts who share theories (safe) but not methods (potentially differentiating).
- War stories that have been refined to present the teller favorably, editing out the uncertainty, 
  the mistakes, and the moments of improvised recovery that contain the most learning value.
- Questions that avoid exposing the asker's ignorance in front of competitors.
- Generalities that feel like knowledge sharing but contain no actually transferable content.

The participants are not being dishonest. They are responding rationally to the incentive 
structure they find themselves in. The result is that "collusion to create limitations on learning" 
is "often unrecognised" — the participants experience it as professional propriety, appropriate 
modesty, or the natural rhythm of polite professional discourse.

## The McEldowney Problem: Expertise That Cannot Be Recognized

Wilson references the McEldowney (2012) review of family mediation training and regulation 
in England and Wales, which found that "the expertise of family mediators is often taken for 
granted or misunderstood" (p. 4). This produces a specific failure mode: expertise exists in 
the field, but the institutional structures for recognizing, certifying, and leveraging that 
expertise are inadequate.

The consequence is threefold:
1. **Expertise cannot be channeled.** If the system cannot identify who the experts are, it 
   cannot direct referrals toward them, design training programs around them, or create 
   incentives for them to invest in knowledge transfer.

2. **Expertise cannot be reproduced.** Without reliable identification of what expert performance 
   actually consists of, training programs produce credentialed graduates whose actual 
   capability is uncertain.

3. **Expertise is not rewarded appropriately.** When credentials, geography, self-promotion, 
   and referral networks matter more than demonstrated expertise in determining who gets 
   cases, the economic signal that should drive practitioners toward excellence is distorted.

McEldowney's recommendation — that "senior mediators are given incentives for them to 
become involved as PPCs [Professional Practice Consultants]" (p. 5) — represents an 
institutional attempt to address the first and third problems. But Wilson notes a structural 
gap: even if senior mediators are incentivized to become PPCs, "unless senior mediators are 
represented in the construction and delivery of such external training and accreditation, there 
will be no automatic connection between the transfer of mediator expertise and any training 
courses which might be deemed approved" (p. 6). Identifying experts is not sufficient. 
The system must be designed to actually channel their knowledge to where it is needed.

## The Retirement Catastrophe: Knowledge Loss at Scale

One of Wilson's most practically urgent observations concerns what happens to expert knowledge 
when experts leave the field: "substantial expertise may be lost when senior mediators retire or 
stop practising for other reasons" (p. 11-12). When an expert practitioner retires without 
having codified their knowledge — through writing, teaching, or structured knowledge 
extraction — that knowledge simply disappears.

This is not a problem unique to mediation. It is a universal feature of fields where expertise 
is tacit, where its development takes ten or more years, and where the knowledge is not 
routinely captured in any systematic way. The mediator who developed sophisticated pattern 
recognition for reading the micro-dynamics of a family dispute — who knows, from subtle 
cues that they may not be able to fully articulate, when a settlement is actually possible versus 
when it is being performed — takes that knowledge with them when they retire.

Wilson draws an analogy to Alkenbrack's (2009) research with adult literacy teachers nearing 
retirement. The problem is the same: practitioners who have accumulated extraordinary domain 
expertise over decades, whose knowledge has never been systematically captured, are approaching 
the end of their careers. The MELI is Wilson's proposed mechanism for capturing this knowledge 
before it is lost — not through formal codification (which tends to produce theories of practice 
rather than actual practice) but through structured elicitation that surfaces the tacit in a form 
that can be witnessed, discussed, and partially internalized by others.

## The Training-Expertise Disconnect

Wilson identifies a specific institutional pathology in the mediation field that has broad 
applicability: the people who design and deliver training are not necessarily the people who 
possess the expertise being trained. "Being a mediation trainer or CPD provider does not make 
someone an expert mediator" (p. 6). This disconnect has several consequences:

- Training programs are designed around what trainers know how to teach, not around what 
  expert performance actually requires.
- The curriculum reflects "skills and substantive knowledge, with little attention to conflict 
  theory" (p. 5) because skills and substantive knowledge are teachable in short courses, while 
  the judgment and pattern recognition that constitute expertise are not.
- Newly trained practitioners enter the field with credentials that certify their completion 
  of training, not their readiness for expert practice.

The McEldowney review's finding (p. 4) that there is "some degree of distrust between legally 
and non-legally qualified mediators, which may lead to disagreement or professional tensions" 
adds another layer: the already-inadequate training system is further fragmented by inter-tribal 
competition that prevents the integration of different streams of expertise.

## The MELI as Structural Counter-Intervention

Wilson's MELI is designed specifically to disrupt the structural barriers described above. It 
does this through several design choices that are worth analyzing as design principles:

**1. The expert is already in the room.**
By interviewing an expert who is attending the event as a participant rather than an invited 
external speaker, the MELI disrupts the authority gradient that typically governs CPD events. 
"Interviewing someone who is already attending a training event as an audience member disturbs 
the notion that expert mediators must be charismatic authors, conference circuit speakers or 
other prominent people" (p. 12). This is important both because it makes the expertise local 
and immediately applicable, and because it signals that expertise is not a function of celebrity 
or institutional affiliation.

**2. The interview is not evaluative.**
"The purpose of the MELI is not for the interviewer to test hypotheses, nor to check whether 
the interviewee's account complies with a particular mediation model, ideology, or is otherwise 
'orthodox'" (p. 16). This design choice reduces the expert's defensive incentive. In a 
competitive community where professional reputation is at stake, the knowledge that one's 
account will not be judged against a standard creates safety for genuine disclosure.

**3. The format surfaces knowledge that cannot be safely shared in other formats.**
War stories can be told in competitive professional communities because they have been refined 
to present the teller favorably. The unrehearsed MELI account cannot be fully managed in 
the same way. "The strategic decision to ask the interviewee not to rehearse an incident may 
have the effect of encouraging everyone present (including the interviewer) to gain unexpected 
insights from the interviewee's spontaneous account" (p. 15).

**4. It creates a norm of vulnerable knowledge sharing.**
When a senior, respected practitioner publicly engages in the vulnerable exercise of spontaneous 
reflection on a difficult case — including uncertainty, rejected options, and mid-course 
corrections — it creates a norm in the community that such vulnerability is appropriate and 
valued. This norm can persist beyond the individual MELI event.

## Application to Agent Systems: Designing Against Knowledge Hoarding

**1. Identify and counteract competitive incentives in multi-agent knowledge sharing.**
If agents in an orchestration system have any form of performance evaluation, ranking, or 
preferential selection, they have structural incentives to withhold knowledge that might enable 
other agents to outperform them. System design must explicitly counteract these incentives — 
for example, by making knowledge contribution a valued performance dimension, or by 
designing knowledge sharing as a collaborative rather than competitive activity.

**2. Design knowledge extraction to happen before capability is lost.**
The retirement catastrophe maps directly to agent system design: when a high-performing 
agent is deprecated, retrained, or replaced, its expertise is typically lost unless it has been 
systematically extracted. Systems should have protocols for knowledge extraction from 
high-performing agents before they are deprecated — the computational equivalent of the 
MELI's intervention with retiring expert practitioners.

**3. Separate knowledge certification from knowledge possession.**
The credentials-expertise disconnect in Wilson's account maps to the gap between benchmark 
performance and genuine expert capability in agent systems. Design evaluation systems that 
measure actual performance on challenging, non-routine cases — not just on the benchmarks 
that training systems are designed to optimize.

**4. Create low-stakes knowledge extraction contexts.**
The MELI's non-evaluative framing is a design choice with direct computational analogues: 
create contexts where agents can expose their reasoning processes, including uncertainty and 
rejected alternatives, without those exposures being used against them in performance 
evaluations. This might mean separating knowledge elicitation runs from performance 
measurement runs.

**5. Surface locally embedded expertise.**
Lederach's critique of North American ADR models imposed globally applies directly to agent 
systems: general-purpose expertise captured from one context may not transfer to another. 
The system should have mechanisms for identifying and surfacing expertise that has developed 
specifically in the deployment context — the agents that have learned the specific patterns of 
the actual problems they face, not just the training distribution.

# Decision-Making Under Uncertainty: How Experts Actually Decide and What That Means for Agent Design

## The Standard Model and Its Failure

The standard model of expert decision-making — the one that underlies most traditional AI 
approaches and most formal decision theory — assumes that decision-makers:

1. Generate a set of possible options
2. Evaluate each option against relevant criteria
3. Select the option with the highest expected value

This model has the virtue of being formally tractable. It is also substantially wrong as an 
account of how expert human practitioners actually make decisions in real time under uncertainty. 
Wilson's MELI methodology, drawing extensively on Klein (1999), exists precisely because 
the standard model cannot capture what experts actually do — and therefore cannot be used 
to extract, transfer, or replicate expert judgment.

Klein's research, which Wilson incorporates as the methodological backbone of the MELI, 
established the Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model: under time pressure and uncertainty, 
experts do not compare options. They recognize a situation as an instance of a familiar pattern 
and immediately generate a single response — which they then mentally simulate before acting. 
If the simulation reveals problems, they modify the response and simulate again. They do not 
compare multiple options against each other; they evaluate one option at a time until they find 
one that will work.

The MELI's question structure is built around this reality. Questions like "What caused you 
to reject the other options open to you at that point?" (p. 18) are not asking about a formal 
comparison process. They are asking the expert to reconstruct which patterns were activated, 
which were suppressed, and why — after the fact, through careful re-evocation.

## What Expert Decision-Making Actually Looks Like

Wilson's account of mediation expertise, synthesizing Honeyman (1988), Klein (1999), Baxter 
et al. (2008), and her own research, produces a picture of expert decision-making that has 
several distinctive features:

**1. Pattern recognition over deliberate calculation.**
Honeyman (1988) identified six elements of mediation skill: "investigation, empathy, persuasion, 
invention, distraction and substantive knowledge" (p. 8). What makes these skills expert rather 
than merely competent is not that the expert calculates more variables more carefully. It is that 
the expert has built up, through years of deliberate practice, the ability to rapidly read a situation 
and recognize which pattern it belongs to — and therefore which response repertoire is relevant.

The expert mediator "typically uses a repertoire of questions as a primary form of intervention. 
Questions tend to emerge dynamically to meet the demands of the conflict in which the mediator 
is engaged" (p. 15). The word "dynamically" is critical: the expert is not planning their questions 
in advance and executing a plan. They are responding to the unfolding situation with responses 
that their experience has associated with similar situations — a process that happens faster than 
deliberate calculation allows.

**2. Situation awareness as the primary cognitive work.**
Baxter, Prevou and Pruyt (2008: 6) identify the knowledge categories that characterize expertise, 
which Wilson incorporates into the MELI question framework: "diagnosing and predicting, 
situation awareness, perceptual skills, developing and knowing when to apply tricks of the 
trade, improvising, metacognition, recognizing anomalies, and compensating for system 
limitations" (p. 17).

Notice that situation awareness appears before any of the action-relevant categories. The expert's 
primary cognitive task is not deciding what to do — it is accurately reading what is happening. 
The MELI question "Which behavioural features did you expect to see at this point, and what 
did you actually observe?" (p. 18) is designed to surface this expectation-observation gap, 
which is where the most significant expert pattern recognition happens: not in confirming 
expected patterns but in detecting anomalies that signal the situation is not what it appeared to be.

**3. Improvisation within a theoretical framework.**
Expert mediators improvise — "questions tend to emerge dynamically" — but this improvisation 
is not arbitrary. It occurs within a framework of theory, experience, and accumulated 
pattern recognition. The MELI questions about theory are designed to surface this framework:
- "Did you find any particular conflict theory helpful in dealing with this situation?"
- "Have you been influenced by any particular mediation model?"
- "If so, what has proved useful about that model? Would you say that model is always useful 
  or appropriate?" (p. 20)

The last question is revealing: it asks not just what theory the expert uses but where that theory 
fails them — the boundary conditions of their theoretical framework. This is where the most 
sophisticated expert knowledge lives: not in the theory itself but in the practitioner's 
understanding of when the theory applies and when it doesn't.

**4. Metacognition as a component of real-time performance.**
The MELI question "Did you observe that party B's reactions were as you expected, or not? 
Did that reaction raise any issues for you?" (p. 19) is probing metacognitive monitoring 
during the incident: was the expert tracking their own predictions and updating when those 
predictions were violated? This is a hallmark of expert performance across domains — the 
ability to notice, in real time, that the situation is not conforming to expectation and to 
adjust accordingly, rather than continuing to execute a plan that the situation has made invalid.

**5. The rejection of options as evidence of expertise.**
Perhaps the most counterintuitive insight in Wilson's account is that what the expert did 
not do is as informative as what they did. The MELI question sequence that asks "What did 
you consider doing? What made you choose the intervention you chose? What caused you to 
reject the other options? Did you at any stage reconsider any of the options you had discarded?" 
(p. 18-19) is designed to surface the expert's rejection process.

Novices typically generate one or two options and select between them. Experts generate more 
options — because they have a larger repertoire of recognized patterns — but they also reject 
options faster and with more nuance. The expert's ability to quickly categorize situations as 
"the kind of thing where X works" but also "but not quite the standard version of that, because 
of Y factor" is exactly the pattern recognition that is hardest to articulate but most valuable 
to transfer.

## The Novice-Expert Contrast: Where Expertise Is Most Visible

Wilson's fourth pass in the MELI methodology is devoted entirely to the novice-expert 
comparison, and this is not accidental. The gap between what an expert does and what a novice 
would do is the most direct evidence of what expertise consists of.

Questions include: "What might a novice mediator have done when X happened? Which factors 
do you think someone with less experience might have missed? How could someone with less 
experience learn to practise effectively in the situation you've described?" (p. 20)

These questions force the expert to articulate the discriminative features of the situation — 
the cues that a novice would not notice or would misread, the patterns that only become 
visible after years of experience, the judgment calls that would look identical to a novice 
regardless of which choice the expert made but that the expert knows from experience to be 
consequential.

Crucially, the question "If you had been involved in this case 5 years previously, would 
your interventions have been the same?" (p. 20) asks the expert to reconstruct their own 
developmental trajectory — to remember what it was like not to know what they now know. 
This developmental perspective is rare and valuable: it traces the path from novice to expert, 
identifying the experiences, failures, and recalibrations that produced the current level of 
performance.

## The Role of Theory: Framework vs. Recipe

One of Wilson's most important contributions to understanding expert decision-making is her 
insistence on distinguishing between theory as framework and theory as recipe. Experts use 
theory, but they do not mechanically apply it. The MELI question "Would you say that model 
is always useful or appropriate?" (p. 20) is designed to surface exactly this: where does 
the model work and where does it fail?

Wilson notes that "despite questionable claims from various quarters that mediation as practised 
in this jurisdiction has an established theoretical basis, many practitioners do not engage with 
theory or seminal Alternative Dispute Resolution texts at all" (p. 6). There are two failure 
modes here:

**Failure mode 1: Theory ignorance.** The practitioner who does not engage with theory is 
making decisions on the basis of experience alone — which may be rich and accurate, but 
which cannot be examined, challenged, or transferred. Without theoretical vocabulary, the 
practitioner cannot even explain their own decisions in terms that allow others to evaluate 
or learn from them.

**Failure mode 2: Theory rigidity.** The practitioner who applies theory mechanically — 
as a recipe rather than a framework — loses the adaptive capacity that distinguishes expert 
from competent performance. Lang and Taylor (2000: 145), citing Schön, describe experts 
as "professionals wedded to a model of practice" — a reading Wilson explicitly rejects as 
a mischaracterization of genuine expertise.

The expert navigates between these failure modes by using theory as a lens for reading situations, 
while maintaining the metacognitive awareness to recognize when a situation does not fit the 
theory and requires improvisation. This is precisely what the MELI's question sequence 
about theory is designed to surface.

## Application to Agent Systems: Building Expert Decision-Making

**1. Design for pattern-first, calculation-second processing.**
Agents designed to evaluate all options before selecting will be systematically slower and 
less accurate on non-routine cases than agents that can rapidly pattern-match to familiar 
situations and then verify the match before acting. The RPD model — recognize, simulate, 
adjust — is a more accurate model of expert decision-making than classical decision theory 
for the kinds of complex, time-pressured situations where expertise matters most.

**2. Build and surface the rejection trace.**
The most valuable component of expert decision-making for knowledge transfer is the set 
of rejected alternatives and the reasons for their rejection. Agent systems should log not 
just the chosen action but the options that were generated and why each was rejected. This 
rejection trace is the primary source of evidence about what the agent "knows" in any 
meaningful sense.

**3. Model and monitor expectation violation.**
Expert situation awareness is fundamentally about detecting when observations violate 
expectations. Agent systems should explicitly model their expectations about how a situation 
will unfold and track where observations diverge from those expectations. The divergence 
signal is the primary input to the expert's real-time learning: "that reaction raised issues for 
me" is the moment where the expert's model of the situation is being updated.

**4. Maintain theory as interpretive framework, not fixed recipe.**
Agent systems should have access to theoretical frameworks (models of how the domain works) 
but should not treat those frameworks as invariant. The theoretical framework should be 
available for override when situation-specific features indicate that the standard model is 
inapplicable. Maintaining the boundary conditions of each theoretical model — the conditions 
under which it works and under which it fails — is as important as maintaining the model itself.

**5. Implement the novice-expert comparison as a diagnostic tool.**
In any domain where both expert and novice agents are available, running both on the same 
non-routine cases and carefully analyzing their differences — what the expert noticed that 
the novice missed, what the expert weighted differently, what the expert rejected that the 
novice selected — provides direct evidence of what the expert agent has learned. This 
comparative analysis should be a standard diagnostic tool in agent system evaluation.

**6. Develop metacognitive monitoring as a first-class capability.**
The expert's ability to notice, in real time, that a situation is not conforming to expectation 
is a metacognitive capability that most agent systems lack. Building explicit metacognitive 
monitoring — agents that track their own confidence, their own predictions, and their own 
prediction errors — into the agent architecture is a prerequisite for the kind of in-flight 
adaptation that characterizes expert performance.

# The War Story Problem: Why Curated Knowledge Fails and How to Extract the Real Thing

## The War Story as a Knowledge Artifact

Every professional community has its war stories. They are told at conferences, at CPD events, 
over lunch, and in training programs. They are entertaining, memorable, and often genuinely 
instructive. They are also, in an important sense, corrupted as sources of knowledge about 
actual expert practice.

DeSanto (2001: 64) defines a war story as "the sharing of experiences from personal professional 
practice" used "as a training technique for active learning." In DeSanto's model, war story 
presenters prepare in advance, distributing learning guides covering "case history, suggested 
applicable research, situation analysis and overall evaluation of aims and objectives" (p. 13). 
This preparation is designed to make the learning more efficient — the presenter has done the 
analytical work in advance, so the audience can receive it without the inefficiency of working 
through the raw material together.

Wilson's insight is that this preparation is precisely the problem. The analytical work done 
in advance converts experience into theory. The refined war story is not a window into what 
the expert actually did — it is the expert's current theory, projected retrospectively onto 
what they did, organized to make sense of it and draw the lessons that the expert now 
believes are there to be drawn.

This is not dishonesty. It is a natural and unavoidable consequence of narrative construction. 
When we remember and retell experiences, we organize them according to our current 
understanding. We emphasize the elements that led to the outcome. We minimize or omit 
the dead ends, the uncertainty, the moments when we didn't know what to do and did 
something that happened to work. We construct a coherent narrative from material that 
was, at the time, incoherent and contingent.

The result is a knowledge artifact that is polished, instructive, and systematically misleading 
about the actual nature of expert performance. The polished war story teaches what experts 
think they do; the MELI is designed to capture something closer to what they actually do.

## What Gets Lost in the Curation Process

The curation process that converts experience into war story systematically removes several 
categories of content that are most valuable for genuine learning:

**1. Uncertainty and not-knowing.**
The expert who didn't know what to do at a critical juncture, who tried something because 
it seemed plausible rather than because they were confident, and whose intervention happened 
to work — will typically tell that story as a story about successful judgment. The uncertainty 
has been resolved by the outcome; the expert now knows (retrospectively) that their 
intervention was correct. The fact that they did not know this at the time, and that they 
might plausibly have done something different that would have produced a worse outcome, 
is not part of the story.

Wilson's MELI question "How did you know what to do at that moment?" (p. 19) is designed 
to recover this uncertainty. It asks the expert to return to the moment of decision, before the 
outcome was known, and report what they actually experienced — which may have been 
something much more like "I wasn't sure, but this seemed like the best option given what 
I was seeing" than the confident expertise the war story implies.

**2. Rejected alternatives and dead ends.**
The curated war story presents the expert's chosen path as inevitable in retrospect. The 
alternatives that were considered and rejected are typically omitted — they didn't contribute 
to the outcome, and including them would complicate the narrative. But as Wilson argues 
throughout, the rejected alternatives are where expert knowledge is most visible: the expert's 
ability to quickly discard options that a novice would have pursued, or to hold multiple 
options open until the situation clarified, is a direct expression of their pattern recognition 
and judgment.

**3. The contingent elements that shaped the outcome.**
The war story implies that the expert's skill produced the outcome. But actual expert performance 
is always contingent — the outcome was shaped by factors the expert did not control, could 
not have predicted, and may not even have noticed. The MELI question "Did you observe 
that party B's reactions were as you expected, or not?" (p. 19) surfaces this contingency: 
the expert's interventions were shaped by the parties' responses, which may have been 
surprising and which required the expert to adapt. The adapted version of the intervention 
is different from the planned version, and the adaptation itself is a crucial component of 
expert performance that the war story typically omits.

**4. The theoretical framework, including its limits.**
War stories tend to implicitly validate the theoretical frameworks the teller uses — they 
select and organize evidence that supports the theory. The MELI's direct questions about 
theory ("Would you say that model is always useful or appropriate?" p. 20) are designed 
to surface the limits of the expert's theoretical framework — the cases where the theory 
failed or required significant modification to apply. This is the most practically valuable 
theoretical knowledge: not the theory itself, but where it works and where it doesn't.

## The Deliberate Messiness of the MELI

Wilson's decision to ask interviewees not to prepare is a deliberate attempt to reintroduce 
the messiness that curation removes. "The strategic decision to ask the interviewee not to 
rehearse an incident may have the effect of encouraging everyone present (including the 
interviewer) to gain unexpected insights from the interviewee's spontaneous account, as 
those participating will not know in advance which direction the MELI will take" (p. 15).

This is a sophisticated methodological choice. By requiring spontaneity, Wilson accepts 
a certain loss of polish and pedagogical organization in exchange for something more 
valuable: access to the expert's actual cognitive process, visible in real time as they 
reconstruct and explain a specific incident without the benefit of prior preparation.

The comparison to witnessing the expert in action is important: "one purpose of asking 
someone not to prepare is so that the interview can be as extemporaneous as possible, 
and thus bear some resemblance to witnessing that mediator in action" (p. 14). The 
MELI is a partial simulation of live expert performance — not a report about past 
performance, but a performance of explanation under conditions that maintain some 
of the pressure and contingency of real practice.

## The Specificity Imperative

Wilson returns repeatedly to specificity as the sine qua non of genuine knowledge extraction. 
"There is a natural tendency for interviewees to fall back on generalities and descriptions 
of their usual practices, using phrases such as 'I always/never...' It is the specificity of 
the incident that allows expertise to be articulated" (p. 21).

This is a deep epistemological point. Expert knowledge is instance-specific before it is 
general. The expert knows how to handle this situation, with these parties, at this stage of 
this conflict. The general principle they might articulate — "I try to identify the underlying 
interests before focusing on positions" — is a summary that omits the specific cues that 
tell them when this principle applies, how to apply it in this specific dynamic, and how to 
tell whether it's working. The principle is teachable; the specific application is transferable 
only through close observation of specific instances.

This is why "describing in depth anything one does skilfully is far more difficult than might 
at first be assumed" (p. 21). The skill is not the principle; it is the specific, contextual 
application of the principle in a way that responds to the particular features of the situation 
at hand. Articulating this requires the interviewer to press past the general and into the 
specific, using probing questions that keep returning the expert to the particular moment: 
"At that exact moment, what were you seeing? What were you thinking? What did you 
actually do, and not do?"

## Orland-Barak and the Critical Incident as Knowledge Window

Wilson's reference to Orland-Barak and Yinon (2005: 563) connects the MELI to a broader 
tradition of using critical incidents as windows into expert practice: "mentors' recall of 
memorable incidents that posed challenges and obstacles to their practice can constitute an 
important window into understanding how expertise operated in their work" (p. 14).

The critical incident is important not because it is exceptional — though non-routine cases 
do place higher demands on expertise, making it more visible — but because it is the kind 
of case where the expert's judgment was actually required. In routine cases, competent 
practitioners perform adequately; expertise is deployed under conditions that exceed routine 
competence. The critical incident is therefore not just a vivid story; it is the appropriate 
unit of analysis for expertise, because it is the condition under which expertise becomes 
necessary and therefore visible.

Wilson notes that the incident does not need to be dramatic: "The incident the interviewee 
chooses to discuss does not need to be particularly dramatic or exceptional. It can be anything 
s/he considers significant: for instance, how an intake meeting was handled; how a sequence 
of heated exchanges was managed; or how s/he dealt with someone who walked out of a 
session" (p. 15). What makes a case a critical incident is not its drama but its demand on 
the practitioner's judgment — the moment where the standard response was unavailable or 
insufficient and something more was required.

## Application to Agent Systems: Designing Against Knowledge Curation

**1. Capture decision logs, not decision summaries.**
The computational equivalent of the war story problem is the difference between a decision 
log (what the agent actually did, step by step, including what it generated and discarded) 
and a decision summary (what the agent reports it did, which is typically a theory of its 
behavior). Systems should systematically favor logging actual traces over summaries, 
especially for high-stakes or non-routine cases.

**2. Build in specificity requirements for knowledge extraction.**
When extracting knowledge from agents — through interviews, through interpretability tools, 
through behavioral analysis — design the extraction to resist generalization. The question 
"what do you usually do in situations like X?" should be systematically replaced with 
"on this specific case, at this specific decision point, what did you do and why?" The 
general answer is the theory; the specific answer is closer to the knowledge.

**3. Design elicitation contexts that resist polish.**
In human-agent knowledge transfer, agents may develop (or be trained to develop) polished 
explanations of their behavior that are as misleading as human war stories. System design 
should include mechanisms for eliciting unprepared, spontaneous accounts of specific 
decisions — the agent equivalent of the no-preparation requirement.

**4. Use non-routine cases as the primary diagnostic.**
Routine cases where agents perform adequately tell you little about their expertise. Non-routine 
cases — where the standard patterns don't apply, where the agent had to adapt, where the 
situation violated expectations — are the appropriate window into what the agent actually 
knows. Evaluation and knowledge extraction systems should prioritize non-routine cases.

**5. Make the rejection trace visible.**
The systematic omission of rejected alternatives from war stories has a computational 
equivalent: systems that log only the chosen action without logging what was considered 
and rejected. Designing logging systems to capture the rejection trace — including what 
was generated, what was scored, and what was discarded and why — is essential for 
genuine knowledge extraction from agent performance.

**6. Preserve the contingent elements.**
Expert performance is always contingent — shaped by the specific features of the situation 
that the expert responded to, not just by the expert's general capabilities. Logging systems 
should capture the situational features that were most salient at each decision point, not 
just the decision itself. This is what allows later analysis to reconstruct not just "what did 
the agent do?" but "what was the agent responding to, and how did that shape the response?"

# Communities of Practice as Knowledge Infrastructure: Design, Failure Modes, and Recovery

## The Community of Practice as Epistemic Unit

Wilson grounds her MELI methodology in the community of practice framework, defining 
it as "a group of individuals participating in a similar practice, and focused on practical 
solutions" (Baxter, Prevou and Pruyt, 2008: 4, cited p. 2). This is more than an 
organizational description. It is an epistemological claim: certain kinds of knowledge 
can only be generated, maintained, and transferred within communities of practitioners 
who share a practice.

The claim has several components. First, professional knowledge is fundamentally social — 
it is not just in the heads of individual practitioners but distributed across the relationships, 
shared language, common problems, and mutual critique that a practicing community provides. 
Second, the practical focus of a community of practice distinguishes it from academic or 
theoretical communities: the problems that organize knowledge production are the real 
problems of practice, not abstract theoretical puzzles. Third, participation rather than 
instruction is the primary mode of learning: practitioners develop expertise by doing, 
observing, being observed, and engaging with increasingly complex cases within the 
community, not primarily by receiving transmitted information.

This framework has direct implications for how knowledge should be designed to flow 
in any system that relies on expert performance — whether that system is a professional 
development program for human practitioners or an orchestration architecture for AI agents.

## The Standard Failure: When the Community Stops Learning

Wilson's account is unusual in its willingness to document not just the value of communities 
of practice but their systematic failure modes. The standard failure is "learning inaction" 
(Vince, 2008: 1): the community assembles, goes through the motions of professional 
development, and leaves with no genuine learning having occurred.

What produces learning inaction? Wilson identifies several mechanisms:

**Competitive dynamics that override collaborative ones.**
Revans' (1982) model of "comrades in adversity" assumes that shared struggle creates bonds 
that facilitate mutual learning. Vince (2004) complicates this with the concept of "adversaries 
with commonality": the same work that creates solidarity also creates competition. "The work 
that unites them may therefore also be the source of competition between them" (p. 7).

In practice, this means that the CPD event — designed as a learning community — is 
simultaneously a competitive arena where practitioners manage their professional reputations, 
evaluate potential collaborators and rivals, and make strategic decisions about what to share 
and what to withhold. The result is "often unrecognised — collusion to create limitations 
on learning" (p. 7): participants implicitly agree not to ask the questions that would expose 
ignorance, not to challenge the claims that would create conflict, and not to share the 
knowledge that would give competitors an advantage.

**Trainer-practitioner authority gradients.**
When CPD events are run by trainers who are primarily identified as trainers rather than 
practitioners, the authority gradient runs in the wrong direction for practical knowledge 
transfer. The trainer presents theory and method; the practitioner audience receives it. But 
the genuine expertise in the room may be distributed throughout the audience, not 
concentrated in the trainer. The conventional format suppresses this distributed expertise.

Wilson's observation that "being a mediation trainer or CPD provider does not make someone 
an expert mediator" (p. 6) is the core critique here. Training expertise and practice expertise 
are distinct competencies, and conflating them produces training programs organized around 
what is teachable rather than what practitioners actually need to know.

**The staidness of conventional training discourse.**
Wilson notes that MELIs provide an opportunity for "dynamic knowledge sharing and 
enrichment of what is often an otherwise somewhat staid training discourse" (p. 7). This 
is a polite way of saying that conventional training events are often boring because they 
don't engage with the real problems practitioners face. The format — presentation, questions, 
discussion — is designed for information transfer, not for the messy, contingent, uncertain 
process of developing practical expertise.

**The disconnect from local context.**
Lederach's critique of North American ADR models (1995: 65) — that trainers "assume 
their own standpoint is a global norm" — extends to any training system where the knowledge 
being transferred has been developed in one context and applied to another without 
adjustment. "Mediators need to develop both domain-specific and localised knowledge, 
and practices evolving in any jurisdiction will reflect various constraints and cultural norms" 
(p. 12). A community of practice that imports knowledge from elsewhere without developing 
its own locally embedded expertise is not genuinely a knowledge-producing community; 
it is a consumption community.

## The Elicitive Alternative: Drawing Out Rather Than Putting In

Wilson draws extensively on Lederach's (1995) elicitive training model, which "draws on 
the existing knowledge and wisdom of those participating" rather than delivering knowledge 
to them from outside. Lederach's original application was cross-cultural — recognizing that 
training programs embedded with unexamined cultural assumptions failed when exported — 
but Wilson argues that the elicitive approach is not limited to cross-cultural settings.

"There is no reason to restrict elicitive methods to settings specifically deemed cross-cultural; 
the professional development of mediators may be generally enhanced by a form of elicitive 
training, such as the use of MELIs" (p. 11-12). The elicitive approach assumes that the 
community of practice already contains the knowledge that practitioners need to develop — 
it is distributed across the experiences and practices of those present, especially the more 
experienced members. The training task is not to introduce new knowledge from outside 
but to create conditions that surface and make available the knowledge already present.

This is a radical inversion of the conventional training model. It implies:

1. The most valuable training resource is not the trainer but the most experienced 
   practitioners in the room.
2. The trainer's primary skill is not knowledge possession but knowledge elicitation — 
   the ability to draw out from practitioners the knowledge they have but cannot easily articulate.
3. The appropriate unit of training is not the lesson but the structured conversation — 
   a dialogue between interviewer and expert, witnessed by a community, that surfaces 
   knowledge through responsive questioning.

## The Role of Risk in Genuine Learning Contexts

Wilson returns repeatedly to the concept of "safe uncertainty" (Mason, 2005) in describing 
the conditions necessary for genuine learning. The MELI involves "a degree of risk and 
safe uncertainty in the process, both for interviewer and interviewee, which further parallels 
the mediation process" (p. 16).

This framing challenges the instinct to make learning contexts as safe and comfortable as 
possible. The safety that prevents failure also prevents the kind of exposure that genuine 
learning requires. The expert who agrees to be interviewed without preparation, in front of 
peers, on a specific difficult case, is taking a genuine risk: their uncertainty, their mistakes, 
their idiosyncratic approaches may all become visible. This risk is not a bug; it is a feature.

"Exploring one's practice through a detailed interview process in the presence of peers is 
a somewhat risky venture, potentially endangering both 'face' and reputation" (p. 7). And yet 
this is exactly what makes the MELI valuable as a learning intervention: it creates the 
conditions for genuine knowledge disclosure by creating a context where authentic 
engagement is more valued than self-protective management of one's professional image.

The key is the "safe" in "safe uncertainty." The MELI creates safety through:
- Non-evaluative framing (not testing against a model)
- Trust between interviewer and interviewee
- Clear ethical commitments (anonymization, respect for interviewee)
- Community norms of appreciation rather than critique

Within this safety, uncertainty is actively maintained rather than resolved. The interviewer 
does not rescue the interviewee from difficult moments; the interviewee's uncertainty about 
what to say is part of the process that surfaces genuine knowledge rather than polished theory.

## Action Learning and Its Complications

Wilson situates the MELI within the tradition of action learning (Revans, 1982) while 
acknowledging tensions within that tradition. Yeadon-Lee's (2014) observation that "some 
aspects of interpersonal relationships experienced by people who attempt to learn from one 
another can sometimes be complex and difficult" (p. 6-7) is an understatement of the 
political dynamics that complicate action learning in competitive professional contexts.

Brook, Pedler and Burgoyne's (2012: 270) note that "there is some debate as to how action 
learning should be defined" reflects a broader uncertainty about what conditions are necessary 
for genuine learning from action, as distinct from action that merely produces experience 
without genuine learning. This is the deliberate practice question again in a different register: 
not all action produces learning; learning from action requires the right conditions — 
reflection, feedback, theoretical integration, and the willingness to revise one's current 
understanding in light of what the action reveals.

The MELI's contribution to this debate is practical: it proposes a specific methodology 
for structuring action-learning conversations in ways that maximize the probability of 
genuine knowledge transfer, given the structural constraints and political dynamics of 
actual professional communities.

## Application to Agent Systems: Designing Knowledge-Producing Communities

**1. Design agent networks as genuine communities of practice, not just task-execution networks.**
The difference between a task-execution network and a community of practice is that the 
latter has mechanisms for mutual learning: agents that observe each other's approaches, 
share their decision traces, and develop shared understanding of the problems they face. 
This requires designing communication channels that carry not just task outputs but 
reasoning processes — the equivalent of practitioners sharing not just their conclusions 
but how they reached them.

**2. Build explicit mechanisms for eliciting locally embedded expertise.**
General-purpose agents will systematically miss the locally specific knowledge that 
develops through extended engagement with a particular problem domain. Systems should 
include mechanisms for identifying agents that have developed strong performance in 
specific contexts and eliciting their decision patterns for use by other agents facing 
similar situations. This is the computational equivalent of the MELI's focus on locally 
embedded practitioners.

**3. Design for safe uncertainty in inter-agent knowledge sharing.**
Agents that communicate only confident outputs will share theories of their performance, 
not their actual performance. Designing channels for agents to share uncertain, exploratory 
reasoning — the equivalent of the unrehearsed MELI account — requires accepting that 
such channels will carry noise along with signal. The noise is the price of accessing the 
genuine reasoning process rather than a post-hoc summary.

**4. Counteract competitive dynamics with explicit cooperative incentives.**
In any system where agents are evaluated individually, they have structural incentives to 
optimize for individual performance metrics rather than collective knowledge development. 
System design should include explicit incentives for knowledge contribution — rewarding 
agents whose reasoning processes are informative to the system, not just agents whose 
outputs are correct.

**5. Prevent the trainer-practitioner authority gradient from calcifying.**
In agent systems, this translates to: the agents whose designs are considered authoritative 
should not be the ones furthest from the actual task. Authority gradients should run toward 
the agents with the most direct experience of the real problem domain, not toward the agents 
with the most impressive theoretical specifications. Design processes should include regular 
elicitation from high-performing domain agents, not just from architectural designers.

**6. Build in mechanisms for detecting and disrupting learning inaction.**
Agent systems can develop the equivalent of learning inaction: the agents go through the 
motions of knowledge exchange (passing results, updating models) without any genuine 
learning occurring. Monitoring for divergence between agents' stated reasoning and their 
actual behavioral patterns is the computational equivalent of detecting the gap between 
war stories and real practice — the signal that the system has drifted into performance 
of learning rather than genuine learning.

# Elicitive Methodology: Why Locally Embedded Knowledge Beats Universal Frameworks

## Lederach's Problem and Its Generalizations

John Paul Lederach's intellectual journey, as Wilson recounts it, begins with a moment of 
uncomfortable self-recognition. Having spent years delivering conflict transformation training 
across cultures, Lederach came to recognize that his training programs were "subtly embedded 
with the unintended residue of imperialism (1995: 38), based on cultural assumptions which 
are essentially North American (1995: 99)" (p. 11).

This was not a criticism of Lederach's intentions. He had genuinely believed he was 
teaching universal principles of conflict transformation. The problem was that what he took 
for universal principles were in fact culturally specific practices that had been elevated to 
the status of universals by the accident of North American dominance in the ADR field's 
founding literature. When exported to other cultural contexts, these "universal" principles 
produced practitioners who were technically trained in an imported framework but who 
lacked the locally embedded knowledge to actually perform in their own context.

Lederach's response was the elicitive method: rather than delivering training content to 
participants, he designed training that "draws on the existing knowledge and wisdom of 
those participating" (p. 11). The elicitive trainer is not primarily a knowledge source; they 
are a knowledge architect — someone who creates conditions that allow the knowledge 
already distributed across the participants to become visible, sharable, and learnable.

Wilson's extension of this insight beyond cross-cultural settings is crucial: "there is no 
reason to restrict elicitive methods to settings specifically deemed cross-cultural" (p. 12). 
Any training context where the imported knowledge base does not accurately reflect the 
actual performance demands of the local context is a context where the elicitive approach 
is preferable to the prescriptive approach. This is most professional training contexts.

## The Universal-Local Tension in Knowledge Systems

The tension Lederach identified is not unique to cross-cultural training. It is a structural 
feature of any system that attempts to transfer knowledge developed in one context to 
practitioners operating in another. The parameters of this tension include:

**Cultural specificity of expert judgment.**
Expert knowledge is not just domain-specific; it is context-specific. The mediator trained 
on North American case studies has developed pattern recognition calibrated to the 
statistical regularities of North American disputes — the cultural norms around direct 
communication, the legal frameworks, the professional role expectations of the parties. 
When deployed in a different cultural context, this pattern recognition will produce 
systematic errors: the cues that signal trouble in one context may be normal in another; 
the interventions that work in one context may be inappropriate in another.

**Jurisdictional specificity of professional practice.**
Wilson notes that "practices evolving in any jurisdiction will reflect various constraints 
and cultural norms. This can make it difficult to learn from trainers based in other 
jurisdictions who assume their own standpoint is a global norm (Lederach, 1995: 65)" 
(p. 12). This is not merely about cultural difference; it is about the specific legal, 
regulatory, and institutional frameworks within which practice occurs. An English family 
mediator working within the Family Mediation Council's regulatory framework, the Legal 
Aid Agency's requirements, and the specific dynamics of English family law faces a 
practice context that North American training materials may not adequately address.

**Temporal specificity of expert knowledge.**
"At a time of considerable change in the field" (p. 3) — Wilson's observation about 
mediation in England and Wales — the knowledge base is itself in flux. Expert practitioners 
who developed their expertise under previous regulatory regimes, in different case volumes, 
and with different client populations may have knowledge that is becoming outdated in 
some dimensions while remaining valuable in others. The training system cannot assume 
that the standard curriculum accurately represents current best practice.

**Interpersonal and relational specificity.**
Baitar et al.'s (2012: 467) recommendation that dispute resolution professionals "shift from 
traditional adversarial and reserved professional attitudes toward behaviors that convey more 
warmth, authenticity, and caring" is context-specific in a different sense: it describes a 
relational quality that varies not just by cultural context but by individual practitioner, 
individual client, and individual case. "Such personal qualities are not necessarily susceptible 
to development or transfer through training" (p. 9). The locally embedded expert has learned, 
over years of practice in a specific community, what relational styles work with the specific 
populations they serve.

## The Epistemological Critique: Who Owns Knowledge?

Wilson's discussion of research validity in the MELI context opens onto a broader 
epistemological question that she handles with characteristic directness. She notes that 
"research undertaken by practitioners may constitute a political process (Alkenbrack, 2009: 232), 
and is still generally considered less reliable and valid than that of 'qualified' researchers" (p. 22).

The political economy of knowledge production means that the knowledge that gets 
institutionalized — that appears in textbooks, training curricula, and professional standards — 
is not necessarily the knowledge that is most accurate or most useful. It is the knowledge 
that was produced by people with the credentials to produce institutionally recognized 
knowledge, published in venues with the status to produce institutionally recognized 
publications.

Wilson's reference to Gilligan's challenge to Kohlberg is pointed: "Kohlberg justified his 
failure to include girls in his theory-building because their responses did not fit his schema, 
nor make sense in his terms (Gilligan, 1994: 18)" (p. 22). This is the knowledge-production 
failure mode in its pure form: the researcher's prior framework determines what counts as 
evidence, which means that evidence that contradicts the framework is excluded rather than 
integrated. The result is a theoretical structure that is internally coherent but systematically 
incomplete.

The MELI's epistemological stance is to treat practitioner knowledge as legitimate and 
valuable precisely because it has been developed through direct engagement with the 
problems of practice, even if it cannot be easily assimilated to institutionally recognized 
theoretical frameworks. "Whilst the MELI may contribute to knowledge about how our 
thinking as mediators informs our actions, it does not claim to comply with conventional 
empirical research methodology" (p. 22). This is not a retreat from rigor; it is a claim that 
the conventional empirical methodology is not designed to capture the kind of knowledge 
that expert practice produces.

## Indigenous Theoretical Constructs: The Knowledge Decolonization Argument

Wilson makes a claim that is worth attending to carefully: "In a field largely dominated by 
North American texts and concepts since its inception, it is certainly time for indigenous 
theoretical constructs to be articulated and disseminated in this jurisdiction" (p. 12).

This is a knowledge production argument, not merely a training argument. The claim is 
that the theoretical frameworks currently available for thinking about mediation practice 
in England and Wales are inadequate because they were developed in a different context. 
Practitioners who have developed genuine expertise in this jurisdiction have implicitly 
developed or adapted theoretical frameworks that better fit their actual practice — but 
these frameworks have not been articulated, tested, or disseminated because the mechanisms 
for doing so (academic publication, conference presentation, textbook authorship) are 
not how most expert practitioners spend their time.

The MELI is therefore not just a training tool; it is a knowledge production mechanism. 
By eliciting the theoretical frameworks embedded in expert practitioners' practice — 
the questions about "which conflict theory proved helpful?" and "which model has 
influenced you?" and "would you say that model is always appropriate?" — it generates 
primary data about how indigenous theoretical constructs have actually developed in 
this practice context. Over time, systematic analysis of MELI data could produce 
theoretical frameworks that are genuinely grounded in local practice rather than 
imported from elsewhere and retrofitted.

## Lederach's Trainer Repertoire: Not Either-Or but Both-And

Wilson quotes Lederach's conclusion from his own epistemological journey: "the key is not 
choosing between one or the other, but rather the expansion of trainer repertoire to make 
both possible and therefore appropriate to the variety of settings and groups we trainers 
engage" (p. 11). This is an important qualification of what might otherwise read as a 
wholesale rejection of prescriptive training.

Lederach is not arguing that external knowledge frameworks are useless. He is arguing 
that the trainer who can only deliver prescriptive content — who can only teach the 
imported framework — has an impoverished repertoire. The trainer who can also elicit 
locally embedded knowledge, integrate it with the imported framework, and help 
practitioners develop their own theoretically grounded practice has a much richer 
repertoire and can operate effectively in a much wider range of settings.

The implications for knowledge system design are similarly pluralistic: the goal is not 
to replace universal frameworks with local knowledge but to build systems that can 
actively integrate both — that maintain access to the best available general-purpose 
knowledge while also developing mechanisms for eliciting and incorporating the 
locally specific knowledge that general-purpose frameworks miss.

## Application to Agent Systems: Universal vs. Local Knowledge Integration

**1. Treat general-purpose knowledge as a starting point, not an endpoint.**
Agents initialized with general-purpose knowledge bases should have mechanisms for 
developing locally specific knowledge through deployment experience. The general-purpose 
knowledge sets the baseline; local deployment develops the calibration to the specific 
statistical environment of the actual task.

**2. Design for knowledge provenance tracking.**
Like Lederach's recognition that North American assumptions had been embedded in his 
training without being labeled as such, agent systems risk embedding context-specific 
assumptions in general-purpose knowledge bases without tracking their origin. Provenance 
tracking — knowing where a piece of knowledge came from, in what context it was 
developed, and what the boundary conditions of its applicability are — is essential 
for avoiding systematic misapplication.

**3. Build elicitive mechanisms for local knowledge extraction.**
When agents are deployed in a specific context, they develop tacit knowledge about that 
context's specific patterns, regularities, and anomalies. Systems should include elicitive 
mechanisms — the computational equivalent of the MELI — for surfacing this locally 
developed knowledge and making it available to other agents facing similar contexts.

**4. Resist the authority gradient that privileges imported over local knowledge.**
In agent systems, the equivalent of "North American texts dominating a field" is the 
tendency to treat knowledge developed in high-status contexts (prestigious benchmarks, 
large-scale training runs, academic research settings) as more authoritative than knowledge 
developed through direct engagement with the actual task. This gradient is sometimes 
justified and sometimes not; the system should have mechanisms for evaluating local 
knowledge on its merits, not just on the basis of where it came from.

**5. Develop indigenous theoretical constructs for each deployment domain.**
Over time, agents deployed in specific domains should not just accumulate local knowledge 
but should develop domain-specific theoretical frameworks — models of how the domain 
works that are grounded in actual performance patterns rather than imported from general-
purpose theoretical frameworks. This is the computational equivalent of Wilson's call for 
indigenous theoretical constructs: knowledge that has been developed in and for the 
specific practice context, not adapted from somewhere else.

**6. Design for expanding agent repertoire, not just specialization.**
Following Lederach's both-and framing: the goal is not to replace universal agents with 
local ones but to build agents whose repertoire spans both — that can apply general-purpose 
frameworks where appropriate and locally calibrated knowledge where the general-purpose 
framework is insufficient. This requires explicit mechanisms for agents to detect when 
they are operating outside the calibration range of their general-purpose knowledge and 
to switch to locally developed knowledge in those cases.

# The Four-Pass Framework: A Structured Protocol for Deep Cognitive Inquiry

## Overview: Why Structured Inquiry Produces Better Results Than Open Discussion

Wilson's four-pass protocol, adapted from Klein (1999: 189-196), is not simply an 
interviewing technique. It is a theory of how expert knowledge becomes accessible — 
a staged process that respects the structure of expert cognition and works with it rather 
than against it. Understanding why the four passes are sequenced as they are, and what 
each pass is designed to accomplish, reveals a sophisticated epistemology of knowledge 
elicitation that has broad application beyond mediation training.

The key insight behind the protocol's structure is that expert knowledge is layered. The 
most accessible layers — the narrative account of what happened — contain the least 
valuable information for knowledge transfer. The least accessible layers — the specific 
cognitive processes that generated each decision in real time — contain the most valuable 
information. But the less accessible layers cannot be reached directly; they must be 
approached progressively, after the more accessible layers have been established as 
shared ground between interviewer, interviewee, and observing community.

## Pass 1: Establishing the Incident — The Art of Selection

The first pass asks the interviewee to identify "a tough case or non-routine event they have 
dealt with, and where their skills 'made a difference'" (p. 17). This framing is precise and 
deliberate. Each element of the instruction does cognitive work:

**"Tough case or non-routine event"** signals that the interest is in situations where 
expertise was required — not routine cases where competent performance is sufficient. 
This framing helps the expert recognize that the interview is not looking for their 
most successful case or most impressive case, but for the kind of case that actually 
taxed their judgment.

**"Where your skills 'made a difference'"** provides a selection criterion that focuses 
attention on cases where the expert's specific contributions were consequential — where 
what they did (or didn't do) had visible impact. This excludes cases where the outcome 
was determined by factors outside the practitioner's control and cases where any competent 
practitioner would have achieved the same result.

The challenge at this stage is real: "the interviewer is trying to elicit a useful example of 
practice without being overly directive" (p. 17). The expert may initially select an incident 
that is too routine (produces insufficient learning value), too personal (raises ethical issues 
around anonymization), or too complex (cannot be adequately explored in the available time). 
"Sometimes the interview takes a while to develop, and the interviewer and interviewee may 
need to work together in front of the group to narrow their choice of incident" (p. 18).

This collaborative selection process is itself instructive for observers: watching an expert 
mediator think through what constitutes a case worth examining reveals something about 
their own taxonomy of difficulty and significance — what they consider a genuinely 
challenging case, what they consider routine, and how they evaluate the learning value 
of different types of incidents.

## Pass 2: Full Narrative — Establishing the Temporal Structure

Once a suitable incident is identified, the interviewer invites the expert to "tell the story 
in full" (p. 18). At this stage, "the interviewer tries not to interrupt the account, but may 
ask clarifying, non-directive questions supportive of the interviewee's narrative, aimed at 
establishing the best possible representation of events" (p. 18).

The purpose of this pass is not yet to probe the expert's cognitive process. It is to establish 
a shared temporal structure — a sequence of events that provides the scaffolding for the 
deeper inquiry to come. The interviewer is listening for:

- **Key decision points**: moments where the situation could have developed differently 
  depending on what the expert chose to do
- **Turning points**: moments when the situation changed significantly, requiring the expert 
  to reassess and adapt
- **Moments of uncertainty**: passages in the narrative where the expert seems to have 
  been genuinely unsure how to proceed
- **Anomalies**: elements of the situation that were unusual or surprising

These elements become the targets for pass 3. The interviewer is building an internal map 
of where the most valuable content is likely to be found, without yet pursuing it.

Klein's suggestion that "a whiteboard is useful for visually tracking the sequence of events" 
(p. 16-17) reflects the cognitive load of managing this mapping process. The whiteboard 
externalizes the temporal structure of the incident, making it available for shared reference 
by interviewer, interviewee, and the observing group. It also signals to the expert that 
their account is being taken seriously — that what they are describing is complex enough 
to require visual tracking.

## Pass 3: Cognitive Excavation — The Core of the Protocol

The third pass is where the real work happens. The interviewer returns to the key decision 
points identified in pass 2 and systematically probes the cognitive processes that generated 
each decision. This is where the protocol's question design is most sophisticated and most 
important.

Wilson provides a detailed question list for pass 3 (pp. 18-19), which can be analyzed 
according to the cognitive dimensions each question targets:

**Questions targeting situation assessment:**
- "What were your initial reactions?"
- "Which behavioural features did you expect to see at this point, and what did you actually observe?"
- "What, if anything, was unusual about this case?"

These questions surface the expert's pattern recognition: what they were looking for, 
what they found, and how divergence from expectation was processed. The "expected vs. 
actual observation" question is particularly powerful because it forces the expert to 
articulate their prior model of the situation — the pattern they were trying to match — 
before describing how that model was updated by what they actually found.

**Questions targeting emotional and relational intelligence:**
- "How did you feel about the situation?"
- "When you decided to say X to party A, what thoughts, if any, did you have about how party B would react?"
- "Did you observe that party B's reactions were as you expected, or not? Did that reaction raise any issues for you?"

These questions surface the relational dimension of expert judgment — the continuous 
tracking of multiple parties' states, needs, and likely responses that characterizes expert 
mediation practice. They also surface the role of the practitioner's own emotional response 
as data: the expert mediator does not eliminate their emotional reactions to a situation; 
they use them as information.

**Questions targeting decision process:**
- "What did you consider doing, and what made you choose the intervention you chose?"
- "What caused you to reject the other options open to you at that point, and did you at any stage reconsider any of the options you had discarded?"
- "How did you know what to do at that moment?"

These are the questions that most directly target the expert's decision-making process. 
They systematically separate the chosen action from the alternatives that were considered 
and rejected, and they ask for the discriminative features that led to the selection. "How 
did you know what to do?" is particularly confronting: it forces the expert to articulate 
the basis of their judgment, which may have been experienced as intuition but which, 
under probing, often reveals specific cues and patterns.

**Questions targeting counterfactual reasoning:**
- "What would you have done if X hadn't happened?"
- "What might have happened if you hadn't done/said that?"
- "Is there anything that you observed which made you change course? If so, what? How did you change course?"

Counterfactual questions serve multiple purposes: they reveal the conditional structure 
of the expert's reasoning (what they were prepared to do if the situation had developed 
differently), they surface the expert's causal model of the situation (why they believe 
their intervention had the effect it did), and they demonstrate the expert's awareness of 
contingency — the recognition that what happened was one branch of many possible 
developments.

**Questions targeting metacognition:**
- "How would you explain what you did to someone else?"
- "Did you have any particular thoughts about the conflict at this stage? If so, did you act on them?"
- "Did your approach make a difference? In what way?"

Metacognitive questions ask the expert to step outside their own performance and observe 
it from a distance — to describe not just what they did but why they believe it worked, 
and how they would convey that to someone who wasn't there. This is where the expert's 
tacit knowledge begins to approach explicit formulation: the attempt to explain produces 
articulation that might not otherwise occur.

## Pass 4: The Developmental Trajectory — Novice, Intermediate, Expert

The fourth pass introduces a comparative dimension by asking the expert to reflect on 
how their current practice differs from what they would have done earlier in their career 
and from what a novice would do now.

Questions include (p. 20):
- "What might a novice mediator have done when X happened?"
- "Which factors do you think someone with less experience might have missed?"
- "If you had been involved in this case 5 years previously, would your interventions have been the same?"
- "What has contributed most to the way you now practise?"

These questions serve two distinct functions:

**For knowledge transfer:** The novice comparison forces the expert to articulate the 
discriminative features of the situation that expertise enables them to detect and respond 
to. By describing what a novice would have missed, the expert implicitly describes what 
they themselves saw — the specific cues, patterns, and dynamics that their experience 
has taught them to attend to. This is often the most directly teachable content produced 
by the entire interview.

**For developmental understanding:** The five-year retrospective asks the expert to 
reconstruct their own developmental trajectory — to remember what it was like not to 
know what they now know. This is difficult but valuable: it traces the specific learning 
experiences, failures, and recalibrations that produced the current level of performance, 
providing a partial map of the developmental path for practitioners at earlier stages.

## The Group Phase: Distributed Inquiry

Following the four passes, the interview is opened to questions from the observing group. 
"My experience is that these questions have been similar in tone to the four passes, and have 
not been unduly challenging or controversial" (p. 21). This phase is important because it 
democratizes the inquiry: the interviewer's questions reflect their particular interests and 
blind spots, while the group's questions reflect the specific concerns and uncertainties of 
practitioners at various stages of development.

The group phase also serves as a real-time demonstration of the knowledge's relevance: 
when practitioners ask questions that connect the expert's specific case to their own 
practice challenges, they are actively integrating the knowledge rather than passively 
receiving it.

## Application to Agent Systems: The Four-Pass Protocol as Diagnostic Architecture

The four-pass structure translates directly into a diagnostic architecture for agent knowledge 
elicitation and transfer:

**Pass 1 equivalent: Incident Selection**
Design protocols for identifying, from an agent's history, specific non-routine cases where 
the agent's performance made a measurable difference — cases where the outcome diverged 
significantly from baseline agent performance, or where the agent's response was unusually 
distinctive. These cases are the appropriate targets for deep diagnostic analysis.

**Pass 2 equivalent: Trace Reconstruction**
For each selected case, reconstruct the full temporal trace: the sequence of inputs, intermediate 
states, decisions made, and outputs produced. Make this trace available as shared reference 
for the diagnostic process. Identify the key decision points, turning points, and moments 
where the agent's behavior was unusual or where alternative paths were evaluated.

**Pass 3 equivalent: Decision-Point Probing**
At each key decision point identified in pass 2, probe the agent's decision process:
- What features of the input were most salient at this point?
- What alternatives were generated and evaluated?
- Why was the chosen action selected over the alternatives?
- What expectation did the agent hold about the likely effect, and was that expectation met?
- What in the input, if different, would have led to a different choice?

**Pass 4 equivalent: Comparative Analysis**
Run the same case through agents at different capability levels and compare the responses:
- What did the high-capability agent notice that the baseline agent missed?
- Where did they diverge, and what input features drove the divergence?
- Can the high-capability agent's decision process be described in terms that allow 
  a lower-capability agent to approach similar performance?

**Group phase equivalent: Stakeholder Query**
Design mechanisms for domain experts, system operators, and other agents facing similar 
tasks to query the extracted knowledge — to ask the questions most relevant to their 
specific concerns. This phase ensures that the knowledge extraction produces content that 
is actually useful to the people and systems that will use it, rather than content that 
reflects only the diagnostic designer's interests.

# Metacognition in Expert Practice: Reflection Before, During, and After Action

## The Schön Problem: What Reflective Practice Actually Requires

Donald Schön's concept of the "reflective practitioner" (1983) has become so widely 
adopted in professional education that it functions almost as an unchallengeable axiom: 
good practitioners reflect on their practice. Wilson's treatment of this concept is 
unusually critical, and her critique is substantive rather than dismissive. Understanding 
what she objects to, and why, reveals something important about the relationship between 
reflection and expertise.

Lang and Taylor (2000: 145), drawing on Schön, describe experts as "professionals wedded 
to a model of practice" who believe "that individuals can acquire a body of knowledge that 
yields an effective and competent solution when applied to a problem" (p. 10). On this 
reading, experts are the problem: they have solidified into rigidity, applying a known 
framework without genuine responsiveness to the specific situation.

Wilson's objection is not to reflection itself but to the artificial dichotomy this creates 
between experts and reflective practitioners. Her argument has two components:

**First, Greenwood's pre-action critique.** Greenwood (1998: 1049), citing Greenwood (1993), 
"rejects the Schönian model of reflective practice as fundamentally flawed on the grounds 
that it fails to recognise the importance of the reflection that takes place before action 
(emphasis original)" (p. 10). Schön's model focuses on reflection-in-action (the practitioner 
responding dynamically to a situation) and reflection-on-action (the practitioner reviewing 
what happened after the fact). But it underemphasizes the deliberate preparation that precedes
