# The Problem of Tacit Knowledge: How to Extract What Experts Cannot Articulate

## The Core Problem

Every intelligent agent system requires a knowledge base. The question is how that knowledge base should be built — what methodologies should be used to extract and represent expert knowledge, and what pitfalls await the system designer who chooses the wrong approach.

Klein and MacGregor open with a deceptively simple observation that has deep consequences: "Expertise often resides in an understanding of the processes by which skilled performance evolves and in explanations of performance that emphasize tacit knowledge obtained through experience" (p. 3, citing Polanyi, 1966). And further: "Just as language fluency cannot be explained in terms of phonetic rules, syntax, or vocabulary, expert performance cannot be explained by resorting to decomposed taxonomies of skills" (p. 3).

This is the knowledge elicitation problem at its deepest level. The knowledge that makes the difference — the knowledge that separates the expert from the advanced beginner — is not the knowledge that can be stated. It is the knowledge that has been internalized through experience to the point where it operates automatically, holistically, and below the threshold of conscious articulation.

The consequences for system design are severe. If you build a knowledge base by asking experts to list their rules and procedures, you will get an accurate representation of the *explicit* fraction of their knowledge — the portion that can be stated. You will systematically miss the tacit fraction, which is often the most valuable part.

## Where Expertise Actually Lives

Klein and MacGregor make a critical observation about the *location* of expertise within a problem domain: "Expertise often occurs only at the boundaries of a problem domain" (p. 4). Most problems that experts encounter are routine in nature, requiring only the standard training that most practitioners have received. The distinctive knowledge of the expert — the knowledge that makes them different from a well-trained novice — is called upon only for *low base-rate problems* that "lie outside the bounds of routine or 'textbook' knowledge for a field" (p. 4).

This creates a fundamental sampling problem for knowledge elicitation. If you study what experts do in routine cases, you are not studying expertise — you are studying the common knowledge that everyone has. The distinctive knowledge becomes visible only when the expert encounters a problem that exceeds the novice's capabilities.

Furthermore, the most automatic knowledge — the patterns that the expert has internalized most deeply through years of practice — is precisely the *least accessible to verbal report*. Routine cases are handled automatically and unconsciously, making protocol analysis useless: "the knowledge has become automatic and unconscious" (p. 13). Novel cases lack content expertise by definition.

As Klein and MacGregor conclude: "We feel that it is at the boundary between routine and novel where expertise is stretched far enough to become visible" (p. 14).
