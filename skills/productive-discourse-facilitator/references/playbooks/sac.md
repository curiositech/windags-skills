# Structured Academic Controversy (SAC): Full Reference

## What Is SAC?

Structured Academic Controversy (SAC) is a cooperative learning and conflict resolution method developed by **David W. Johnson and Roger T. Johnson** at the University of Minnesota Cooperative Learning Center. Unlike debate, which aims to win, SAC uses structured opposition to generate *deeper understanding* and more durable synthesis.

The central insight: **intellectual conflict, properly structured, produces better thinking than either consensus or unconstrained argument.** When participants are required to fully understand and argue the other side, they stop caricaturing it.

### Research Basis
- Johnson & Johnson's research (1979–2009) showed SAC improved learning outcomes, conceptual depth, and attitude change compared to debate, concurrence-seeking, and individual study.
- SAC produces greater epistemic humility — participants rated their certainty lower *and* their understanding higher after completing the protocol.
- The perspective-taking requirement (Phase 2) is what most distinguishes SAC from standard debate and is responsible for most of its benefit.

## The Four Phases: Full Implementation Guide

### Phase 1: Present Your Position (5–10 minutes per party)

**Goal**: Each party states their position as clearly and completely as possible.

**Rules during Phase 1**:
- The other party listens without interrupting
- The listener's only job is to understand — not to rebut
- The facilitator may take notes on key claims
- If the speaker is unclear, the facilitator may ask a brief clarifying question after they finish (never during)

**Facilitator prompts**:
- "What is your core position?"
- "What are the strongest reasons you hold it?"
- "What evidence or experience most shapes this view?"
- "What concerns you most about the opposing view?"

**What good Phase 1 looks like**: The speaker feels fully heard. The listener can accurately restate the position without mocking or minimizing it. The facilitator has captured the main claims.

**Common failure mode**: Speakers use Phase 1 to preemptively attack the other's position ("My position is X, and obviously the other view fails because…"). Redirect: "In Phase 1, we stay with your own position. There will be room to engage the other view later."

---

### Phase 2: Argue the Other's Position (5–10 minutes per party)

**Goal**: Each party presents the opposing position in its strongest form.

This is the heart of SAC and the most uncomfortable phase for participants with strong convictions. It is also the most transformative.

**Rules during Phase 2**:
- You argue the position you *disagree with*, as compellingly as you can
- The original holder of that position listens and may briefly correct factual errors after the presentation
- You are not pretending to believe the position — you are demonstrating that you understand it well enough to argue it

**Facilitator prompts**:
- "What is the strongest argument for their position — not the weakest?"
- "What concerns might a reasonable, thoughtful person have that would lead them to hold this view?"
- "What evidence would you cite if this were your position?"
- "What does this position protect or preserve that matters?"

**Facilitator move if someone straw-mans**: Gently pause. "I'd like to push us toward the strongest version of that argument. What would a very smart person who holds this view say to counter your characterization?"

**What good Phase 2 looks like**: The original position-holder says something like "Yes, that's actually a good way to put it" or "You captured something I hadn't quite said myself." This is a signal that genuine understanding has occurred.

**Common failure mode**: Participants argue a weak or caricatured version of the other's position, then "defeat" it. This defeats the purpose of the exercise. The facilitator must push for genuine steelmanning.

---

### Phase 3: Joint Identification of Strongest Points (15–20 minutes, collaborative)

**Goal**: Both parties work together to identify the most compelling arguments from *both* positions.

The facilitator shifts the framing: "You're no longer arguing for or against anything. You're now analysts together. Your job is to identify which arguments, from either side, are genuinely strong."

**Process**:
1. Facilitator writes two columns on a shared surface (or in shared notes): "Strongest Arguments for Position A" | "Strongest Arguments for Position B"
2. Both parties together nominate arguments. Either party can nominate an argument from either column.
3. Facilitator records without judgment. No argument is struck down in Phase 3 — only sorted and refined.
4. After the list is complete, both parties rank the top 2–3 from each column by consensus.

**What emerges**: Often, the real disagreement becomes visible not as "who is right" but as "what values or facts are in tension." Naming the underlying tension is itself significant progress.

**Common failure mode**: One party refuses to nominate arguments for the opposing position, or votes down every argument from the other side. Facilitator reframe: "We're not voting on winner and loser. We're building a shared map. Even if you disagree with an argument, if it's *strong*, it belongs on the map."

---

### Phase 4: Collaborative Synthesis (10–20 minutes)

**Goal**: Both parties together draft a synthesis that either (a) integrates genuine insights from both positions, or (b) clearly articulates the remaining real disagreement as a *productive* tension rather than a deadlock.

**Important**: SAC does not always produce agreement, and forcing false consensus is worse than honest disagreement. The synthesis may be:
- A shared statement of what is genuinely uncertain and why
- A decision that explicitly acknowledges the values tradeoff it makes
- A reframe: "We agree on X and Y. We disagree about Z, and here's why that's a hard problem."

**Facilitator prompts**:
- "What can both of you say together that is more true than what either of you said alone?"
- "If you had to write one sentence that both of you would sign, what would it say?"
- "What do you agree on that you didn't know you agreed on at the start?"
- "What's the real tradeoff this question involves?"

**Closing ritual**: The facilitator reads the synthesis aloud and asks each party: "Does this feel fair to your position — even if it doesn't fully represent it?" If yes, the session closes. If no, one more revision pass.

## Adapting SAC for 1-on-1 Conversations

The original SAC protocol was designed for classroom groups. These adaptations make it work for two people.

### Timing Adjustments
- Phases 1 and 2: 5–7 minutes each (not 10)
- Phase 3: 10 minutes
- Phase 4: 10–15 minutes
- Total: ~35–45 minutes for a focused session

### Role of the Facilitator in 1-on-1
In a classroom, a teacher facilitates. In 1-on-1, the facilitator may be an AI, a therapist, a mediator, or one party agreeing to take turns. Key facilitation responsibilities:
- Enforce time limits gently but firmly
- Interrupt straw-manning and redirect to steelmanning
- Prevent Phase 2 from collapsing into Phase 1
- Hold space during Phase 4 without rushing to resolution

### When One Party Is the Facilitator
If one party is facilitating, they lose some of the benefit of Phase 2 (they must argue the other's position, which feels uncomfortable when you're also managing the process). Consider: the AI facilitator argues neither position; both humans complete all four phases.

## SAC Adaptations for Couples

SAC was designed for intellectual controversy. Couples often bring emotional, relational, and identity dimensions that require modification.

### Topic Selection
SAC works best for couples when the topic:
- Has a genuine external referent (parenting philosophy, finances, major decisions)
- Is not about identity-level wounds ("You never valued my emotions")
- Has been raised before but not resolved, suggesting a real difference in values or priorities

### Pacing for Couples
Couples tend to take longer. Build in more check-ins. After Phase 2, ask explicitly:
- "How does it feel to hear your partner argue your position?"
- "Was anything surprising about how they understood you?"

These questions use Phase 2 itself as a bonding moment — and that is appropriate and valuable.

### Household Issues Example
**Topic**: How much should be spent on children's extracurricular activities?

- Phase 1: Each partner states their position and the values underneath it (financial security vs. developmental investment, for example)
- Phase 2: Each argues the other's position, including the emotional reasons (not just financial calculations)
- Phase 3: Together, they identify the real tension — e.g., "We both want to give our kids what they need. We differ on what 'what they need' means."
- Phase 4: Draft a decision framework rather than a single answer: "We'll revisit this decision each semester with these criteria…"

### Parenting Example
**Topic**: Screen time limits for children

SAC helps couples stop arguing about the rule and start understanding what each parent is *afraid of* — the value underneath. One parent fears developmental harm; the other fears social exclusion. Phase 3 reveals that both fears are legitimate. Phase 4 can produce a policy that addresses both fears.

### Finances Example
**Topic**: How aggressively to pay down debt vs. maintain lifestyle

Phase 2 is especially powerful here: the "saver" must argue the case for present enjoyment; the "spender" must argue the case for financial security. This almost always produces empathy — and often reveals that both positions are attempts to manage anxiety.

## Facilitator Tips

### Timing
- Use a visible timer. Do not rely on verbal reminders alone.
- Five minutes feels short. Two minutes feels extremely short. Three minutes is right for couples in most contexts.
- Give a 60-second warning before time ends.

### Managing Emotion
- Emotion is not the enemy of SAC. It is data.
- If someone cries in Phase 1, pause. Acknowledge. Ask if they want to continue or take a breath. Do not rush past feeling.
- If someone becomes contemptuous or dismissive in Phase 2 (arguing the other's position sarcastically), stop the exercise. Name what happened: "What just happened felt like it shifted from arguing the position to mocking it. Can we try again?"

### Ensuring Equity
- Track speaking time loosely. If one person dominates Phase 3, redirect: "I'd like to hear from [other person] — what arguments from either side do you find strongest?"
- Notice who the synthesis tends to favor. If one person's values consistently win in Phase 4, name it: "I notice the synthesis has leaned toward [Person A]'s framing a few times. [Person B], does this synthesis feel like it captures your view fairly?"

## When SAC Works — and When It Doesn't

### SAC Works Well When:
- Both parties are genuinely uncertain at some level
- The controversy has a real intellectual or values dimension (not just ego)
- Both parties have roughly equal standing and comfort speaking
- The topic can be discussed without triggering trauma responses
- There is enough trust to complete Phase 2 without it becoming a weapon

### SAC Does Not Work Well When:
- **Active abuse or coercive control**: SAC assumes parity. If one party uses Phase 2 to humiliate the other ("Even your own arguments are weak"), stop immediately.
- **Power imbalances**: An employee and their direct supervisor, or a financially dependent partner and a controlling one, cannot safely engage as equals. Use mediated dialogue instead.
- **Acute crisis**: If one party is in acute distress — grief, substance use, crisis — SAC is not appropriate. Stabilize first.
- **Bad faith**: If one party has already decided the outcome and is using the process performatively, name it and stop.
- **Identity-level wounds as the topic**: "You don't respect me" is not an SAC topic. It is a therapeutic one. Redirect to observable behavior and specific incidents before attempting any structured protocol.
