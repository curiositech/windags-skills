# Decision Support Systems for Recognition: What Should Actually Be Built

## The Wrong Thing Is Being Built

The history of decision support systems (DSS) development has been, in Klein and Calderwood's assessment, largely a history of building the wrong thing. DSS have been built around the analytical model of decision-making: they help users enumerate options, estimate probabilities, weight criteria, and calculate expected utilities. They assume that better decisions come from more systematic analysis, and they support that analysis.

But if expert decision-making is fundamentally recognitional rather than analytical, then analytical decision support systems are solving the wrong problem. They are providing powerful tools for a process that experts don't use and that non-experts shouldn't be trained to rely on for operational decisions.

What should be built instead? Klein and Calderwood provide a detailed specification for recognition-based decision support — one that remains astonishingly relevant decades later.

## The Design Target: Faster, More Accurate Situation Recognition

"Decision support systems based on a model of recognitional decision making would be designed to improve speed and accuracy of situational understanding."

This is a fundamentally different design target than what classical DSS optimized for. The target is not better option comparison, more precise utility calculation, or more rigorous probability estimation. The target is: **help the practitioner recognize what type of situation they're in, faster and more accurately**.

Everything else in the RPD model follows from this. Once the situation is correctly recognized, the experienced practitioner already knows:
- What goals are feasible
- What cues to monitor
- What expectancies to hold
- What a plausible first action looks like

The support system doesn't need to generate options, score them, and recommend a choice. It needs to accelerate and improve the recognition step that triggers everything else.

## Specific Design Recommendations

### Analogue Displays Over Alphanumeric Data

"One way to improve recognition speed would be to use analogue displays, such as maps and graphics, rather than alphanumeric data, on the assumption that the patterns presented in analogue displays would facilitate perceptual matching."

Pattern recognition operates on perceptual structures — spatial relationships, visual patterns, analog representations. A table of numbers does not directly support pattern recognition. A visual map that makes the spatial structure of a situation immediately perceivable does. Interface design for recognition-based decision support should prioritize visual, spatial, and analogue representations over tables, lists, and numeric displays.

For agent systems, this translates to: **outputs intended for human review should present information in forms that facilitate human pattern recognition, not just in forms that are informationally complete**. An agent that surfaces a dense table of metrics is providing less useful decision support than one that provides a visual representation of the same data structured to reveal the patterns that matter.

### Case Databases Organized Around Prior Situations

"Additional facilitation could come through the use of databases organized around prior cases. Prior cases could be stored in an analogy bank so that they could be retrieved individually, or there could be a means of retrieving and synthesizing several cases at once, to allow prototype matching."

The decision support system should not ask "what options do you have and what are their expected values?" It should ask "what situation does this look like?" — and provide immediate access to cases that match, so the practitioner can confirm or disconfirm the match and retrieve the goal-cue-action bundles associated with each case.

Case retrieval systems for decision support should:
- Retrieve by situational similarity, not just by keyword or category
- Surface the most relevant features of matched cases alongside the current situation, enabling comparison
- Allow prototype synthesis: combining multiple cases to form a prototype when no single case is a close match
- Annotate cases with the cues that were diagnostic, the expectancies that were or weren't confirmed, and the outcomes that resulted

### Flagging Mismatches and Relevant Cases Not Retrieved

"Decision support systems could facilitate performance by flagging mismatches or by prompting the decision maker about potentially relevant cases that were not retrieved."

This is a proactive support function. The system doesn't wait for the practitioner to query — it monitors the current situation and alerts when:
- The current situation violates expectancies generated by the current recognition (potential misidentification)
- There are potentially relevant cases in the database that the practitioner may not have considered
- The current situation has unusual features that diverge from the recognized prototype (may require situation reassessment)

This is the system as a monitoring and alerting layer for the practitioner's situation assessment, not a decision-making layer that generates recommendations.

### Supporting Mental Simulation

The evaluation step in the RPD model is mental simulation. Decision support systems can facilitate this by:
- Providing rich, detailed scenario information that supports the practitioner's ability to mentally project an action forward
- Making simulation constraints explicit (what resources are available, what the current state of dependent systems is, what time pressures apply)
- Allowing the practitioner to externalize their simulation (describe the scenario they're running) and then checking it against available information for accuracy

### Interface Design Centered on Decision Points

"If system designers investigate the critical cues that operators use in categorizing or distinguishing situations, and if the designers identify the different cues used by experienced/inexperienced operators then the displays and interfaces can be centered around decisions rather than around data flows."

This is a design methodology, not just a feature list. The system should be built backward from the decision points that practitioners face, not forward from the data that is available. What are the critical recognition decisions in this domain? What cues differentiate one situation type from another? What displays would make those cues immediately apparent?

"Furthermore, if the designers can address the most difficult, time-pressured decisions then the operators should find the interfaces easier to use under stress."

Design for the hard cases, not the easy ones. Systems designed for well-bounded, low-time-pressure decisions will fail at exactly the moments when they are most needed.

## Training Design for Recognition, Not Analysis

"Rather than trying to train general skills it would be more effective to use domain-specific training programs and augment these with feedback and training scenario features that focus on decision processes."

"We may need to learn how to restructure existing courses so that students can learn how to conceptualize situations quickly and effectively, and to perform effective mental simulations."

The training implications are as significant as the system design implications. Training for recognition-based expertise looks very different from training for analytical competence:

**Recognition training focuses on:**
- Exposure to many varied cases across the full range of situation types
- Deliberate attention to the features that distinguish one situation type from another
- Metacognitive training: learning to notice when recognition is failing, when cues are ambiguous, when the situation is genuinely novel
- Mental simulation practice: learning to run accurate projections of action outcomes
- Expectancy generation and verification: learning to predict what should happen next and to notice when predictions fail

**Recognition training does NOT focus on:**
- General analytical procedures that transfer across domains
- Probability estimation techniques
- Multi-attribute utility scoring
- Systematic option enumeration procedures

The training should also address the management of one's own decision-making process: "one area where decision training appears to be effective is metacognition, teaching the decision maker to notice cues signaling information overload, confusion, and so on, and teaching procedures to better manage the decision-making task."

## Implications for Agent System Design

### Designing Agent Outputs for Recognition Support

When agent systems are designed to support human decision-makers, the agent's outputs should be structured to accelerate human pattern recognition, not to replace it. This means:

- Present situation characterization before options: tell the practitioner what type of situation this appears to be before suggesting what to do about it
- Surface the diagnostic cues that drove the system's characterization, so the practitioner can assess whether those cues are actually present and reliable
- Present prior cases alongside the current situation, formatted for comparison
- Flag when the current situation has features that don't fit the recognized prototype

### The Case Base as Core Infrastructure

For agent systems operating in domains where expertise accumulates, the case base is core infrastructure — not a feature to be added later. It should be:
- Indexed by situational features relevant to recognition (not just task categories)
- Annotated with cues, expectancies, and outcomes
- Designed for retrieval by similarity, not just by keyword
- Updated with new cases as they accumulate

### Monitoring as Proactive Support

The "flagging mismatches" function is proactive monitoring infrastructure: the system continuously compares the evolving situation against the current recognition model and alerts when divergence exceeds a threshold. This requires:
- An explicit representation of the current situation model (not just the current task state)
- Expectancies generated by the current recognition, stored and tracked
- Comparison of incoming observations against stored expectancies
- Threshold-based alerting when expectancy violations accumulate

### Supporting Rather Than Replacing Expert Judgment

The design philosophy throughout is supportive rather than replacement. The system accelerates recognition, surfaces relevant cases, flags mismatches, supports simulation — but the expert judgment remains central. This is a more appropriate role for decision support than the analytical model suggests, and it is more likely to actually improve performance without degrading the expertise it's meant to support.

The failure mode to avoid: building systems that are so prescriptive and structured that practitioners follow them mechanically rather than engaging their own expertise. The goal is to enhance expert performance, not to create a procedure that can be followed without expertise.
